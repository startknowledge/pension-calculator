Understanding the EPS Pension Structure: How It Works in 2024

Before diving into the 2026 hike, let’s break down how the Employees’ Pension Scheme (EPS) currently functions.

1. Who is Eligible for EPS?

EPS is available to employees of organised sector companies (those registered under the Employees’ Provident Fund and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1952). Key eligibility criteria include:

  • Minimum service of 10 years (though pension is paid from the age of 50+ if retired early).
  • Monthly salary contribution (both employer and employee contribute 8.33% of basic salary + DA to EPS).
  • Minimum pensionable salary of ₹6,500 per month (as per the latest EPFO rules).

2. How is the Current EPS Pension Calculated?

The pension amount is determined using the following formula: Pension = (Basic Salary + Dearness Allowance) × (1/70) × Years of Service

For example:

  • If your basic salary + DA is ₹15,000 and you’ve worked for 25 years, your pension would be: ₹15,000 × (1/70) × 25 = ₹5,357 per month

However, the minimum pension is capped at ₹1,000 (as per the 2023 revision), and the maximum pension is ₹7,500 (for those with high salaries).

3. Why is a Pension Hike Expected in 2026?

The Government of India has been indexing pensions with inflation to maintain their purchasing power. Key reasons for an expected hike:

  • Rising inflation (CPI) in 2024-25 (currently around 5.4% as per RBI data).
  • EPFO’s surplus funds (over ₹1.5 lakh crore in 2023-24) allow for pension revisions.
  • Pressure from pensioners’ associations demanding higher minimum pensions.
  • Comparison with other pension schemes (like NPS and PM-SYM), where benefits are periodically increased.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your EPS Pension Hike in 2026

If you want to ensure the best possible pension increase, follow these proven strategies:

1. Check Your EPS Account Statement for Accuracy

Problem: Many pensioners receive lower-than-expected pensions due to incorrect salary contributions recorded in their EPS account.

Solution:

  • Request a detailed EPS passbook from your former employer.
  • Verify your basic salary + DA (should match your PF slips).
  • Report discrepancies to EPFO (via EPFO’s online portal) if your contributions are lower than expected.

Real-World Example: A retired government employee in Mumbai noticed his EPS pension was only ₹1,200 instead of the expected ₹1,800. After reviewing his records, he found that his salary contributions were capped at ₹10,000 instead of ₹15,000. He filed a correction request, and his pension was revised to ₹1,750.

2. Extend Your Service Period (If Possible)

Problem: The pension formula (1/70 × service years) means longer service = higher pension.

Solution:

  • If you’re retiring early (before 58), consider working longer to increase your service years.
  • Part-time or contractual work (if eligible) can also add to your pensionable service.

Real-World Example: A teacher in Delhi retired at 55 with 20 years of service, receiving ₹1,400/month. By working for 5 more years (until 60), her pension increased to ₹2,100/month—a 50% jump.

3. Ensure Your Salary Includes Full DA (Dearness Allowance)

Problem: Some employers exclude DA from pension calculations, leading to lower pensions.

Solution:

  • Check your PF slips—your basic salary + DA should be clearly mentioned.
  • Demand a revised pension statement if DA is missing.
  • File a complaint with EPFO if your employer refuses to adjust DA.

Real-World Example: A bank employee in Chennai found that his DA was not included in EPS calculations, reducing his pension by ₹300/month. After submitting proof, his pension was revised to the correct amount.

4. Claim Family Pension if Eligible

Problem: Many pensioners don’t know they can claim family pension for their spouse/children after their death.

Solution:

  • Family pension is 30% of the deceased pensioner’s pension (for spouse) or 50% for dependent children.
  • Apply within 2 years of the pensioner’s death (late claims may face penalties).

Real-World Example: A widow in Pune was unaware she could claim a family pension of ₹450/month (30% of her husband’s ₹1,500 pension). After filing the claim, she received the additional amount immediately.

5. Use the EPS Pension Calculator to Project Your Future Pension

Problem: Without a pension projection, you may underestimate your future benefits.

Solution:

  • Use the Pension Calculator by EPFO to estimate your pension.
  • Input your current salary, service years, and expected retirement age.
  • Adjust for inflation (assuming a 5–7% annual increase).

Real-World Example: A software engineer in Bangalore used the EPS calculator to see that working until 60 (instead of 58) would increase his pension from ₹2,200 to ₹2,800. This motivated him to stay in the workforce longer.

7. Consider Switching to NPS (If Eligible) for Higher Returns

Problem: EPS pensions are fixed and inflation-proofed, but NPS (National Pension System) offers higher returns (though with market risks).

Solution:

  • If you’re under 58, consider partially switching to NPS for tax benefits and better growth.
  • NPS Tier-II allows flexible withdrawals, while NPS Tier-I is locked until retirement.

Real-World Example: A corporate employee in Gurgaon switched 20% of his PF contributions to NPS at age 45. By retirement, his NPS corpus grew to ₹50 lakh, supplementing his EPS pension of ₹2,500/month with an additional ₹3,000/month in withdrawals.

8. Plan for Post-Retirement Tax Efficiency

Problem: Pensions are tax-free, but excess withdrawals or investments can attract taxes.

Solution:

  • Invest in tax-free instruments (like PPF, ELSS, or NPS) to offset pension income.
  • Claim deductions under Section 80C (if applicable).
  • Use the ₹50,000 exemption under Section 10(10A) for family pension.

Real-World Example: *A retired professor in Kolkata used PPF and NPS to reduce his taxable income from his ₹2,200 pension. By investing ₹1.5 lakh/year, he saved ₹30,000 in taxes annually.

Common Mistakes That Reduce Your EPS Pension (And How to Fix Them)

Many pensioners unintentionally lose out on higher pensions due to these avoidable mistakes:

1. Not Verifying Your EPS Passbook Before Retirement

Mistake: Some employees assume their pension will be calculated automatically without checking their EPS passbook.

Fix:

  • Request a final EPS statement from your employer at least 6 months before retirement.
  • Cross-check your basic salary, DA, and service years with your PF slips.

Real-World Example: *A factory worker in Ahmedabad retired without checking his EPS records. He later found that his salary was recorded as ₹12,000 instead of ₹15,000, reducing his pension by ₹300/month.

2. Not Linking Aadhaar with EPS Account

Mistake: Aadhaar linkage is mandatory for pension disbursement, but many pensioners forget to update it.

Fix:

  • Visit the EPFO portal and link your Aadhaar with your EPS account.
  • Update your bank details to avoid delays in pension payments.

Real-World Example: *A pensioner in Chennai faced 3-month delays in receiving his pension because his Aadhaar was not linked. After updating it, his pension was restored within a week.

3. Not Claiming Arrears for Past Years

Mistake: If your pension was underpaid in previous years, you miss out on arrears.

Fix:

  • File a claim for arrears within 3 years of the discrepancy.
  • Submit proof of underpayment (like EPFO statements, salary slips).

Real-World Example: *A retired bank employee in Delhi received ₹1,800/month instead of the correct ₹2,200. After filing a claim, he received ₹1.2 lakh in arrears over 2 years.

4. Not Updating Your Address with EPFO

Mistake: Pension payments get delayed or stopped if your address is not updated.

Fix:

  • Update your address in the EPFO portal (online or via the EPFO office).
  • Verify delivery status of your pension passbook.

Real-World Example: *A pensioner in Lucknow stopped receiving his pension because his address was outdated. After updating it, his payments resumed within 10 days.

5. Withdrawing PF Before Retirement

Mistake: Partial PF withdrawals reduce your pensionable service.

Fix:

  • Avoid PF withdrawals unless absolutely necessary.
  • Use the PF for emergencies only (after retirement).
