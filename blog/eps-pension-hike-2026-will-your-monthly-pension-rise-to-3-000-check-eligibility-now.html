Understanding the EPS Pension Scheme: How It Works

Before diving into the 2026 hike, let’s recap how EPS works to ensure you’re fully informed.

What is the Employees’ Pension Scheme (EPS)?

EPS is a defined benefit pension scheme administered by the EPFO, offering a monthly pension to employees after retirement. It is mandatory for employees earning ₹15,000 or less per month (as of 2024).

Key Components of EPS:

  1. Contribution Rate:

    • Employee: 8.33% of basic salary + dearness allowance (DA)
    • Employer: 3.67% (for pension) + 10% (for PF)
    • Total Pension Contribution: 12% of salary (split between employer and employee)

  2. Pension Calculation Formula:

    • Pension = (Basic Salary + DA) × (Years of Service / 70) × Pensionable Salary Ratio
    • The maximum pensionable salary is ₹15,000 (as per current rules).

  3. Minimum & Maximum Pension:

    • Minimum Pension: ₹1,000 (for employees with 10+ years of service)
    • Maximum Pension: ₹7,500 (under current rules, but reforms may change this)

Why the 2026 Hike?

The Government has been pushing for pension reforms to: ✔ Increase the minimum pension to ₹3,000–₹5,000 ✔ Raise the maximum pension cap (currently ₹7,500) ✔ Improve pension for women (due to lower life expectancy) ✔ Encourage longer service with better returns

The Budget 2024-25 hinted at these changes, and EPS 2026 reforms are expected to formalize them.

EPS Pension Hike 2026: Will You Get ₹3,000+?

The big question is: Will your pension rise to ₹3,000 or more? The answer depends on three key factors:

1. Years of Service (Employment Tenure)

  • Current Rule: Pension is calculated as (Basic + DA) × (Service Years / 70)
  • Proposed Change (2026): Some experts suggest a new formula where service years are weighted more heavily, potentially increasing pension for long-serving employees.

Example:

  • Current Scenario: 20 years of service, basic salary ₹12,000 → Pension = ₹12,000 × (20/70) ≈ ₹3,428
  • With 2026 Hike: If the formula changes to (Service Years / 60), pension could jump to ₹4,000+.

2. Salary & Pensionable Limit

  • Current Cap: ₹15,000 (pensionable salary cannot exceed this)
  • Proposed Change: Rumors suggest the pensionable salary limit may increase to ₹21,000 (as per some draft reports).

Example:

  • Current: ₹15,000 salary → Pension capped at ₹7,500
  • With New Limit: ₹21,000 salary → Higher pension potential (if reforms pass)

3. Gender-Based Adjustments

  • Current Issue: Women retire earlier (due to marriage/health), leading to lower pension years.
  • Proposed Fix: Some reports suggest bonus years for women (e.g., adding 5 extra years to service tenure).

Example:

  • A woman with 15 years of service may get 20 years credited, increasing her pension significantly.

8 Actionable Steps to Maximize Your EPS Pension in 2026

If you want to secure the highest possible pension, follow these proven strategies:

1. Check Your EPS Account Statement (UAN Portal)

  • Why? Many employees don’t realize they’re not fully contributing to EPS.
  • How?
    1. Log in to EPFO’s UAN Portal
    2. Go to "Member Passbook" → "EPS Account Balance"
    3. Verify if 8.33% is being deducted from your salary.
  • Fix: If missing, raise a grievance with your employer.

2. Ensure Continuous Service (No Gaps)

  • Why? Gaps in service reduce pension years, cutting your final amount.
  • How?
    • If you switch jobs, transfer your EPS balance to the new employer.
    • If unemployed, apply for EPS withdrawal (if eligible) to avoid breaks.

Real-World Example: A bank employee had a 2-month gap between jobs. When he retired, his pension was reduced by ₹500/month because those months weren’t counted.

3. Opt for the New Pension Scheme (NPS) Alongside EPS

  • Why? While EPS is mandatory, NPS offers higher returns (10-12% vs. EPS’s fixed rate).
  • How?
    • If your employer offers NPS, contribute extra ₹500–₹1,000/month for tax benefits + higher returns.
    • At retirement, combine EPS pension + NPS corpus for financial security.

4. Claim All Eligible Dearness Allowance (DA) Increases

  • Why? DA is added to pension calculations, and unclaimed increases reduce your pension.
  • How?
    • Check if your DA is updated in the UAN portal.
    • If DA is not reflected, file a grievance with EPFO.

Common Mistake: Many employees forget to claim DA hikes, leading to ₹200–₹500/month less in pension.

5. Consider Early Retirement (If Eligible)

  • Why? Some employees retire early (e.g., at 50-55) to get a higher pension per year.
  • How?
    • If you have 20+ years of service, check if your employer allows early retirement.
    • Downside: Lower total pension years → lower monthly amount.

Example: A government employee retired at 55 instead of 60 and got a ₹2,500/month pension (vs. ₹1,800 if he waited).

6. Transfer EPS Balance When Changing Jobs

  • Why? If you switch jobs, your EPS balance doesn’t transfer automatically.
  • How?
    • Old Job: Get a transfer certificate (TC) from your previous employer.
    • New Job: Submit the TC to the new employer’s EPFO office.
    • Online Process: Use the UAN portal to request transfer.

Real-World Case: A private sector employee changed jobs 3 times but never transferred EPS. By the time he retired, his pension was ₹1,200 instead of ₹2,500.

7. Claim Family Pension (If Applicable)

  • Why? If you die before retirement, your spouse/children can claim family pension.
  • How?
    • Spouse: Gets 50% of your pension (minimum ₹3,000).
    • Children: Each child gets 20% of pension (minimum ₹1,000 per child).
  • Process: Submit death certificate + family details to EPFO.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Extra Pension

Many employees unintentionally lose out on higher pensions due to these biggest mistakes:

1. Not Updating Personal Details in UAN Portal

  • Problem: If your name, salary, or service details are incorrect, your pension calculation will be wrong.
  • Fix: Log in to UAN portal monthly and verify:
    • Basic salary
    • DA amount
    • Service years

2. Missing Out on PF & EPS Contributions

  • Problem: Some employers don’t deduct PF/EPS or pay late.
  • Fix:
    • Check your salary slip to confirm 8.33% is deducted.
    • If missing, file a grievance with EPFO.

3. Not Claiming Pension on Time

  • Problem: Many employees wait too long to claim pension, leading to delays and penalties.
  • Fix:
    • Apply 2-3 months before retirement (some employers take time to process).
    • Submit all documents (Aadhaar, PAN, bank details).

4. Ignoring EPFO Notifications

  • Problem: EPFO sometimes announces new rules (e.g., pension hikes, DA updates).
  • Fix:
    • Subscribe to EPFO’s official newsletter.
    • Check UAN portal regularly for updates.

5. Not Transferring EPS When Changing Jobs

  • Problem: If you don’t transfer EPS, your service years reset, reducing pension.
  • Fix:
    • Always request a transfer certificate (TC) from your old employer.
    • Submit it to the new employer within 30 days.

6. Assuming EPS is the Only Retirement Source

  • Problem: Relying only on EPS can lead to financial shortages in old age.
  • Fix:
    • Contribute to NPS, PPF, or mutual funds alongside EPS.
    • Invest in tax-saving instruments (ELSS, NPS, etc.).
