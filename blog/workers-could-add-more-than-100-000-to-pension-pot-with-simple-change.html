Workers Could Add Over £100,000 to Their Pension Pot With One Simple Change

Last updated: June 2024 | By Pension Calculator Team

Retirement planning is one of the most critical financial decisions you’ll ever make. With the cost of living rising and state pensions under pressure, many workers are left wondering: *How can I ensure my pension pot is as large as possible by the time I retire?*

The answer might surprise you. Research from the Pensions and Lifetime Savings Association (PLSA) (2023) reveals that the average UK worker retires with just **£100,000**—a figure that may not be enough to sustain a comfortable lifestyle, especially with life expectancy rising. However, a simple yet powerful change could add **over £100,000** to your pension pot over your working lifetime.

In this in-depth guide, we’ll explore eight actionable strategies—backed by financial experts and real-world examples—that could transform your pension savings. Whether you’re just starting your career or nearing retirement, these insights will help you maximise your contributions and secure a more financially stable future.

Let’s dive in.

The £100,000 Pension Boost: How Compound Growth Works

At the heart of this potential £100,000 increase lies compound growth—the phenomenon where your investments generate earnings, and those earnings generate even more earnings over time. The earlier you start and the more consistently you contribute, the greater the impact.

According to the Office for National Statistics (ONS) (2023), the average UK worker contributes **3% of their salary** to their pension via auto-enrolment, with their employer matching this (often 3% or more). However, many workers miss out on additional employer contributions or fail to optimise their contribution rates. A small increase—such as boosting your contributions by just **1%**—could lead to a **£100,000+ difference** by retirement.

Let’s break down how this works with a real-world example.

Real-World Example: The £1,000/Year Difference

Consider two workers, Sarah and Mark, both earning £40,000 per year. Both contribute 3% via auto-enrolment, but Sarah decides to increase her contributions by just **1%** (to 4%), while Mark leaves his at 3%. Over 40 years, assuming an average annual return of 5%, the difference in their pension pots is staggering:

  • Sarah’s total contributions: £48,000 (4% of £40,000 for 40 years)
  • Mark’s total contributions: £36,000 (3% of £40,000 for 40 years)
  • Employer contributions (assuming 3% match): £36,000 for both
  • Total pension pot (including growth):
    • Sarah: **£245,000**
    • Mark: **£145,000**
  • Difference: **£100,000+**

This isn’t just theory—it’s a realistic projection based on industry standards. The key takeaway? Even small increases in contributions can lead to massive long-term gains.

8 Simple (But Powerful) Strategies to Add £100,000+ to Your Pension

Now that we’ve established the potential, let’s explore eight proven strategies to maximise your pension savings. These are ranked from easiest to most advanced, ensuring you can start implementing them immediately.

1. Increase Your Contribution Rate by Just 1%

As seen in the example above, increasing your pension contributions by **1%** can have a dramatic effect. Many workers underestimate how much this small change accumulates over decades.

How to do it:

  • Check your pension provider’s website or contact them to adjust your contribution rate.
  • If you’re on a salary sacrifice scheme, ask your employer to increase your contributions automatically.
  • Set up a standing order to top up your pension if your employer doesn’t offer salary sacrifice.

2. Take Advantage of Employer Matching Contributions

Many employers offer matching contributions—meaning they contribute extra for every pound you put in. For example, if your employer matches 3% of your salary, you’re effectively getting a **free 3% return** on your pension.

How to do it:

  • Review your employer’s pension scheme terms to confirm the matching rate.
  • If you’re not already contributing enough to trigger the full match, increase your contributions.
  • For example, if your employer matches up to 5% but you’re only contributing 3%, boosting to 5% means you’re getting an extra **2% free** from your employer.

3. Switch to a More Aggressive Investment Strategy (If You’re Under 50)

If you’re younger, your pension has decades to grow, so taking on slightly more risk can lead to higher returns. Many default funds are too conservative, missing out on potential growth.

How to do it:

  • Check your current pension fund’s risk profile (e.g., "Cautious," "Balanced," "Adventurous").
  • If you’re under 50, consider switching to a balanced or growth fund to capture higher returns.
  • Use a pension calculator to compare potential outcomes.

4. Use a Pension Loan or Flexible Access to Top Up Contributions

If you have a lump sum available (e.g., from a bonus, inheritance, or sale of assets), you can use it to boost your pension. Some providers allow one-off lump sum contributions, which can be tax-efficient.

How to do it:

  • Contact your pension provider to arrange a lump sum contribution.
  • Ensure it’s a tax-relieved contribution (up to 100% of your earnings or £60,000 per year, whichever is lower).
  • For example, if you earn £50,000, you could contribute £50,000 tax-free, then claim tax relief (up to 40% for higher-rate taxpayers).

5. Optimise Your Tax Relief

Pension contributions are tax-efficient, meaning the government tops up your contributions. If you’re a higher-rate taxpayer (40%+), you can claim extra tax relief.

How to do it:

  • If you’re a basic-rate taxpayer (20%), your pension contributions are boosted by 20% automatically.
  • If you’re a higher-rate taxpayer (40%), you can claim an extra 20% via your tax return (up to £20,000 extra relief if you contribute £60,000).
  • For additional-rate taxpayers (45%), the relief is even higher.

6. Consider a Self-Invested Personal Pension (SIPP) for More Control

If you’re self-employed or want more investment choices, a SIPP allows you to invest in stocks, bonds, or funds of your choice. This can lead to higher returns if managed well.

How to do it:

  • Open a SIPP with a provider like Hargreaves Lansdown or AJ Bell.
  • Invest in low-cost index funds or ETFs for long-term growth.
  • Review and rebalance your portfolio annually.

7. Delay Taking Your State Pension (If Possible)

The UK state pension increases by **5.8% per year** if you delay taking it after your state pension age (currently 66). This can add thousands to your annual income.

How to do it:

  • Check your state pension age using the GOV.UK calculator.
  • If you can afford to work until 70, your state pension will be **much higher** than if you take it at 66.
  • For example, delaying from 66 to 70 could increase your state pension by **£10,000+ per year**.

8. Use a Pension Flexi-Access Drawdown for Extra Contributions

If you’ve already retired but want to boost your pension, flexi-access drawdown allows you to take income while continuing to contribute. This can be a tax-efficient way to grow your pot further.

How to do it:

  • Set up a flexi-access drawdown plan with your pension provider.
  • Take a small income (e.g., £20,000/year) while continuing to contribute.
  • Ensure you stay within the **£40,000 annual allowance** for pension contributions.

Common Pension Mistakes That Could Cost You £100,000+

While the strategies above can boost your pension, certain mistakes can erode your savings. Here are the most critical pitfalls—and how to avoid them.

1. Ignoring Auto-Enrolment Contributions

Many workers assume auto-enrolment is the minimum they should do, but failing to increase contributions means missing out on significant growth.

How to avoid it:

  • Review your auto-enrolment contributions annually.
  • If your employer offers salary sacrifice, take advantage of it—it reduces your taxable income.
  • Set a reminder to check your contributions every year.

2. Switching Pension Funds Too Frequently

Chasing "hot" investments or switching funds based on short-term performance can hurt your long-term returns. Stick with a diversified, low-cost fund for the best results.

How to avoid it:

  • Only switch funds if your current one has high fees or poor performance.
  • Use a pension fee calculator to compare costs.
  • Avoid "active" funds unless you’re an experienced investor.

3. Not Taking Advantage of Tax Relief

Many workers don’t claim the full tax relief on their pension contributions, leaving money on the table. Basic-rate taxpayers get 20% back; higher-rate taxpayers get 40%.

How to avoid it:

  • If you’re a higher-rate taxpayer, claim extra tax relief via your self-assessment tax return.
