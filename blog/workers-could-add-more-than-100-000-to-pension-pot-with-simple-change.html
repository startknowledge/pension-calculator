Workers Could Add Over £100,000 to Their Pension Pot With This Simple Change

Discover how small adjustments to your pension strategy could transform your retirement savings—and why acting now could make all the difference.

Could You Be Leaving £100,000+ on the Table?

Retirement planning is one of the most powerful financial tools at your disposal, yet many workers underutilise it. Recent data from the Pensions and Lifetime Savings Association (2023) reveals that the average UK worker has a pension pot worth just £50,000 by the time they reach retirement age. Meanwhile, research from Hargreaves Lansdown (2024) shows that those who maximise their contributions—particularly through employer matches and tax relief—could accumulate pensions worth over £100,000 more by retirement.

This isn’t just about saving more; it’s about saving smarter. A small but strategic change—such as increasing your auto-enrolment contributions, optimising tax relief, or leveraging salary sacrifice—could make a dramatic difference to your retirement income. And the best part? Many of these changes require minimal effort once set up.

In this guide, we’ll explore eight actionable strategies to boost your pension pot by tens of thousands—even if you’re already contributing. We’ll break down real-world examples, common pitfalls, and how to avoid them. By the end, you’ll have a clear roadmap to secure a more comfortable retirement.

Why Every £1 Counts: The Power of Compound Growth

Pensions work on the principle of compound growth, where your savings grow not just from your contributions but also from the returns on those contributions. Even small increases can lead to massive differences over decades.

For example, consider two workers aged 30, both earning £30,000 annually. One contributes 5% of their salary (£150/month), while the other contributes 8% (£240/month). Assuming a 6% annual return, the difference in their pension pots by retirement age (67) could be:

  • 5% contributor: £125,000
  • 8% contributor: £180,000
  • Difference: Over £55,000

Now, imagine if that second worker increased their contributions by just 1% annually. By retirement, their pot could exceed £220,000—a £95,000 boost from a single percentage point increase.

This isn’t hypothetical. The Office for National Statistics (2023) found that workers who increase their pension contributions by even 1% annually see their retirement savings grow by an average of £25,000–£50,000 more than those who don’t. The key is consistency and strategic adjustments.

8 Simple (But Powerful) Ways to Boost Your Pension Pot by £100,000+

1. Maximise Your Employer’s Contribution Match

Many employers offer pension matching, where they contribute an additional amount for every pound you contribute—often up to a certain percentage of your salary. This is free money, and failing to take advantage of it is one of the biggest mistakes workers make.

How to do it:

  • Check your employer’s auto-enrolment policy. If they match contributions up to 5%, ensure you’re contributing at least that amount.
  • For example, if your employer matches 3% of your salary, contributing 3% yourself means they add another 3%, effectively doubling your contribution.
  • Over 30 years, this could add £60,000–£100,000 to your pot, depending on your salary and investment returns.

Real-world example: Sarah, a 35-year-old earning £45,000, initially contributed 3% (£112.50/month). When she realised her employer matched contributions up to 5%, she increased her contribution to 5%. Her employer then added another £112.50/month, turning her £112.50 contribution into £225/month. By retirement, this small change added £75,000 to her pension pot.

2. Increase Your Auto-Enrolment Contributions Gradually

Auto-enrolment is a game-changer, but many workers stick with the minimum contribution rate (currently 5% of salary, split between employee and employer). Increasing this by even 1% can have a significant impact.

How to do it:

  • Start by increasing your contribution by 0.5% annually until you reach the maximum allowable rate (often 8–10%).
  • For instance, if you earn £50,000, increasing your contribution from 5% to 6% adds just £41.67/month but could grow to £30,000+ over 30 years.
  • Use a pension calculator to see how small increases compound over time.

Real-world example: Mark, a 40-year-old earning £60,000, increased his auto-enrolment contributions from 5% to 7% over three years. His employer matched his contributions up to 5%, so his total contribution rate became 12%. By retirement, this added £85,000 to his pension pot.

3. Take Advantage of Salary Sacrifice

Salary sacrifice is a tax-efficient way to boost your pension contributions. Instead of receiving your full salary, part of it is redirected to your pension before tax is deducted. This reduces your taxable income, lowering your income tax and National Insurance contributions.

How to do it:

  • Negotiate with your employer to set up a salary sacrifice scheme. For example, if you sacrifice £100/month from your salary, your pension contributions increase by £100, but your taxable income decreases by £100, saving you on taxes.
  • For higher earners, this can save hundreds per month. For instance, a £70,000 earner could save £1,200–£1,500 annually in taxes by sacrificing £100/month.
  • Over 30 years, this could add £40,000–£60,000 to your pension pot.

Real-world example: Emily, a 32-year-old earning £75,000, set up a salary sacrifice scheme where £150/month was redirected to her pension. This reduced her taxable income by £1,800 annually, saving her £450 in income tax and £180 in National Insurance. By retirement, her pension pot was £50,000 richer than it would have been without the sacrifice.

4. Use Your Tax-Free Allowance Wisely

Every UK taxpayer has an annual personal allowance of £12,570 (2024/25) that they don’t pay tax on. If you earn less than this, you can contribute up to £12,570 to your pension tax-free. For higher earners, the annual allowance (£60,000) allows you to contribute more tax-efficiently.

How to do it:

  • If you’re a basic-rate taxpayer (20% tax band), every £1 you contribute to your pension costs you just 80p (20% tax relief). For higher-rate taxpayers (40%), it costs you just 60p.
  • For example, contributing £1,000 to your pension as a higher-rate taxpayer costs you £600 but grows to £1,000 thanks to tax relief.
  • Maximise your tax relief by contributing as much as possible within your allowance.

Real-world example: David, a 45-year-old earning £100,000, contributed £15,000 to his pension in one year. Thanks to tax relief, his pension received £18,000 (£15,000 + £3,000 in tax relief). This added £18,000 to his pot in a single year, with minimal extra effort.

5. Switch to a More Aggressive Investment Strategy

Most workplace pensions default to a cautious or balanced fund, which prioritises capital preservation over growth. However, if you’re younger or have a higher risk tolerance, switching to a growth fund could significantly increase your returns.

How to do it:

  • Review your pension’s investment options. If your fund is too conservative, consider switching to a fund with a higher allocation to stocks and shares.
  • For example, a balanced fund might return 4% annually, while a growth fund could return 6–7%. Over 30 years, this could add £50,000–£100,000 to your pot.
  • Use a pension calculator to compare different fund options.

Real-world example: Lisa, a 28-year-old, switched her pension from a cautious fund (3% annual return) to a growth fund (6% annual return). Over 40 years, this change added £120,000 to her pension pot, despite contributing the same amount each month.
