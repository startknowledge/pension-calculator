8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your 2026 Social Security Benefits

Not all retirees claim benefits the same way—and some strategies can increase your payout by thousands per year. Here’s how to optimize your Social Security income in 2026.

1. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 for the Highest Possible Benefit

Why It Works:

  • For every year you delay beyond full retirement age (FRA), your benefit increases by 8%.
  • If you live 10+ years in retirement, delaying until 70 can be worth tens of thousands.

Example:

  • FRA benefit: $2,000/month.
  • Claim at 62: $1,400/month (30% reduction).
  • Delay until 70: $2,664/month (32% increase).

Best For: Those with long life expectancies or high earnings who can afford to wait.

2. Use the "Claim & Suspend" Strategy (If Eligible)

(Note: This option is phasing out for those born after 1954, but may still apply to some.)

How It Works:

  • One spouse files for benefits (but suspends them) at FRA.
  • The other spouse claims spousal benefits early (as young as 62).
  • The first spouse resumes benefits later (up to age 70) for the highest possible payout.

Example:

  • Husband (born 1953) files & suspends at 66.
  • Wife claims spousal benefit at 62 ($1,000/month).
  • Husband resumes at 70 ($3,500/month).
  • Wife switches to survivor benefit at 70 ($1,750/month).

Best For: Couples where one spouse has a much higher benefit and the other wants early income.

3. Claim Spousal Benefits Early (If Your Spouse Has Higher Earnings)

If your spouse has a much larger benefit, claiming spousal benefits early can provide immediate income without reducing their future payout.

Example:

  • Husband’s FRA benefit: $4,000/month.
  • Wife’s benefit: $1,500/month.
  • Wife claims spousal benefit at 62: $2,000/month (50% of husband’s FRA).
  • Husband delays until 70 ($5,280/month).
  • Wife switches to her own benefit at 70 ($1,800/month).

Best For: Stay-at-home spouses or those who need income before FRA.

4. Use the "Restricted Application" Strategy (For Those Born Before 1954)

(This option is no longer available for those born after 1954, but may still apply to some.)

How It Works:

  • File for spousal benefits only (without claiming your own) at FRA.
  • Later, switch to your own benefit (which continues to grow until 70).

Example:

  • Wife files restricted application at 66 for spousal benefit ($1,500/month).
  • Husband claims his benefit at 62 ($2,000/month).
  • Wife switches to her own benefit at 70 ($3,000/month).

Best For: Couples where one spouse has a significantly lower benefit than the other.

5. Claim Social Security at 62 Only If You Have Other Income

Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by 25–30%, but if you have pensions, rental income, or savings, it may still make sense.

Example:

  • FRA benefit: $2,500/month.
  • Claim at 62: $1,750/month.
  • If you have $500,000 in savings, you might not need the full benefit and can supplement with withdrawals.

Best For: Those with alternative income sources who don’t need the full Social Security payout.

6. Coordinate Benefits with a Pension or Annuity

If you have a corporate pension or annuity, claiming Social Security early may reduce your pension payout (due to the Windfall Elimination Provision, or WEP).

Example:

  • Government employee with WEP: Social Security benefit reduced by $0–$480/month.
  • Delaying until 70 could offset this reduction.

Best For: Public sector workers or those with government pensions.

7. Consider the "Claim Now, Claim More Later" Strategy for Divorced Spouses

If you’re divorced but were married for 10+ years, you can claim ex-spouse’s benefits—even if they’ve remarried.

Example:

  • Ex-husband’s FRA benefit: $3,500/month.
  • Wife claims ex-spousal benefit at 66: $1,750/month.
  • Ex-husband delays until 70 ($4,550/month).

Best For: Divorced individuals who no longer qualify for their own benefit.

8. Use a "Bridge Retirement" Strategy to Delay Social Security

If you retire early but don’t want to claim Social Security yet, you can:

  • Work part-time (if possible).
  • Use 401(k) or IRA withdrawals to cover expenses.
  • Delay Social Security until 70 for the highest payout.

Example:

  • Retire at 62, work part-time until 65.
  • Claim Social Security at 65 (FRA) or delay until 70.

Best For: Those who can afford to delay and want the maximum benefit.
