How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before using the calculator, it’s essential to understand how your benefits are determined. The SSA uses a three-step formula:

1. Calculate Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA adjusts your highest 35 years of earnings (indexed to inflation) to reflect current wage levels. If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are included—which drags down your average.

Example:

  • If you earned $80,000/year for 30 years and $0 for 5 years, your AIME is based on $80K × 30 + $0 × 5 = $2.4M total earnings, divided by 420 months (35 years).
  • If you work longer, replacing a low-earning year with a higher one increases your AIME.

2. Apply the Bending Formula (2026 Rates)

The SSA uses a progressive formula to determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA):

  • First $1,173/month (2024 equivalent, adjusted for 2026): 90% of your AIME
  • Next $704/month (2024 equivalent): 32% of the difference
  • Amount above $1,877/month (2024 equivalent): 15% of the difference

(Note: These thresholds will increase slightly in 2026 due to wage indexation.)

Example Calculation (2026 Projection): If your AIME = $3,500/month:

  • First $1,173 × 0.90 = $1,055.70
  • Next $704 × 0.32 = $225.28
  • Remaining $1,623 × 0.15 = $243.45
  • Total PIA = $1,055.70 + $225.28 + $243.45 = ~$1,524.43/month

(This is before any claiming adjustments.)

3. Adjust for Claiming Age

  • Claim at 62 (early retirement): ~70% of PIA (permanent reduction)
  • Claim at Full Retirement Age (FRA, 66-67): 100% of PIA
  • Delay until 70: Up to 124% of PIA (8% annual increase)

Real-World Impact:

  • If your PIA = $1,500/month, claiming at 62 gives $1,050/month (~$12,600/year).
  • Delaying to 70 could yield $1,800/month (~$21,600/year)—a $9,000+ annual difference.

Step-by-Step Guide to Maximizing Your Estimate

1. Gather Your SSA Earnings Data

  • Log in to www.ssa.gov/myaccount to access your Social Security Statement.
  • Note your highest 35 years of earnings (or fewer if you worked less).

2. Input Your Claiming Age

  • 62 (early): Lowest payout but immediate access.
  • FRA (66-67): Full benefit without penalty.
  • 70 (delayed): Highest possible payout.

3. Account for Spousal Benefits (If Married)

  • If your spouse has a lower benefit, you may claim 50% of their PIA (if you’re the lower earner).
  • Example: If your spouse’s PIA = $2,000, you could receive $1,000/month (even if your own PIA is higher).

4. Factor in Work History Gaps

  • If you took time off (for caregiving, unemployment, etc.), replace those years with zeros—but working more can boost your average.

5. Run Multiple Scenarios

  • Test claiming at 62 vs. 70 to see the largest financial impact.
  • Consider bridging strategies (e.g., claiming spousal benefits early while delaying your own).

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your 2026 Social Security Benefits

1. Work Longer to Increase Your AIME

Why it works: The SSA averages your highest 35 years of earnings. If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros drag down your average.

Example:

  • A 40-year career with $50K/year averages $50K × 40 = $2M total earnings → $5,000/month AIME.
  • A 30-year career with $50K/year averages $1.5M total earnings → $3,750/month AIME (a 25% drop).

Action Step:

  • If you’re under 35 years of earnings, keep working to replace low-earning years.

2. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 for the Highest Payout

Why it works: For every year you delay beyond FRA, your benefit increases by 8% until age 70.

Example:

  • PIA = $1,500/month
    • Claim at 62: $1,050/month
    • Claim at 66 (FRA): $1,500/month
    • Claim at 70: $1,800/month (+$300/month)

Action Step:

  • If you can afford to wait, delaying until 70 can increase lifetime benefits by tens of thousands.

3. Claim Spousal Benefits Early (If Your Spouse Has a Higher PIA)

Why it works: If your spouse has a higher benefit, you can claim 50% of their PIA as early as 62—even if you haven’t filed for your own benefits yet.

Example:

  • Spouse’s PIA = $2,500/month
  • You claim spousal only at 62: $1,250/month (instead of waiting for your own lower benefit).

Action Step:

  • If your spouse is much higher earner, claim spousal benefits early to supplement income.

4. Use the "File & Suspend" Strategy (If Eligible)

(Note: This was discontinued in 2015, but alternatives exist.) Why it works: Previously, a spouse could file and suspend at 66, allowing their benefit to grow until 70 while still receiving spousal payments.

Modern Alternative:

  • Claim restricted application (if married) to receive spousal benefits while delaying your own for higher payouts.

Action Step:

  • Consult a Social Security planner to explore restricted application strategies.

5. Optimize Earnings in High-Income Years

Why it works: The SSA indexes earnings to inflation, so earning more in high-wage years boosts your AIME.

Example:

  • If you earn $150K in 2026 (vs. $50K in earlier years), that year counts more heavily in your AIME calculation.

Action Step:

  • If possible, maximize earnings in your peak working years (especially if under 35 years of work).

6. Consider the "Claim Now, Claim More Later" Strategy

Why it works: If you need income early, you can claim reduced benefits now and switch to a higher delayed amount later (if eligible).

Example:

  • Claim at 62 for $1,000/month, then switch to a higher delayed amount at 70 (if your PIA increases).

Action Step:

  • Use our calculator to compare early vs. delayed claiming to see which strategy fits your financial needs.

7. Factor in Taxes on Social Security Benefits

Why it works: Up to 85% of your benefits may be taxable if your income + half your benefits exceed $44K (single) or $55K (married).

Example:

  • If your combined income = $50K, you may owe taxes on $200/month of benefits.

Action Step:

  • Use a Social Security tax calculator to estimate tax liability and adjust claiming strategies accordingly.

8. Plan for Survivor Benefits (If Applicable)

Why it works: If you’re married or have dependents, survivor benefits can provide up to 100% of the deceased spouse’s PIA.

Example:

  • If your spouse dies at 66, your survivor benefit = $1,500/month (even if your own PIA is lower).

Action Step:

  • Ensure your benefits are coordinated with life insurance and estate planning.
