Why Your Social Security Estimate in 2026 Matters More Than Ever

Social Security isn’t just a safety net—it’s often the largest source of retirement income for many Americans. But with cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs) fluctuating and the Trust Fund’s long-term sustainability in question, your benefit amount could be higher or lower than you expect.

Recent Trends Affecting Social Security Benefits

  • 2023 COLA (8.7%) – The highest increase in 40 years, helping offset inflation.
  • 2024 COLA (3.2%) – A smaller adjustment due to easing inflation.
  • Projected 2026 Benefits – The SSA estimates that the average retiree will receive around $2,000 per month in 2026 (if no major policy changes occur). However, individual benefits vary widely based on earnings history, claiming age, and other factors.

If you’re 5 years away from retirement, even a small difference in your claiming strategy could mean thousands more (or less) per year in benefits.

8 Proven Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

Now that you understand how the calculator works, let’s explore how to boost your benefits—legally and strategically.

Strategy 1: Delay Claiming Until Age 70 (If Possible)

The Rule: For every year you delay beyond your full retirement age (FRA), your benefit increases by 8% per year until age 70.

Real-World Example:

  • John (born 1960, FRA = 67) claims at 62 and receives $1,500/month.
  • Jane (same birth year) waits until 70 and gets $2,400/month—an extra $1,200/month for life.

Why It Works:

  • The delayed retirement credit (DRC) compensates for the lost years of inflation-adjusted benefits.
  • If you expect to live 10+ years, delaying is almost always the best move.

Exception: If you have health concerns or financial need, claiming early may be necessary.

Strategy 2: Use the "File & Suspend" or "Restricted Application" Tactics (If Still Available)

(Note: These strategies are phasing out but may still apply to some retirees.)

The Rule:

  • File & Suspend: One spouse files for benefits but suspends them, allowing the other to claim spousal benefits while the first accumulates delayed credits.
  • Restricted Application: Claim spousal benefits without claiming your own, letting your own benefit grow until 70.

Real-World Example:

  • Mark (66, FRA) files & suspends his benefits, allowing his wife, Lisa (62), to claim 50% of his benefit ($1,200/month) while his own grows to $2,400/month by age 70.

Why It Works:

  • Dual income in retirement (spousal + own benefit).
  • Maximizes lifetime payouts for both spouses.

Note: The Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015 ended these strategies for most retirees, but some born before 1954 may still qualify.

Strategy 3: Work Longer to Increase Your AIME

The Rule: Social Security replaces about 40% of your average earnings (capped at a certain amount). The more you earn in your highest-35-year period, the higher your benefit.

Real-World Example:

  • Sarah (55) earns $60,000/year for 10 years, then switches to a $120,000/year job for 25 years.
  • Her AIME is based on those high-earning years, boosting her benefit significantly compared to someone who maxed out at $60K.

How to Apply This:

  • If you’re under 62, consider working longer to increase your earnings average.
  • If you’re close to FRA, delay Social Security while working to boost your AIME.

Strategy 4: Manage Your Taxable Income to Avoid Benefit Taxes

The Rule: If your combined income (SS + wages + interest) exceeds $25K (single) or $32K (married), up to 85% of your benefits may be taxed.

Real-World Example:

  • Tom (single, $40K SS + $30K pension) has $70K total income—50% of his SS is taxable.
  • If he reduces taxable income (e.g., by contributing to a Roth IRA), he could lower his taxable SS portion.

How to Reduce Taxable Income: ✔ Convert traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth (taxed now, not in retirement). ✔ Delay claiming Social Security until after RMDs (Required Minimum Distributions) start. ✔ Use tax-loss harvesting in investment accounts.

Strategy 5: Claim Spousal Benefits Strategically

The Rule: If you’re married, you can claim up to 50% of your spouse’s benefit (even if they haven’t filed yet).

Real-World Example:

  • Mike (67, $2,500/month benefit) lets his wife, Susan (62), claim $1,250/month (50% of his benefit) while he waits until 70 for $3,200/month.
  • Total household income: $4,450/month (vs. $2,500 if Susan waited).

Best For:

  • Lower-earning spouses who would otherwise get a smaller benefit.
  • Couples where one earns significantly more than the other.

Strategy 6: Use the "Claim Now, Claim More Later" Strategy for Divorced Spouses

The Rule: If you were married for 10+ years and divorced, you can claim ex-spouse’s benefit (even if they remarry).

Real-World Example:

  • Laura (65) divorced John (70) after 15 years. John’s benefit is $2,800/month.
  • Laura can claim $1,400/month (50% of John’s benefit) even if she never worked.

Why It’s Powerful:

  • No need to remarry to qualify.
  • Works even if ex-spouse claims early or delays.

Strategy 7: Consider the "Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) & Government Pension Offset (GPO)"

The Rule: If you have a government pension (e.g., teacher, firefighter), your Social Security may be reduced due to WEP or GPO.

Real-World Example:

  • Tom (teacher) gets a $2,000/month pension but only $1,200/month in Social Security due to WEP.
  • Solution: Delay claiming Social Security until 70 to maximize the benefit before the reduction kicks in.

How to Mitigate:

  • Work in the private sector to increase your private-sector earnings average.
  • Delay Social Security to reduce the impact of WEP/GPO.

Strategy 8: Plan for Survivor Benefits (If Applicable)

The Rule: If you’re married or have dependent children, your survivor benefit could be higher than your own.

Real-World Example:

  • David (67, $2,000/month benefit) dies. His wife, Ellen (65), receives $1,500/month (71.5% of his benefit).
  • If Ellen waits until 70, her benefit increases to $2,100/month—more than David ever received.

Best For:

  • Widows/widowers who rely on survivor benefits.
  • Couples where one earns significantly less than the other.

Common Mistakes That Shrink Your Social Security Benefits (And How to Fix Them)

Even the best-laid plans can backfire if you make these critical errors.

Mistake 1: Claiming at 62 Without Financial Need

The Problem: Claiming early permanently reduces your benefit by 25-30% for life.

Real-World Example:

  • Bob (62) claims $1,200/month instead of waiting until 67 ($1,800/month).
  • Total lifetime loss: Over $300,000 in missed benefits.

Solution:

  • Run projections using our calculator to see if early claiming is worth it.
  • Delay if possible, even if you need income—other assets (401(k), IRA, rental income) can bridge the gap.

Mistake 2: Not Adjusting for Inflation in Your Planning

The Problem: Social Security benefits increase with COLA, but early claimers lose purchasing power.

Real-World Example:

  • Carol (62) gets $1,500/month, but by 2030, inflation erodes her buying power.
  • If she waited until 70, her $2,400/month would be more valuable in real terms.

Solution:

  • Use a calculator that accounts for inflation (like ours).
  • Consider annuities or other inflation-protected income to supplement.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Spousal & Survivor Benefits

The Problem: Many retirees don’t claim spousal benefits because they assume their own is higher.

Real-World Example:

  • John (67, $2,500 benefit) lets his wife, Susan (62, $1,000 benefit), claim $1,250/month (50% of his benefit).
  • Total household income jumps from $3,500 to $3,750/month—a 7% increase.

Solution:

  • Always check spousal benefit eligibility before claiming your own.
  • Coordinate with your spouse to maximize combined income.

Mistake 4: Not Updating Your Earnings Record

The Problem: If the SSA has incorrect earnings data, your benefit estimate will be wrong.

Real-World Example:

  • Mike’s SSA record shows $50K/year for 10 years, but he actually earned $70K.
  • His AIME is underreported, leading to a lower benefit.

Solution:

  • Request a free Social Security statement at www.ssa.gov/myaccount.
  • Report corrections if your earnings are misreported.

Mistake 5: Claiming Social Security Before Taking Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

The Problem: If you claim Social Security before RMDs (age 72), your taxable income spikes, increasing SS taxes.

Real-World Example:

  • Tom (70) claims Social Security before RMDs, pushing him into a higher tax bracket.
  • Result: More of his SS is taxable, reducing his net benefit.

Solution:

  • Delay Social Security until after RMDs to keep taxable income lower.
  • Use Roth conversions to reduce taxable income in retirement.
