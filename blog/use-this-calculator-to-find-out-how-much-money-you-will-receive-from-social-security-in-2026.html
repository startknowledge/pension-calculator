How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before diving into the calculator, it’s crucial to understand how your benefits are determined. The SSA uses a three-step formula to calculate your monthly retirement benefit:

1. Indexing Your Earnings (Adjusting for Inflation)

The SSA adjusts your highest 35 years of earnings to account for wage growth since you worked. This is called "indexing." For 2026, the average wage index (AWI) will likely be based on 2025 earnings, which could see a smaller COLA due to lower inflation projections.

2. Calculating Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA takes your 35 highest-earning years, sums them up, and divides by 12 to get your AIME. This number is then used to determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)—the benefit you’d receive if you retired at full retirement age (FRA).

3. Applying the Bending Formula (Progressive Benefit Structure)

The SSA uses a progressive formula to calculate your PIA:

  • 90% of the first $1,174 (2026 estimate) of your AIME.
  • 32% of the next $7,062 (2026 estimate) of your AIME.
  • 15% of any amount above $8,236 (2026 estimate).

This means higher earners get a larger percentage of their benefits from the lower wage brackets, creating a progressive benefit structure.

How to Maximize Your Social Security Benefits in 2026

Knowing how benefits are calculated is just the first step. To get the most out of Social Security, you need a strategy. Here are 10 actionable tips to optimize your payouts by 2026.

1. Delay Retirement Until Full Retirement Age (FRA) or Later

Your FRA depends on your birth year:

  • 1960 or later: FRA is 67.
  • 1955-1959: FRA is 66 and 2-10 months.
  • 1954 or earlier: FRA is 66.

Why delay?

  • For every month you wait beyond FRA, you get an 8% increase in benefits until age 70.
  • If you delay until 70, you could receive up to 124% more than if you claimed at FRA.

Real-World Example: A person born in 1960 with an AIME of $5,000 would receive:

  • $2,500/month at FRA (67).
  • $3,125/month at age 70 (after 36 months of delayed credits).

That’s an extra $87,000 over 10 years—just by waiting.

2. Use the "File-and-Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

The File-and-Suspend rule was phased out in 2016, but if you were born before 1954, you may still qualify under certain conditions. This strategy allowed one spouse to claim benefits early while the other delayed for maximum growth.

Alternative for Younger Retirees:

  • Claim spousal benefits early (as early as 62) while your spouse delays until 70.
  • Once your spouse reaches 70, switch to experienced spouse benefits (which can be 50% of their full benefit).

3. Claim Spousal or Survivor Benefits Strategically

If you’re married (or divorced for at least 10 years), you may qualify for:

  • Spousal benefits (up to 50% of your spouse’s full benefit).
  • Survivor benefits (if your spouse passes away).

Best Strategy:

  • If your spouse has a higher benefit, claim spousal benefits at 62 while they delay until 70.
  • If you’re the higher earner, delay your claim and let your spouse take spousal benefits early.

Real-World Example: A couple where Husband A earns $120,000/year and Wife B earns $60,000/year:

  • If Wife B claims at 62, she gets $1,200/month (50% of Husband A’s FRA benefit).
  • If Husband A delays until 70, his benefit grows to $3,000/month, and Wife B can switch to experienced spouse benefits at $1,500/month (50% of his delayed benefit).

4. Consider Working Longer to Increase Your AIME

The SSA uses your 35 highest-earning years to calculate benefits. If you have years with low or no earnings, replacing them with higher-earning years can increase your AIME.

How?

  • Work past FRA to boost your indexed earnings.
  • If you have gaps in employment, fill them with higher-paying jobs before retirement.

Example: A person with 20 years of low earnings and 15 years of high earnings could replace the low years with better-paying work in their late 50s or early 60s.

5. Use the "Restricted Application" Strategy (For Younger Retirees)

If you were born before 1954, you could file a restricted application to claim spousal benefits while allowing your own benefit to grow until age 70.

How it works:

  • You claim spousal benefits at 62 (or FRA).
  • Your own benefit continues to grow until you switch to it at 70.

Note: This strategy is no longer available for those born 1954 or later, but spousal claiming rules still allow similar flexibility.

6. Factor in Taxes on Social Security Benefits

Social Security benefits are taxable if your combined income exceeds certain thresholds:

  • Single filers: Over $25,000/year (2024 limit).
  • Married couples: Over $32,000/year.

2026 Projections:

  • The taxable threshold may increase slightly due to inflation, but higher earners will still pay taxes.
  • Strategy: If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket, consider delaying benefits to reduce taxable income in early retirement.

7. Plan for Medicare and Other Retirement Income

Social Security is not enough for most retirees. You’ll need to combine it with:

  • 401(k) or IRA withdrawals.
  • Pensions (if applicable).
  • Rental income or part-time work.

Real-World Example: A retiree with:

  • $3,000/month in Social Security.
  • $2,000/month from a 401(k).
  • $500/month from a side business.

By diversifying income, they ensure financial stability even if Social Security benefits are lower than expected.

8. Avoid Common Mistakes That Reduce Your Benefits

Many retirees unintentionally reduce their Social Security payouts due to these mistakes:

Mistake #1: Claiming Too Early (Before FRA)

  • Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by up to 30% for life.
  • Solution: Wait until FRA or later unless financial hardship forces an early claim.

Mistake #2: Not Considering Spousal Benefits

  • Many single retirees miss out on spousal benefits they could have claimed.
  • Solution: If married, file a restricted application (if eligible) or claim spousal benefits early.

Mistake #3: Working Past FRA Without Reporting Earnings

  • If you work while collecting benefits, the SSA may withhold payments if you earn over $21,240/year (2024 limit).
  • Solution: Report earnings accurately and adjust claims if needed.

Mistake #4: Not Updating Your SSA Records

  • If your earnings are misreported, your AIME (and thus benefits) could be lower than expected.
  • Solution: Verify your work history with the SSA and correct any errors.

Mistake #5: Ignoring State Taxes on Social Security

  • 12 states tax Social Security benefits, including:
    • California, Florida, Texas, and others.
  • Solution: Check your state’s tax laws and adjust retirement planning accordingly.
