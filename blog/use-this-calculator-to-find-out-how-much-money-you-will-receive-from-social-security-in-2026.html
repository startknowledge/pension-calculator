How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before using a calculator, it’s essential to understand how Social Security determines your benefits. The formula is complex, but here’s a simplified breakdown:

1. Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA calculates your AIME by:

  • Adjusting your highest 35 years of earnings for inflation.
  • Indexing those earnings to today’s wage levels.
  • Taking the average of those adjusted earnings.

Example: If you earned $50,000 per year for 35 years (adjusted for inflation), your AIME would be the average of those indexed amounts.

2. The Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) – Your Full Retirement Age (FRA) Benefit

Your PIA is the benefit you’d receive if you claim at Full Retirement Age (FRA), which is:

  • 66 (for those born between 1943–1954)
  • 66 and 2 months (for 1955)
  • 66 and 4 months (for 1956)
  • 66 and 6 months (for 1957–1960)

The formula for PIA (as of 2024, with adjustments expected in 2026) is: PIA = 90% of the first $1,174 of your AIME + 32% of the next $7,062 + 15% of any amount over $8,236

Example: If your AIME is $4,000/month, your PIA would be:

  • 90% of $1,174 = $1,056.60
  • 32% of ($7,062 - $1,174) = $1,886.48
  • 15% of ($4,000 - $8,236) = $0 (since $4,000 is below the threshold) Total PIA ≈ $2,943/month

3. Adjustments Based on Claiming Age

  • Claiming before FRA (Early Retirement): Your benefit is permanently reduced by ~6.67% per year before FRA.
  • Claiming after FRA (Delayed Retirement): You get an 8% increase per year up to Age 70.
  • Claiming at Age 70: You receive the maximum possible benefit (no further increases).

Example: If your PIA is $2,500/month and you claim at Age 62 (FRA = 67), your benefit would be: $2,500 × (1 - 0.25) = $1,875/month (25% reduction for claiming 5 years early).

4. Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLA) for 2026

The 2024 COLA was 3.2%, but 2026 projections depend on inflation. If inflation remains high, retirees could see another significant increase, while low inflation might result in a smaller adjustment.

Example: If your 2026 PIA is $3,000/month and the COLA is 3.5%, your benefit would increase to $3,000 × 1.035 ≈ $3,105/month.

Step-by-Step Guide to Getting the Most Accurate Estimate

1. Gather Your Earnings History

The SSA uses your 35 highest-earning years (adjusted for inflation) to calculate benefits. To ensure accuracy:

  • Request your SSA earnings statement (via mySocialSecurity.gov).
  • Verify all reported wages—missing or misreported years can lower your benefit.
  • Include side income (self-employment, freelance work) if it affects your earnings.

Real-World Example: A retiree named Mark noticed a gap in his earnings from 2005–2007 because he was self-employed but didn’t report all income. When he updated his SSA record, his AIME increased by $200/month, boosting his PIA by $150/month.

2. Choose the Right Claiming Age

Your claiming age has the biggest impact on your benefit. Here’s how different ages affect your payout:

Claiming Age Monthly Benefit (vs. FRA) Best For
Age 62 ~70–75% of PIA Immediate income needed, lower life expectancy
Full Retirement Age (FRA) 100% of PIA Balanced approach, no penalty
Age 70 ~124% of PIA High earners, long life expectancy

Example: If your PIA is $2,800/month:

  • Claim at 62: $1,960/month
  • Claim at FRA (67): $2,800/month
  • Claim at 70: $3,472/month

Strategy: If you can afford to delay, waiting until 70 can increase your benefit by 32% compared to claiming at FRA.

3. Account for Spousal and Survivor Benefits

If you’re married or have dependents, your benefits may be affected:

  • Spousal benefits (up to 50% of your spouse’s benefit if you claim at FRA).
  • Survivor benefits (if your spouse passes away, you may receive 100% of their benefit at FRA).
  • Divorced spousal benefits (if you were married 10+ years, you may qualify even after divorce).

Example: Jane and her husband both have PIAs of $2,500. If Jane claims at FRA, she could receive:

  • Her own benefit: $2,500
  • Spousal benefit: $1,250 (50% of husband’s benefit) Total: $3,750/month

4. Factor in Future COLA Adjustments

While you can’t predict exact COLA increases, historical data suggests:

  • Average COLA since 1980: ~2.7% annually
  • Highest COLA (2023): 8.7%
  • Lowest COLA (2010–2015): 0–1.7%

Example: *If your 2026 PIA is $3,000 and COLA averages 3% annually, by 2036, your benefit could grow to ~$3,800/month.

5. Consider Taxes on Social Security Benefits

Up to 85% of your Social Security benefits may be taxable if your combined income exceeds:

  • $25,000 (single filers)
  • $32,000 (married couples)

Example: If your combined income (SS + other sources) is $40,000 (single), you may owe taxes on $2,575/month of benefits.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security Benefits in 2026

1. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 (If Possible)

  • Why? For every year you delay past FRA, your benefit increases by 8%.
  • Best for: High earners, long life expectancy, those who can afford to wait.
  • Risk: If you pass away before 70, you won’t receive the full delayed benefit.

Example: *A couple delayed claiming until 70 and received $4,500/month each, totaling $9,000/month—40% more than if they claimed at FRA.

2. Use the "File and Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

  • How it worked: One spouse could claim benefits and then suspend them, allowing the other to claim a spousal benefit while the first spouse’s benefit grew.
  • Status: No longer available (phased out in 2016), but alternatives like "Restricted Application" (for spousal benefits only) may still apply.

Alternative Strategy: If one spouse has a much higher PIA, they can claim at 62 while the other waits until 70 to maximize their own benefit.

3. Optimize Your Work History

  • Add missing years (even if you earned little) to increase your AIME.
  • Replace low-earning years with higher-earning years (if possible).
  • Consider part-time work in lower-earning years to boost your average.

Example: A teacher worked 20 years but had 15 years with low earnings. By working part-time in retirement, she increased her AIME by $150/month, raising her PIA by $100/month.

4. Claim Spousal Benefits Strategically

  • If your spouse has a higher PIA, claim spousal benefits at FRA while letting their benefit grow.
  • If you have a lower PIA, you may claim your own benefit first and then switch to spousal benefits later.

Example: Mike’s PIA is $2,000, but his wife’s is $3,500. Instead of claiming his $2,000 at 62, he waits until 67 to claim $1,750 in spousal benefits while his wife’s benefit grows.

5. Use the "Claim Now, Claim More Later" Strategy

  • Claim a reduced benefit at 62, then switch to a higher spousal or survivor benefit later.
  • Best for: Those who need income early but can afford to wait for a better benefit.

Example: Sarah claimed $1,500 at 62 but later switched to a $2,200 spousal benefit when her husband reached FRA.

6. Consider Working in Retirement (If Under FRA)

  • Earnings test: If you’re under FRA, you can earn $21,240/year ($1,770/month) without penalty in 2024 (limits adjust yearly).
  • After FRA: No earnings test, but excess earnings reduce benefits until you reach FRA.

Example: John worked part-time in retirement for $18,000/year. Since he was under FRA, he faced a $1,240 penalty ($1 for every $3 over the limit), reducing his benefit by $103/month until FRA.

7. Plan for Healthcare Costs

  • Medicare starts at 65, but Social Security doesn’t cover medical expenses.
  • Options:
    • Medicare Supplement (Medigap) – Covers gaps in Medicare.
    • Health Savings Account (HSA) – Tax-free withdrawals for medical costs.
    • Long-term care insurance – Protects against high healthcare costs.

Example: Tom and Linda budgeted $3,000/month for healthcare in retirement, leaving $6,000/month from Social Security and savings.

8. Use a Social Security Calculator Regularly

  • Update your estimate as your earnings or retirement plans change.
  • Test different claiming scenarios (e.g., claiming at 62 vs. 70).
  • Compare with pension and other retirement income to ensure financial security.

Example: Dave used a calculator to see that claiming at 67 (FRA) gave him $2,800/month, but delaying until 70 would give him $3,400/month—a $600/month difference. He decided to wait.
