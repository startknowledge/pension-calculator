Why Your Social Security Estimate Matters in 2026

Social Security is not just a safety net—it’s often the foundation of retirement income for many Americans. According to the 2023 Social Security Trustees Report, the program is projected to remain solvent until 2034, but benefits may be reduced by 20% or more if no changes are made.

By 2026, the average retiree benefit is expected to rise due to COLAs, but individual payouts vary widely based on:

  • Earnings history (your highest 35 years of work)
  • Claiming age (earliest at 62, full retirement age varies by birth year)
  • Marital status (spousal and survivor benefits)
  • Work credits (10 years of substantial earnings required)

Did you know?

  • The maximum monthly benefit in 2026 (for someone retiring at full retirement age) could reach $4,800+ (up from $4,555 in 2024).
  • If you delay claiming until age 70, your benefit could be as much as 80% higher than if you took it at 62.

Step-by-Step Guide to Getting Your Estimate

  1. Enter Your Birth Year

    • Your full retirement age (FRA) depends on when you were born.
    • Example: If you were born in 1960, your FRA is 66 and 10 months.
    • Claiming before FRA reduces benefits; delaying increases them.

  2. Input Your Estimated Earnings

    • The SSA uses your average indexed monthly earnings (AIME) over 35 years.
    • If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are counted—so working longer can boost your benefit.

  3. Select Your Retirement Age

    • Claiming at 62? You’ll get ~70% of your full benefit.
    • Waiting until 70? You could get ~124% of your full benefit.
    • Example: If your full benefit is $2,000/month, claiming at 62 gives $1,400, but waiting until 70 could yield $2,480.

  4. Add Spousal or Survivor Benefits (If Applicable)

    • If married, you may qualify for spousal benefits (up to 50% of your spouse’s benefit if you claim at FRA).
    • Widows/widowers can receive 100% of the deceased spouse’s benefit at FRA.

  5. Get Your 2026 Estimate

    • The calculator adjusts for projected COLAs and inflation.
    • Example: If your current estimate is $2,200/month, by 2026, it could be $2,500+ after adjustments.

8 Proven Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security Benefits in 2026

Knowing your estimate is just the first step—optimizing it ensures you get the most possible. Here’s how:

1. Work Longer to Increase Your Benefit

Why it works: The SSA calculates benefits based on your highest 35 years of earnings. If you have years with low or no income, working longer can replace those zeros with higher earnings, boosting your AIME.

Real-World Example:

  • John (55 years old) had a gap in earnings when he was unemployed for two years.
  • By working two more years before retiring, he increased his AIME from $2,800 to $3,200, raising his monthly benefit by $200.

Action Step:

  • If you’re under 62, consider delaying retirement to add high-earning years.
  • If you’re already retired, check if part-time work could improve your benefit.

2. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 for Maximum Growth

Why it works: For every month you delay claiming after FRA, your benefit increases by ~8%. This compounding effect can add thousands over your lifetime.

Real-World Example:

  • Sarah (65, FRA at 66) could claim at 66 and get $2,500/month.
  • If she waits until 70, her benefit jumps to $3,450/month—a $950/month increase.
  • Over 20 years, that’s an extra $228,000 in benefits.

Action Step:

  • If you’re healthy and financially stable, delaying until 70 is often the best choice.
  • If you need income early, consider claiming at FRA and supplementing with other savings.

3. Coordinate Benefits with Your Spouse for Higher Payouts

Why it works: Married couples can optimize benefits by strategically claiming at different times.

Real-World Example:

  • Mike (66, FRA) and Lisa (64, FRA at 65) both have benefits of $2,000/month.
  • If Lisa claims at 65, she gets $2,000, but Mike waits until 70 and gets $2,800.
  • Now, Lisa can switch to spousal benefits (50% of Mike’s benefit) at $1,400—better than her own $2,000.
  • Total combined benefit: $4,200/month (vs. $4,000 if both claimed at 66).

Action Step:

  • Use the Social Security spousal benefit calculator to test different claiming strategies.
  • If one spouse has a much higher benefit, the other may benefit from switching to spousal benefits.

4. Avoid Early Claiming (Especially If You Have Long Life Expectancy)

Why it works: Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by up to 30%. If you live past 80, this loss adds up to hundreds of thousands.

Real-World Example:

  • David (62) claims $1,500/month instead of waiting until 70 for $2,400.
  • If he lives to 85, he loses $120,000 in benefits.
  • If he lives to 90, the loss grows to $240,000.

Action Step:

  • Use a life expectancy calculator to see if delaying is worth it.
  • If you have family history of longevity, delaying is almost always better.

5. Consider the "File and Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

Why it works: Before 2015, couples could use "File and Suspend" to let one spouse claim early while the other delayed for maximum growth. While this strategy is no longer available, similar tactics exist.

Real-World Example:

  • Tom (66) files and suspends his benefit, allowing Jane (64) to claim spousal benefits at $1,200/month.
  • Tom then restarts his benefit at 70, getting $3,000/month.
  • Jane can switch to her own benefit ($2,000) or keep spousal benefits—whichever is higher.

Action Step:

  • If you’re close to FRA, consult a Social Security planner to explore alternatives.
  • Some states allow restricted applications for spousal benefits.

6. Factor in Taxes on Social Security Benefits

Why it works: Up to 85% of your Social Security benefits may be taxable, depending on your income and filing status.

Real-World Example:

  • Single filer with $32,000 income + $2,000 SS benefit may owe taxes on $800 of that benefit.
  • Married couple with $44,000 income + $3,000 SS benefit may owe taxes on $1,200.

Action Step:

  • Use the Social Security tax calculator to estimate your tax liability.
  • Consider tax-efficient withdrawal strategies (e.g., Roth conversions) to reduce taxes.

7. Plan for Healthcare Costs (They’ll Eat Into Your Benefits)

Why it works: Medicare premiums, prescription drugs, and long-term care can cut deeply into Social Security income.

Real-World Example:

  • Ellen (70) gets $2,500/month in benefits.
  • After Medicare Part B ($174.70/month), Part D ($35/month), and supplemental insurance ($200/month), she’s left with $1,930.
  • If she needs long-term care ($5,000/month), she’ll deplete savings quickly.

Action Step:

  • Budget for healthcare—consider Medicare Advantage plans or supplemental insurance.
  • Look into long-term care insurance if possible.

8. Use the "Windfall Elimination Provision" (WEP) and "Government Pension Offset" (GPO) Workarounds

Why it works: If you have a government pension (e.g., teacher, firefighter), your Social Security benefit may be reduced due to WEP or GPO.

Real-World Example:

  • Gary (a teacher) has a $2,000/month pension and $1,500/month SS benefit.
  • Due to WEP, his SS benefit is reduced by ~$100/month.
  • If he retires at 62, the reduction is even worse.

Action Step:

  • Check if you qualify for WEP or GPO adjustments.
  • Some states offer supplemental pensions to offset these reductions.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands in 2026

Many retirees unintentionally reduce their Social Security benefits due to these avoidable mistakes:

❌ Mistake 1: Claiming Too Early Without Considering Longevity

  • Problem: Claiming at 62 instead of 66 can permanently reduce your benefit by 25-30%.
  • Solution: If you expect to live past 80, delaying is almost always better.

❌ Mistake 2: Not Coordinating with Your Spouse

  • Problem: If one spouse claims early, the other may miss out on higher spousal benefits.
  • Solution: Use Social Security’s benefit planners to test different scenarios.

❌ Mistake 3: Ignoring Taxes on Benefits

  • Problem: Many retirees don’t account for taxes, leading to unexpected deductions.
  • Solution: Use a tax calculator to estimate your liability.

❌ Mistake 4: Working Past Full Retirement Age Without Reporting Earnings

  • Problem: If you earn more than the SS earnings limit ($22,320 in 2024), benefits are reduced.
  • Solution: Report earnings accurately to avoid unnecessary deductions.

❌ Mistake 5: Not Updating Your SSA Records

  • Problem: If your earnings are misreported, your benefit calculation is off.
  • Solution: Verify your work history on the SSA website and correct errors.

❌ Mistake 6: Assuming Your Benefit Will Keep Up with Inflation

  • Problem: COLAs are not guaranteed—they depend on inflation.
  • Solution: Diversify income with pensions, investments, or part-time work.
