How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before diving into the calculator, it’s crucial to understand the three key factors that determine your Social Security benefit:

1. Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA calculates your AIME by:

  • Adjusting your 35 highest-earning years (even if you worked fewer) for inflation.
  • Taking the average of those adjusted earnings.
  • Using a progressive formula (not a simple average) to determine your benefit.

Example: If your highest 35 years average $80,000 per year, your AIME would be adjusted for inflation, and then a portion of that amount would be used to calculate your monthly benefit.

2. Your Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Your FRA (currently 66–67, depending on your birth year) is when you qualify for 100% of your primary insurance amount (PIA). Claiming before FRA reduces benefits, while delaying past FRA increases them.

Birth Year Full Retirement Age (FRA)
1960 or earlier 66
1961–1962 66 & 2/3 months
1963–1964 66 & 4/3 months
1965–1967 66 & 6/3 months
1968 or later 67

Example: If you were born in 1965, your FRA is 66 and 8 months. Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by ~30%, while waiting until 70 increases it by ~24%.

3. The Social Security Cost-of-Living Adjustment (COLA)

Since 2026 isn’t here yet, the 2025 COLA (3.2%) is the most recent adjustment. However, the 2026 COLA will depend on inflation trends in the coming year.

Pro Tip: If inflation remains high, your 2026 benefit could increase by 3–5%, but if inflation drops, the adjustment may be smaller.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

1. Delay Claiming Until at Least Your Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Why? For every month you delay past FRA, your benefit increases by ~8% until age 70.

Real-World Example: A couple in their late 60s delayed claiming until 70. By doing so, they increased their combined monthly benefit by $1,200—enough to cover their mortgage and healthcare costs.

How to Apply:

  • Use our calculator to compare claiming at 62 vs. 70.
  • If possible, wait until 70 unless you need the money early.

2. Use the "File & Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

(Note: This strategy was phased out in 2016, but some older retirees may still benefit.)

Alternative for Younger Retirees:

  • Claim spousal benefits early (at 62) while delaying your own benefit until 70.
  • This allows you to collect spousal income while growing your own benefit.

Example: *A 62-year-old with a spouse who claims benefits could receive $1,500/month while their own benefit grows to $3,500/month by age 70.

3. Optimize Your Work History for Higher Earnings

The SSA uses your 35 highest-earning years—so if you have gaps (e.g., unemployment, caregiving), earning more in later years can boost your benefit.

How to Fix Low Earnings:

  • Work longer if you’re under 62.
  • Replace low-earning years with higher-paying jobs.
  • Consider freelance or side income to increase reported earnings.

Example: *A teacher with 20 years of earnings at $40K/year replaced 15 years of lower earnings with $80K/year in corporate roles, increasing their benefit by $300/month.

4. Claim Spousal Benefits Strategically

If you’re married, you can claim up to 50% of your spouse’s benefit at FRA (or earlier with a reduction).

Best Strategy:

  • If your spouse has a higher benefit, claim spousal benefits at 62 while delaying your own until 70.
  • If your benefit is higher, delay your claim and let your spouse take spousal benefits.

Example: *A stay-at-home parent with a high-earning spouse could claim $1,200/month in spousal benefits while their own benefit grows to $3,000/month by 70.

5. Consider the "Restricted Application" (For Those Born Before 1954)

If you were born before 1954, you can file a restricted application to claim spousal benefits while delaying your own.

How It Works:

  • Claim spousal benefits at 66 while letting your own benefit grow until 70.
  • Once you reach 70, you automatically switch to your higher benefit.

Example: A couple in their late 60s used this strategy to increase their combined monthly income by $2,500 without reducing their long-term benefits.

6. Factor in Healthcare Costs When Planning

Social Security does not cover Medicare premiums, which can cost $174–$505/month depending on income.

Solution:

  • Delay claiming Social Security until 70 to maximize benefits, then use other savings to cover healthcare.
  • Consider a Medicare Supplement Plan to reduce out-of-pocket costs.

Example: *A retiree who claimed at 62 saw their Social Security reduced by $300/month, but they also had to pay $200/month extra for Medicare Part B, leaving them with less take-home pay than if they had waited.

7. Use the "Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) & Government Pension Offset (GPO) Calculator"

If you worked for a government job with a pension, your Social Security benefit may be reduced due to WEP or GPO.

How to Avoid Surprises:

  • Run our WEP/GPO calculator to see how much your benefit could be reduced.
  • Delay claiming if possible to minimize the impact.

Example: *A teacher with a $2,000/month government pension saw their Social Security reduced by $150/month due to WEP. By waiting until 70, they offset some of the loss.

8. Plan for Taxes on Social Security Benefits

Up to 85% of your Social Security benefits may be taxable if your combined income exceeds $44,000 (single) or $55,000 (married filing jointly).

How to Reduce Taxable Income:

  • Delay claiming to lower your taxable income.
  • Use tax-advantaged accounts (Roth IRA, 401(k)) to reduce taxable Social Security.
  • Claim benefits in lower-income years (e.g., retirement vs. working years).

Example: *A retiree who claimed at 62 had $2,500/month in benefits, but $1,200 was taxable due to high income. By waiting until 70, they reduced their taxable portion to $800/month.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands in 2026

Mistake #1: Claiming Too Early Without Considering Long-Term Growth

Problem: Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by ~30% for life, which can be devastating if you live into your 80s or 90s.

Solution:

  • Use our calculator to compare 62 vs. 70.
  • Delay if possible, even if you need the money early—you can take a loan against your future benefits (via Social Security Advance) if needed.

Mistake #2: Not Accounting for Inflation & COLA

Problem: If inflation stays high, your 2026 benefit may not keep up with rising costs.

Solution:

  • Adjust your retirement savings to account for 3–5% annual inflation.
  • Consider a "Social Security Bridge Strategy" (e.g., annuities, part-time work) to supplement benefits.

Mistake #3: Ignoring Spousal & Survivor Benefits

Problem: Many retirees don’t claim spousal benefits, leaving hundreds (or thousands) on the table.

Solution:

  • File a restricted application (if eligible) to claim spousal benefits while growing your own.
  • Ensure your spouse is also optimizing their claim.

Mistake #4: Not Updating Your Earnings Record

Problem: If the SSA has incorrect earnings data, your benefit could be lower than expected.

Solution:

  • Request a free Social Security statement at www.ssa.gov/myaccount.
  • Report any missing or incorrect earnings to the SSA.

Mistake #5: Assuming Social Security Will Be Enough

Problem: Many retirees overestimate how much Social Security will cover their expenses.

Solution:

  • Run a retirement income projection (using our calculator + pension-calculator tools).
  • Have a backup plan (IRA withdrawals, rental income, part-time work).

❓ Can I still claim Social Security at 62 in 2026?

Yes, but your benefit will be permanently reduced by ~30%. If you were born in 1960 or earlier, the reduction is 25%, but for later birth years, it’s higher.

Example: *A 62-year-old born in 1965 would receive ~71.5% of their FRA benefit instead of 100%.

❓ What happens if I work while claiming Social Security before FRA?

If you’re under FRA, the SSA imposes the Retirement Earnings Test:

  • $1 in benefits for every $2 earned over $21,240 (2024 limit).
  • In the year you reach FRA, the limit is $56,520, but you lose $1 for every $3 over.

Solution:

  • Delay claiming if you expect to earn more than the limit.
  • Work strategically (e.g., reduce hours in the year before FRA).

❓ Will Social Security benefits be reduced in 2026 due to trust fund concerns?

The Social Security trust fund is projected to be depleted by 2034, but benefits will not disappear. Instead, payments will be reduced by ~20% unless Congress acts.

How to Prepare:

  • Increase retirement savings to offset potential cuts.
  • Consider private pensions or annuities as a backup.

❓ Can I claim Social Security and still work full-time?

Yes, but:

  • Before FRA, you face the Retirement Earnings Test.
  • After FRA, there’s no earnings limit, but high earnings may increase taxes on your benefits.

Example: *A 68-year-old who works full-time after FRA may see no reduction in benefits, but their taxable income could increase, leading to higher taxes on Social Security
