How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before we explore how much you’ll receive, it’s crucial to understand the three main components of Social Security calculations:

1. Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

Your AIME is the average of your 35 highest-earning years, adjusted for inflation. The SSA uses this to determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA), which is the base benefit you’d receive if you claim at Full Retirement Age (FRA).

2. The Social Security Benefit Formula (2026 Projections)

The SSA uses a progressive formula to calculate benefits:

  • 90% of the first $1,174 of your AIME (2023 benchmark; expected to adjust slightly by 2026).
  • 32% of the next $7,084 (2023 benchmark).
  • 15% of any amount above $7,084.

Note: These numbers are estimated and will be officially updated by the SSA in 2025 based on economic data.

3. Claiming Age & Benefit Adjustments

When (and how long) you claim Social Security directly impacts your monthly payout:

  • Claiming at Full Retirement Age (FRA, 66-67 depending on birth year) → 100% of your PIA
  • Claiming Early (as early as 62) → Reduced by ~25-30% (permanent reduction)
  • Delaying Until Age 70 → Up to 8% annual increase (maximum benefit boost)

Example: If your PIA is $2,000/month, claiming at 62 could reduce it to ~$1,400, while waiting until 70 could increase it to ~$2,560.

8 Proven Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

Now that you understand the basics, let’s explore actionable strategies to optimize your 2026 Social Security payout.

Strategy 1: Delay Claiming Until Age 70 (If Possible)

Why it works: For every year you delay beyond FRA (66-67), your benefit increases by ~8%. Waiting until 70 gives you the highest possible benefit for life.

Real-world example: A couple in their late 60s had a combined PIA of $3,500/month. By waiting until 70, they increased their joint benefits by $1,200/month—a $14,400 annual boost.

When to use it:

  • If you have other retirement income (401k, IRA, pension).
  • If you expect longer life expectancy (delaying benefits can pay off if you live into your 80s or 90s).

Strategy 2: Use the "File & Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

Update (2024): The File & Suspend rule was phased out in 2016, but some spousal strategies still apply.

Alternative Approach:

  • Claim spousal benefits early (if your spouse is eligible for a higher benefit).
  • Switch to your own benefit later when it grows.

Example: A retiree claimed spousal benefits at 62 ($1,200/month) while allowing their own benefit to grow until 70 ($2,800/month). At 70, they switched to their higher benefit—a $1,600/month increase.

Strategy 3: Coordinate Benefits with a Spouse (Spousal & Survivor Benefits)

If you’re married, coordinating claiming strategies can double your benefits:

  • Spousal benefits can be up to 50% of your ex-spouse’s benefit (if married for 10+ years).
  • Survivor benefits ensure your spouse (or children) receive 100% of your benefit if you pass away first.

Example: A couple with equal earnings ($4,000 PIA each) could claim $2,000/month each (spousal benefits) instead of waiting for their own benefits to grow. This provided immediate income while still allowing delayed claiming later.

Strategy 4: Work Longer to Increase Your AIME

Your 35 highest-earning years determine your benefit. If you have years with low or no earnings, working longer can boost your AIME.

How to do it:

  • Replace low-earning years with higher wages.
  • Delay retirement to add more high-earning years.

Example: A teacher with 30 years of earnings (mostly in lower-paying roles) worked an extra 5 years in a higher-paying corporate job. This increased their AIME by $1,200/month, leading to a $1,000/month higher benefit.

Strategy 5: Use the "Restricted Application" (For Those Born Before 1954)

*This strategy is no longer available for those born after 1954, but if you qualify, it allows you to:

  • Claim spousal benefits early (62) while letting your own benefit grow until 70.

Example (Pre-2016 Rule): A woman claimed spousal benefits at 62 ($1,500/month) while allowing her own benefit to grow until 70 ($3,200/month). At 70, she switched to her higher benefit—a $1,700/month increase.

Strategy 6: Consider the "Claim Now, Claim More Later" Approach

If you need income now but want to maximize future benefits, you can:

  1. Claim a reduced benefit early (e.g., at 62).
  2. Switch to a higher benefit later (if your spouse’s benefit is higher).

Example: A retiree claimed a reduced benefit at 62 ($1,200/month) but later switched to their spouse’s higher benefit ($2,500/month) when it became available.

Strategy 7: Factor in Taxes & Medicare Premiums

Social Security benefits are taxable if your combined income exceeds $25,000 (single) or $32,000 (married). Additionally, Medicare Part B premiums can increase with higher income.

How to minimize tax impact:

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands in 2026

Even small errors in claiming strategy can permanently reduce your Social Security benefits. Here are the biggest mistakes and how to avoid them.

Mistake 1: Claiming Too Early Without a Financial Plan

Problem: Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by ~25-30% permanently. If you live 10+ years, you could lose $100,000+ in lifetime benefits.

Solution:

  • Run projections using our calculator to see the long-term impact.
  • Avoid claiming early unless you absolutely need the income.

Example: A retiree claimed at 62 ($1,500/month) instead of waiting until 70 ($2,500/month). Over 20 years, they lost $240,000 in benefits.

Mistake 2: Not Considering Spousal Benefits

Problem: Many retirees don’t claim spousal benefits because they assume their own benefit is higher. But if your spouse has a higher PIA, you could be leaving thousands on the table.

Solution:

  • Check your spouse’s earnings record (via SSA).
  • Claim spousal benefits early if it provides immediate income while allowing your own benefit to grow.

Example: A couple where the wife had a lower PIA ($1,800) but the husband had a higher one ($3,000). By claiming spousal benefits ($1,500), she secured immediate income while her own benefit grew to $2,800 at 70.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Taxes on Social Security

Problem: Up to 85% of your Social Security benefits can be taxed if your combined income exceeds $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (married).

Solution:

  • Delay claiming to reduce taxable income.
  • Use tax-advantaged accounts (Roth IRA, 401k) to lower taxable Social Security income.

Example: A retiree with $50,000 in other income saw 85% of their $2,000/month benefit taxed ($1,700/month). By delaying claiming, they reduced their taxable income and saved $20,400/year.

Mistake 4: Not Updating Your Earnings Record

Problem: If your SSA earnings record is incorrect, your benefit calculation will be wrong. Missing years or underreported wages can reduce your AIME.

Solution:

  • Request a free earnings statement from the SSA.
  • Report any discrepancies immediately.

Example: A retiree discovered that 5 years of earnings were missing from their record. By correcting it, they increased their AIME by $800/month, leading to a $6,400/year higher benefit.

Mistake 5: Claiming Without Considering Medicare Premiums

Problem: Medicare Part B premiums increase for higher-income retirees (IRMAA). If you claim Social Security early and have high income, you could face higher premiums for life.

Solution:

  • Delay claiming to reduce taxable income.
  • Use a financial advisor to optimize Medicare enrollment timing.

Example: A retiree with $100,000+ in income faced $500/month higher Medicare premiums because they claimed Social Security early. By delaying, they avoided the extra cost.
