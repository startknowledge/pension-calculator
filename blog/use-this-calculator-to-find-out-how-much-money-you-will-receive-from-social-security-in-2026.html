How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated (And Why 2026 Matters)

Social Security benefits are not a fixed amount—they depend on:

  1. Your highest 35 years of earnings (adjusted for inflation)
  2. Your full retirement age (FRA) (66–67, depending on birth year)
  3. When you claim benefits (earlier = lower monthly payments, later = higher)
  4. Cost-of-living adjustments (COLA) (which will impact 2026 benefits)

The 2026 Social Security Adjustments You Need to Know

The Social Security Administration (SSA) announced in 2024 that benefits will receive a 3.2% COLA increase in 2025, but what about 2026?

While the exact 2026 COLA hasn’t been officially released yet, experts predict it could range between 2.5% and 4.5% based on:

  • Inflation trends (CPI-W, the measure used for COLAs)
  • Economic growth projections (GDP, wage growth)
  • Historical patterns (COLAs have averaged ~2.5% annually over the past decade)

Example: If you receive $1,500/month in 2024, a 3.5% COLA in 2025 would boost your benefit to ~$1,552.50. If the 2026 COLA is 3.0%, your benefit could rise to ~$1,600/month.

Key Takeaway: Even a small COLA increase can add hundreds of dollars per year to your benefits. That’s why using a precise calculator (like ours) is essential.

Step 1: Input Your Earnings History

The calculator uses your 35 highest-earning years (adjusted for inflation). If you have:

  • Gaps in employment (e.g., caregiving, unemployment), the SSA zeros out those years—so earning more later can replace lower-earning years.
  • Multiple careers, the calculator averages your best years.

Example: If you earned:

The SSA drops the lowest 5 years ($30K) and averages the top 30 years (mostly $100K).

Step 2: Select Your Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Your FRA determines your primary insurance amount (PIA)—the base benefit you’d receive if you claim at 66–67 (depending on birth year).

Example: If you were born in 1968, your FRA is 66 & 10 months. Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by ~30%, while waiting until 70 increases it by ~8% per year.

Step 3: Choose Your Claiming Age

The calculator compares three key claiming strategies:

  1. Claiming Early (62) – Lowest monthly benefit, but longer payout period.
  2. Claiming at FRA (66–67) – Base benefit, no reduction or increase.
  3. Delaying Until 70 – Highest possible benefit, but no further increases after 70.

Example: A person with a PIA of $2,000/month at FRA:

  • Claims at 62 → $1,440/month (30% reduction)
  • Claims at 70 → $2,580/month (29% increase)

Example: If your spouse has a PIA of $3,000/month, you could receive:

  • $1,500/month (50% of their benefit) if you claim at FRA.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

Knowing your estimated 2026 benefit is just the first step. To boost your income, consider these proven strategies:

1. Work Longer to Increase Your Average Earnings

The SSA averages your top 35 years of earnings. If you’re under 62, working even a few more years can replace lower-earning years with higher ones.

Example: A 50-year-old earning $80K/year for the next 5 years could replace $50K years in their average, increasing their PIA by thousands.

Action Step:

  • If you’re under 60, consider delaying retirement to boost your earnings.
  • If you’re close to FRA, side hustles or freelancing can help.

2. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 for the Highest Possible Benefit

Every year you delay beyond FRA, your benefit increases by ~8% per year until 70.

Example:

  • Claim at 67 (FRA): $2,000/month
  • Claim at 70: $2,580/month (~29% increase)

When to Use This Strategy:

  • If you expect a long lifespan (80+ years).
  • If you have other retirement income (pensions, 401k withdrawals).
  • If you don’t need benefits immediately.

Risk:

  • If you die before 70, you won’t receive the full delayed benefit.

3. Use the "File & Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

Before May 2016, spouses could use "File & Suspend" to:

  1. File for benefits at FRA (locking in the amount).
  2. Suspend benefits to allow a spouse to claim spousal benefits.
  3. Later resume benefits at a higher rate.

This strategy is no longer available, but similar tactics (like Restricted Application) can still work for those born before 1954.

Action Step:

  • If you’re 66+, consult a Social Security planner to explore alternative claiming strategies.

4. Claim Spousal Benefits Early (If Your Spouse is Delaying)

If your spouse is delaying until 70, you can claim spousal benefits as early as 62—but your benefit will be reduced by ~30%.

Example:

  • Spouse’s PIA at 70: $3,000/month
  • Your spousal benefit at 62: $1,050/month (35% reduction)
  • Your spousal benefit at 66 (FRA): $1,500/month

Best For:

  • If you need income early but your spouse can wait for a higher benefit.

5. Consider the "Claim Now, Claim More Later" Strategy

If you need benefits now but expect a higher future benefit, you can:

  1. Claim spousal benefits early (e.g., at 62).
  2. Later switch to your own benefit when it’s higher (at FRA or 70).

Example:

  • Claim spousal at 62: $1,200/month
  • Switch to own benefit at 67: $2,000/month (net gain of $800/month)

Caution:

  • The spousal benefit is gone once you switch.

6. Use the "Divorce Bonus" If You Were Married 10+ Years

Even if you’re divorced, you may qualify for ex-spouse benefits if:

  • You were married for 10+ years.
  • You’re currently unmarried.
  • Your ex-spouse is entitled to benefits.

Example:

  • Ex-spouse’s PIA at 70: $3,500/month
  • Your divorcee benefit at 67: $1,750/month (50% of their benefit)

Action Step:

  • If you’re divorced and over 62, check if you qualify.

7. Coordinate Benefits with Other Retirement Income

Social Security is taxable if your combined income exceeds certain thresholds:

  • Single filers: $25K+ in income → up to 50% taxable
  • Married filing jointly: $32K+ → up to 50% taxable
  • $34K+ (single) or $44K+ (married): Up to 85% taxable

Strategy:

  • If you have other retirement income (pensions, 401k, IRA withdrawals), delay claiming Social Security to reduce taxes.

Example:

  • 2024 Threshold: If you earn $30K/year from other sources, $25K of Social Security may be taxable.
