Why Estimating Your Social Security Benefits in 2026 Matters

According to the Social Security Trustees Report (2023), the program faces long-term financial challenges, but benefits remain a cornerstone of retirement planning for 64 million Americans (SSA, 2023). The average monthly Social Security benefit in 2024 is $1,907, but by 2026, this could rise due to projected COLA increases—currently estimated at 3.2% for 2024, with potential adjustments in the coming years.

However, the actual amount you receive depends on:

  • Your work history and earnings
  • Your full retirement age (FRA)
  • Whether you claim benefits early (as early as age 62) or delay (up to age 70)
  • Marital status (spousal and survivor benefits play a huge role)
  • Tax implications on your benefits

Without a clear estimate, you risk underestimating your needs or claiming benefits too early, which could cost you thousands in lost income over your lifetime.

How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before using our calculator, it’s essential to understand the three key steps the SSA uses to determine your monthly benefit:

1. Calculating Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA adjusts your highest 35 years of earnings (indexed for inflation) to reflect 2026 wage levels. This is called your AIME.

  • Example: If you earned $80,000 per year for 35 years, the SSA will adjust those earnings to 2026 dollars (accounting for inflation).
  • Key Point: If you worked fewer than 35 years, the SSA averages zeros for the missing years, which can lower your benefit.

2. Applying the Bending Formula (2026 Rates)

The SSA uses a progressive formula to calculate your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA), which determines your full retirement age (FRA) benefit.

Earnings Level (2026) Percentage Applied
Up to $1,174 90%
$1,174 – $7,032 32%
$7,032+ 15%

Example Calculation:

  • If your AIME is $4,000/month, your PIA would be:
    • $1,174 × 0.90 = $1,056.60
    • ($4,000 – $1,174) × 0.32 = $918.08
    • Total PIA = $1,056.60 + $918.08 = $1,974.68/month

(Note: These rates are projected for 2026 based on 2024 inflation trends.)

3. Adjusting for Claiming Age

Your actual benefit amount depends on when you claim:

  • Early (Age 62): 70–78% of PIA (reduced permanently)
  • Full Retirement Age (FRA, 66–67 depending on birth year): 100% of PIA
  • Delayed (Up to Age 70): Up to 124% of PIA (8% annual increase)

Real-World Example: A person born in 1960 (FRA = 67) with a PIA of $2,500/month would receive:

  • $1,750/month if claiming at 62 (70% reduction)
  • $2,500/month at 67 (FRA)
  • $3,100/month if delaying until 70 (24% increase)

Step-by-Step Guide to Getting an Accurate Estimate

1. Gather Your Earnings History

  • Request a my Social Security account statement from the SSA.
  • If you’re still working, ensure all wages are reported (missing years can hurt your benefit).
  • For self-employed individuals, include net earnings (after deductions).

2. Input Your Claiming Age

  • Will you claim early (62), at FRA, or delay until 70?
  • Our calculator automatically adjusts for delayed retirement credits (DRCs).

3. Account for Spousal Benefits (If Applicable)

  • If married, you can claim up to 50% of your spouse’s benefit (if you wait until FRA).
  • Example: If your spouse’s PIA is $3,000, you could receive $1,500/month (if you claim at FRA).

4. Consider Survivor Benefits

  • If you’re a widow/widower, you may qualify for 100% of your deceased spouse’s benefit at FRA.
  • Example: If your spouse died at 65 with a PIA of $2,800, you could receive $2,800/month at 60 (with reduced benefits).

5. Factor in Taxes

  • Up to 50% of benefits may be taxable if your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) is between $25,000–$34,000 (single) or $32,000–$44,000 (married).
  • Up to 85% may be taxable if earnings exceed those thresholds.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

1. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 for the Highest Possible Benefit

  • Why? For every year you delay beyond FRA, you get an 8% increase until age 70.
  • Example: Claiming at 70 instead of 67 could increase your benefit by 24%.
  • Best for: High earners who can afford to wait.

2. Use the "File and Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available for Some)

  • How it worked (pre-2016): One spouse could claim, then suspend to allow the other to claim a spousal benefit while growing their own.
  • 2026 Note: This strategy is no longer available, but spousal claiming strategies still exist.

3. Claim Spousal Benefits Early (If Your Spouse is Delaying)

  • If your spouse is delaying until 70, you can claim spousal benefits as early as 62 (but they’ll be reduced).
  • Example: If your spouse’s PIA is $3,500, you could receive $1,750/month at 62 (50% of $3,500).

4. Optimize Your Work History

  • Add missing years (even if you earned little) by replacing zeros with earlier lower-earning years.
  • Example: If you worked only 30 years, the SSA averages 5 years of zero earnings, lowering your benefit. Adding more years can increase it.

5. Consider the "Restricted Application" Strategy (For Divorced Spouses)

  • If divorced for at least 10 years, you can claim ex-spouse’s benefits (even if they’re still working).
  • Best for: Lower-earning spouses who can claim 50% of their ex’s PIA.

6. Use a "Bridge Job" to Boost Earnings Before Retirement

  • If you’re close to FRA, taking a part-time job to increase your AIME can raise your future benefit.
  • Example: Earning $50,000 in your 65th year (instead of $30,000) could increase your PIA in future calculations.

7. Minimize Taxable Social Security Income

  • Keep earnings below thresholds to avoid taxable benefits.
  • Example: If you’re single, stay below $25,000 in combined income to avoid taxes on up to 50% of benefits.

8. Plan for Medical Expenses to Preserve Benefits

  • Long-term care costs can deplete savings. Consider Medicare supplements to avoid Medicare premiums eating into benefits.
  • Example: If your Part B premium is $170.10/month, but your benefit is $2,000/month, this is manageable. But if benefits are $1,500/month, it could be a significant burden.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands in 2026

1. Claiming Benefits Too Early Without Considering Future Needs

  • Mistake: Taking benefits at 62 because you’re tired of working.
  • Cost: Up to 30% lower monthly payments for life.
  • Fix: Use our Social Security calculator to compare early vs. delayed claiming.

2. Not Updating Your Earnings Record

  • Mistake: Missing years of work (especially for self-employed individuals).
  • Cost: Lower AIME = reduced benefits.
  • Fix: Request a my Social Security statement and correct errors with the SSA.

3. Ignoring Spousal and Survivor Benefits

  • Mistake: Focusing only on your own benefit while missing spousal (50%) or survivor (100%) benefits.
  • Cost: Thousands in lost income if you could have claimed a higher benefit.
  • Fix: Run scenarios with our calculator for spousal and survivor options.

4. Not Factoring in Taxes

  • Mistake: Assuming all benefits are tax-free.
  • Cost: Up to 85% of benefits taxable if income exceeds thresholds.
  • Fix: Adjust retirement savings to keep earnings below taxable limits.

5. Working Past Full Retirement Age Without Understanding the Earnings Test

  • Mistake: Earning too much while claiming benefits before FRA.
  • Cost: $1 in benefits deducted for every $2 earned over $23,640 (2024 limit).
  • Fix: Delay claiming until after FRA if you plan to work.

6. Not Considering State Taxes on Social Security

  • Mistake: Assuming no state taxes apply (some states tax benefits).
  • Cost: Additional tax burden in states like California, New York, or Vermont.
  • Fix: Check state tax laws and adjust retirement plans accordingly.
