Part 2: 8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

Knowing your estimate is just the first step. To boost your payout, follow these proven strategies:

1. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 (The Biggest Boost)

The longer you wait beyond full retirement age (FRA), the higher your benefit grows—up to 8% per year until age 70.

📊 Example:

  • FRA (67): $2,000/month
  • Claim at 70: $2,560/month (a 32% increase)

Why? Social Security uses a delayed retirement credit (DRC) formula that rewards patience.

⚠️ But: If you expect a short lifespan, claiming early may be better. Use our calculator to compare lifetime vs. monthly payouts.

2. Work Longer to Increase Your Earnings Indexed Amount (EIA)

Your benefit is based on your highest 35 earning years, adjusted for inflation. If you work past 62, you can replace lower-earning years with higher wages, increasing your benefit.

💡 Example: A 60-year-old with 20 years of work history (average $40K/year) could see a $150/month boost if they work 5 more years at $60K/year.

3. Claim Spousal or Survivor Benefits Strategically

If you’re married (or divorced), you may qualify for spousal benefits—even if your own work history is lower.

📌 Key Rules:

  • Spousal benefit = 50% of your spouse’s full retirement benefit (if you claim at FRA).
  • Ex-spouse benefits are available if you were married 10+ years and are single.
  • Survivor benefits can be 100–150% of your deceased spouse’s benefit if you claim at FRA.

💡 Example: A woman with a low personal benefit ($1,200) but a husband with a high benefit ($3,000) could claim $1,500/month as a spousal benefit—**$300 more than her own payout*.*

4. Use the "File & Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

Before April 2017, couples could use "File & Suspend" to let one spouse claim early while the other delayed for a higher payout. While this rule changed, similar tactics still work for some.

🔹 Alternative: Restricted Application (if born before 1954) allows claiming spousal benefits while delaying your own.

5. Minimize Taxes on Your Social Security Benefits

Up to 85% of your Social Security income may be taxable if your combined income exceeds $32,000 (single) or $44,000 (married filing jointly).

💡 How to Reduce Taxes:

  • Delay claiming (lower taxable income in early retirement).
  • Use Roth IRA conversions to shift taxable income to lower-bracket years.
  • Claim benefits in lower-income years (e.g., part-time work in retirement).

6. Consider a "Bridge Job" Before Full Retirement

Working part-time after 62 but before FRA can: ✅ Increase your earnings record (boosting future benefits). ✅ Keep you in the workforce (delaying full retirement). ✅ Provide supplemental income (reducing reliance on early Social Security).

💡 Example: A 63-year-old who works part-time at $20K/year could see a $50–$100/month increase in their benefit when they finally claim at 66.

7. Avoid Early Claiming If Possible (The 62 Trap)

Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by 25–30% for life. For many, this is not worth it.
