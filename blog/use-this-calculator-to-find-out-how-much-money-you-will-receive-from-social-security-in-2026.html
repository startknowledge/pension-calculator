How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated (The Basics)

Before diving into the 2026 projections, it’s essential to understand how Social Security benefits are determined. The SSA uses a three-step formula to calculate your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA), which is the foundation of your benefits.

Step 1: Indexing Your Earnings

The SSA adjusts your earnings history for inflation to reflect current wage levels. This ensures that higher earners in past decades aren’t unfairly penalized.

Step 2: Calculating Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA takes your 35 highest-earning years (even if you worked fewer than 35 years) and averages them. This is called your AIME.

Step 3: Applying the Benefit Formula

Your PIA is calculated using a progressive formula:

  • 90% of the first $1,174 (in 2024) of your AIME
  • 32% of the next $7,062 (in 2024)
  • 15% of any amount above $8,236 (in 2024)

This formula ensures that higher earners receive a smaller percentage of their earnings in benefits compared to lower earners.

How Does This Apply to 2026?

The 2024 benefit formula will adjust slightly in 2025 and 2026 due to COLA increases and wage index adjustments. The SSA releases annual updates, but we can estimate trends based on historical data.

For example:

  • If inflation remains moderate, the bend points (the thresholds in the formula) may increase by 2-4%.
  • If wages grow faster than inflation, your AIME could rise, leading to a higher PIA.

8 Key Factors That Affect Your 2026 Social Security Benefits

Your 2026 payout isn’t set in stone—it depends on several critical factors. Understanding these will help you optimize your claiming strategy.

1. Your Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Your FRA (currently 66-67, depending on your birth year) is the age at which you receive 100% of your PIA. Claiming before FRA reduces your benefit, while delaying beyond FRA increases it by 8% per year until age 70.

Example:

  • If your FRA is 67, claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by ~30%.
  • Delaying until 70 increases it by ~24% compared to FRA.

2. Your Earnings History (The 35-Year Rule)

Social Security uses your 35 highest-earning years to calculate benefits. If you worked fewer than 35 years, the SSA zeros out the missing years, lowering your average.

Real-World Example: A teacher who worked 30 years but took 5 years off to raise children will have 5 years of $0 earnings in their calculation, reducing their average wage and thus their benefits.

Solution: If you have low-earning years, consider working longer or replacing those years with higher earnings.

3. The Cost-of-Living Adjustment (COLA)

The COLA is an annual increase in benefits based on inflation. In 2023, the COLA was 8.7%, the highest in decades, but future increases depend on economic conditions.

2026 Projection:

  • If inflation stays around 3-4%, the COLA could be 3-5%.
  • If inflation spikes, benefits may rise more significantly.

How to Prepare:

  • Use our Social Security Calculator to see how COLA projections affect your 2026 payout.
  • Consider supplemental income sources (like pensions or investments) if benefits feel insufficient.

4. Marital Status & Spousal Benefits

If you’re married, you may qualify for spousal benefits, which can be up to 50% of your spouse’s PIA (if you claim at FRA).

Example:

  • John retires at 67 with a $2,500/month benefit.
  • Jane, who never worked outside the home, can claim $1,250/month (50% of John’s PIA) at 66.

Key Strategy:

  • If one spouse has a much higher benefit, the other can claim spousal benefits while waiting for their own to grow.

5. Divorce & Ex-Spousal Benefits

Even if you’re divorced, you may still qualify for ex-spousal benefits if:

  • You were married for at least 10 years.
  • You’re currently unmarried.
  • The ex-spouse is entitled to Social Security.

Example:

  • Sarah divorced Mike after 12 years of marriage.
  • Mike retires at 67 with a $3,000/month benefit.
  • Sarah can claim $1,500/month (50%) even if she never worked.

6. Working While Claiming Benefits (The Earnings Test)

If you claim before FRA, the SSA applies an earnings test:

  • $21,240 limit in 2024 ($1,770/month).
  • For every $2 earned above the limit, the SSA deducts $1 from your benefit.
  • In the year you reach FRA, the limit drops to $56,520, but benefits are adjusted after FRA.

Solution:

  • If you must work, consider delaying benefits until FRA to avoid penalties.
  • If you’re close to FRA, the earnings test no longer applies after that year.

7. Government Pension Offset (GPO) & Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)

If you receive a government pension (e.g., from a state or local job), your Social Security benefits may be reduced due to:

  • GPO: Reduces spousal or survivor benefits if you (or your spouse) receive a government pension.
  • WEP: Lowers benefits if you have limited private-sector work history.

Example:

  • Tom worked for 20 years as a state employee and receives a $2,000/month pension.
  • His Social Security benefit is reduced by 50% of his pension (under GPO) when claiming spousal benefits.

Workaround:

  • Delay claiming until FRA or beyond to minimize reductions.
  • Consult a financial advisor to structure benefits optimally.

8. Taxes on Social Security Benefits

Up to 85% of your Social Security benefits may be taxable, depending on your income level:

  • Single filers: Taxable if provisional income > $25,000.
  • Married couples: Taxable if provisional income > $32,000.

Example:

  • Lisa (single) has $30,000 in other income + $2,000/month in benefits.
  • Her provisional income ($36,000 + half of benefits) exceeds $25,000, so up to 50% of her benefits may be taxed.

Strategy:

  • Delay claiming to reduce taxable income.
  • Use tax-efficient accounts (like Roth IRAs) to lower taxable income.

Example 2: The Traditional Retiree (Claims at FRA)

Background:

  • Sarah, born in 1960, has an FRA of 67.
  • Her PIA is $1,800/month.
  • She retires at 67 and stops working.

Outcome:

  • Monthly benefit at 67: $1,800
  • Annual benefit (2026): $21,600

Lesson: Claiming at FRA ensures full benefits with no penalties, and taxes may be minimal if you have low other income.

Background:

  • David, born in 1955, has an FRA of 66.
