How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before you can use a calculator, you need to understand how Social Security benefits are determined. The SSA uses a three-step formula based on your highest 35 years of earnings.

Step 1: Index Your Earnings to Inflation

The SSA adjusts your earnings history for inflation to ensure benefits keep up with the cost of living. For 2026, the indexing factor will depend on the average wage index (AWI) for the year.

  • Example: If you earned $60,000 in 2020, the SSA will adjust that amount to 2026 dollars using the CPI (Consumer Price Index).

Step 2: Calculate Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA takes your 35 highest-earning years (even if you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are used for missing years) and calculates the average.

  • Example: If your top 35 years average $5,000/month, your AIME is $5,000.

Step 3: Apply the Bending Formula (Primary Insurance Amount - PIA)

This is where the progressive benefit formula comes into play. The SSA uses three bend points to determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)—the benefit you’d receive if you claimed at full retirement age (FRA).

For 2026, the bend points (based on projected SSA data) will likely be:

  1. 90% of the first $1,173 of your AIME.
  2. 32% of the next $695 (from $1,173 to $1,868).
  3. 15% of any amount above $1,868.

Example Calculation (2026 Projection)

Let’s say your AIME is $3,000/month:

  • First $1,173 × 90% = $1,055.70
  • Next $695 ($1,868 - $1,173) × 32% = $222.40
  • Remaining $1,132 ($3,000 - $1,868) × 15% = $169.80
  • Total PIA = $1,055.70 + $222.40 + $169.80 = $1,447.90/month

(Note: These numbers are projected—the SSA releases official bend points in October of each year for the following year.)

How Claiming Age Affects Your Benefit

Your benefit amount changes depending on when you claim:

  • Claiming before FRA (Early Retirement, as early as age 62): Reduction of 6.67% per year (up to 30 months early).
  • Claiming at FRA (66-67, depending on birth year): No reduction, full PIA.
  • Claiming after FRA (Delayed Retirement, up to age 70): 8% annual increase per year.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

Now that you know how benefits are calculated, here are 8 proven strategies to increase your payout in 2026 and beyond.

Strategy 1: Delay Claiming Until Age 70 (The 8% Rule)

Why it works: For every year you delay beyond FRA, your benefit increases by 8%—up to age 70.

Example:

  • If your FRA benefit is $1,500, delaying to 70 gives you $1,920/month (a $420/month increase).
  • Over 20 years, that’s an extra $100,800 in benefits.

When to use it: ✔ If you have other retirement income (pensions, 401(k), rental income). ✔ If you’re in good health and expect a long retirement. ✔ If you don’t need the money yet and can cover expenses.

Risk: ✔ If you die before 70, you won’t get the full increase (benefits stop at death).

Strategy 2: Claim Spousal Benefits Early (If You’re the Lower Earner)

Why it works: If one spouse has a much higher earning record, the other can claim spousal benefits as early as 62 (up to 50% of the higher earner’s FRA benefit).

Example:

  • Husband’s FRA benefit: $2,500
  • Wife claims at 62: $1,250 (50% of $2,500)
  • If she waits until FRA (67): $1,250 (same as early claim, but no reduction).

Best for: ✔ Stay-at-home parents or lower earners in a marriage. ✔ Couples where one spouse has a much higher income.

Risk: ✔ If the higher earner dies first, spousal benefits disappear.

Strategy 3: Use the "File & Suspend" or "Restricted Application" (If You’re 66+)

Why it works: Before 2016, you could file & suspend at FRA to let your spouse claim spousal benefits while you delayed for more money. While this option is gone, some alternatives remain:

Option A: Claim at FRA, Then Delay for More

  • Claim at 66-67 (FRA), then switch to delayed retirement credits at 70.

Option B: Restricted Application (For Spousal Benefits Only)

  • If you’re 62+, you can claim spousal benefits only while delaying your own benefit until 70.

Example:

  • Husband (67, FRA): Claims $2,500 at FRA.
  • Wife (65): Claims spousal benefits ($1,250) while delaying her own benefit until 70 (where it grows to $2,000).

Best for: ✔ Couples where one spouse has a much lower earning record. ✔ Those who don’t need the money yet but want spousal income early.
