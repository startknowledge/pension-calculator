How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before using the calculator, it’s essential to understand how benefits are determined. The SSA uses a three-step formula:

1. Calculate Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

  • The SSA takes your highest 35 years of earnings (indexed for inflation).
  • If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are used for missing years (reducing your benefit).
  • Example: If you earned $80,000/year for 30 years and $0 for the remaining 5, your AIME is based on $80K × 30 + $0 × 5 = $2.4M total earnings.

2. Apply the Bending Formula (2026 Rates)

The SSA uses a progressive scale to determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA):

Bend Point (2026) Percentage Applied
First $1,174/month 90%
Next $706/month 32%
Everything above 15%

Example Calculation:

  • If your AIME is $3,000/month:
    • First $1,174 × 0.90 = $1,056.60
    • Next $706 × 0.32 = $225.92
    • Remaining $1,119 × 0.15 = $167.85
    • Total PIA = $1,056.60 + $225.92 + $167.85 = $1,450.37/month

3. Adjust Based on Claiming Age

  • Claim at FRA (66–67): You receive 100% of your PIA.
  • Claim Early (62): Benefits are reduced by ~30% (varies by birth year).
  • Delay Until 70: Benefits increase by 8% per year (up to 124% of PIA).

Real-World Impact: A person with a $2,000/month PIA who claims at 62 gets $1,400/month, while waiting until 70 could receive $2,480/month—a $1,080/month difference.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your 2026 Social Security Benefits

Not all retirees claim benefits the same way—and some strategies can increase payouts by thousands per year. Here’s how to optimize yours:

1. Work Until at Least Full Retirement Age (FRA)

  • Why? Every year you delay after FRA increases benefits by 8%.
  • Example: A person born in 1960 (FRA = 67) who waits until 70 gets 124% of their PIA instead of 100%.

2. Delay Claiming If You Can Afford To

  • Best for: High earners or those with long life expectancies.
  • Trade-off: You won’t receive benefits for 3–8 years, but the lifetime payout is higher.
  • Real-World Case: A couple where one spouse dies early—delaying benefits for the survivor can mean $100K+ in extra lifetime income.

3. Use the "File & Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

  • How it worked (pre-2015): One spouse could file for benefits and suspend, allowing the other to claim spousal benefits while the first accrued delayed credits.
  • 2026 Update: This strategy is phased out, but spousal benefits still exist—claiming at FRA (67) for spousal benefits can be smart.

4. Claim Spousal Benefits Early (If Your Spouse Is Delaying)

  • Rule: You can claim up to 50% of your spouse’s full benefit at FRA (66–67).
  • Example: If your spouse’s PIA is $3,000, you can get $1,500/month at FRA—even if you haven’t worked enough for a full benefit.

5. Use the "Restricted Application" for Spousal Benefits (If Born Before 1954)

  • How it works: You can claim spousal benefits only while allowing your own benefit to grow until 70.
  • Best for: Lower earners married to high earners.

6. Consider Working Part-Time in Retirement

  • Why? If you keep earning, the SSA recalculates your AIME—higher earnings = higher benefits.
  • Caution: If you’re under FRA, earnings above $21,240 (2024 limit) reduce benefits by $1 for every $2 earned.

7. Factor in Divorce or Ex-Spousal Benefits

  • Ex-spouse benefits can be claimed if married 10+ years and the ex-spouse hasn’t remarried.
  • Example: A divorced woman whose ex-husband earned $120K/year could claim 50% of his benefit even if she never worked.

8. Use a Financial Advisor to Optimize Your Strategy

  • Why? The best claiming strategy depends on health, life expectancy, and other income sources.
  • Example: A retiree with $5M in savings may not need Social Security as much as someone with $200K in savings—adjusting claiming age accordingly.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands (And How to Avoid Them)

Many retirees make critical errors that reduce their benefits. Here are the biggest mistakes and how to fix them:

Mistake 1: Claiming Benefits Too Early Without a Financial Plan

  • Problem: Claiming at 62 reduces benefits by ~30%, which can be devastating if you live into your 80s.
  • Solution: Use our calculator to compare scenarios before deciding.

Mistake 2: Not Updating Your Earnings Record

  • Problem: If the SSA has incorrect earnings, your benefit could be lower than expected.
  • Solution: Verify your record on SSA.gov and report errors immediately.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Spousal or Survivor Benefits

  • Problem: Many assume only the higher earner claims benefits, but spousal benefits can be 50% of the higher earner’s PIA.
  • Solution: Married couples should coordinate claiming strategies to maximize combined income.

Mistake 4: Working Past FRA Without Adjusting Claims

  • Problem: If you keep working after FRA, the SSA recalculates your benefit—sometimes increasing it.
  • Solution: Check your benefit statement annually to ensure accuracy.

Mistake 5: Assuming Social Security Will Cover All Retirement Needs

  • Problem: The average benefit ($1,907/month in 2024) is often not enough for a comfortable retirement.
  • Solution: Combine Social Security with pensions, 401(k)s, and other income sources.

Mistake 6: Not Considering Taxes on Benefits

  • Problem: Up to 85% of Social Security benefits may be taxable if your income exceeds $44,000 (single) or $55,000 (married).
  • Solution: Use a tax calculator to estimate liability and adjust claiming age accordingly.

Mistake 7: Forgetting About Medicare Premiums

  • Problem: Part B premiums increase with higher income (IRMAA rules).
  • Solution: Delay Medicare Part B if you have other coverage to avoid extra costs.

Mistake 8: Not Exploring Government Benefits for Low-Income Retirees

  • Problem: Many retirees miss out on SSI, Medicare Savings Programs, or state supplements.
