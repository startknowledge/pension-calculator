Introduction: The State of Social Security in 2024 & Beyond

Social Security was established in 1935 as a safety net for retirees, but today, it faces major financial challenges. According to the 2023 Annual Report of the Board of Trustees of the Federal Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) and Disability Insurance (DI) Trust Funds, the OASI Trust Fund (which covers retirement benefits) is projected to be depleted by 2034.

However, even if the trust fund is exhausted, benefits are still expected to cover about 77% of scheduled payments—meaning a significant reduction in what retirees currently receive.

Key Statistics for 2024 & 2026

  • Average monthly Social Security benefit (2024): $1,907 (for retirees aged 65+).
  • Maximum monthly benefit (2024): $4,873 (for workers retiring at full retirement age (FRA) of 67).
  • Cost-of-living adjustments (COLA) for 2024: 3.2%—the highest increase since 1982.
  • Projected COLA for 2026: Estimated ~2.5% (based on inflation trends).
  • Median retirement age (2023): 62 years old (though claiming at 62 reduces benefits by up to 30%).

How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before you can estimate your benefits, you need to understand how the SSA determines your payout. The calculation is based on three key factors:

1. Your Highest 35 Years of Earnings (Indexed to Inflation)

The SSA looks at your 35 highest-earning years (even if you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are used for missing years). These earnings are adjusted for inflation to reflect their value in today’s dollars.

Example: If you earned $60,000 in 2000 (adjusted for inflation in 2026), your indexed earnings might look like this:

  • 2000: $60,000
  • 2026 (inflation-adjusted): ~$95,000

2. The Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) Formula

Your PIA is the benefit you’d receive if you claimed at full retirement age (FRA). The SSA uses a three-bracket formula to calculate it:

Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME) Percentage Applied
Up to $1,174 (2024) 90%
$1,175 – $7,624 (2024) 32%
Over $7,624 (2024) 15%

For 2026, these thresholds will likely increase due to inflation adjustments.

Example Calculation: If your AIME is $5,000/month:

  • First $1,174 × 90% = $1,056.60
  • Next $6,446 ($5,000 - $1,174) × 32% = $2,062.72
  • Total PIA = $3,119.32/month

(Note: These numbers are illustrative—use our calculator for precise estimates.)

3. Claiming Age & Benefit Adjustments

Your claiming age drastically affects how much you receive:

  • Claim at 62 (Earliest Possible Age): ~70% of PIA
  • Claim at FRA (66-67, depending on birth year): 100% of PIA
  • Delay until 70: Up to 132% of PIA (8% annual increase after FRA)

Example: If your PIA is $2,000/month:

  • Claim at 62: $1,400/month
  • Claim at 67 (FRA): $2,000/month
  • Delay until 70: $2,640/month

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

Knowing your numbers is just the first step—strategic planning can help you increase your lifetime benefits. Here are eight proven tactics to optimize your Social Security income.

1. Delay Claiming Until at Least Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Why it works:

  • Every month you delay after FRA (up to age 70) increases your benefit by 8%.
  • If you claim at 62 instead of 70, you could lose up to 30% of your PIA.

Real-World Example: A couple, John and Mary, both born in 1960 (FRA = 66).

  • John claims at 62: Receives $1,500/month (70% of PIA).
  • Mary delays until 70: Receives $2,340/month (132% of PIA).
  • Total difference over 20 years: ~$180,000 more for Mary.

Action Step:

  • If possible, delay claiming until at least FRA, and ideally until 70 if you expect a long retirement.

2. Use the "File & Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

(Note: This strategy was phased out in 2016 for most filers, but some may still benefit.)

How it worked:

  • A spouse could file for benefits and suspend them, allowing the other spouse to claim spousal benefits while the first spouse’s benefit continued to grow until 70.

Alternative for 2026:

  • Claim spousal benefits early (at 62) while delaying your own claim until 70.
  • This ensures both spouses maximize their lifetime benefits.

3. Coordinate Benefits with Your Spouse (If Married or Divorced)

Key Rules:

  • Spousal benefits can be up to 50% of your ex-spouse’s PIA (if divorced for 10+ years).
  • Survivor benefits can replace 100% of the deceased spouse’s benefit (if you’re the higher earner).

Example: A couple where the wife earns $40,000/year and the husband earns $120,000/year.

  • If the wife claims spousal benefits at 66, she could receive $600/month (50% of husband’s PIA).
  • If the husband dies first, the wife could switch to survivor benefits (likely higher than her own PIA).

Action Step:

  • Run spousal benefit scenarios in our calculator to see which strategy works best.

4. Work Longer to Increase Your PIA

How it works:

  • Every year you work after FRA (up to age 70) replaces a lower-earning year in your 35-year average.
  • This boosts your PIA, leading to higher benefits for life.

Example: A worker with 30 years of earnings (including a few low-earning years in their 20s).

  • By working 5 more years, they replace a $0 year with a $50,000/year salary.
  • This increases their PIA by ~$300/month.

Action Step:

  • If you’re close to retirement, consider working a few more years to improve your benefit.

5. Manage Taxable Income to Reduce Social Security Taxes

Social Security benefits may be taxed if your combined income exceeds certain thresholds:

  • Single filers: Over $25,000/year (up to 50% taxable).
  • Married couples: Over $32,000/year (up to 50% taxable).
  • Over $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (married): Up to 85% taxable.

Strategies to Reduce Taxable Income: ✔ Delay claiming benefits (lower taxable income in early retirement). ✔ Use Roth IRA conversions (shift taxable income to lower brackets). ✔ Delay taking required minimum distributions (RMDs) from 401(k)s/IRA until after 70½.

Example: A retiree with $50,000 in Social Security + $30,000 in pension income.

  • If they delay claiming SS until 70, their taxable income drops, reducing their tax burden.

6. Consider the "Restricted Application" Strategy (For Older Couples)

(Note: This is only available if you were born before 1954.)

How it works:

  • One spouse claims spousal benefits early (at 62), while the other delays their own claim until 70.
  • The earlier claimer’s benefit stops growing, but the later claimer maximizes theirs.

Example: A couple where the husband (born 1950) claims spousal benefits at 62 ($800/month), while the wife (also born 1950) delays until 70 ($3,000/month).

  • Total benefit at 70: $3,800/month (vs. $1,600 if both claimed at 62).

Action Step:

  • If you’re older (born before 1954), this strategy can significantly boost lifetime income.

7. Use a "Bridge Job" to Delay Social Security

If you retire early but need income, a part-time "bridge job" can help:

  • Increases your PIA (by replacing lower-earning years).
  • Delays claiming Social Security, allowing benefits to grow.
  • Provides taxable income, which can reduce Social Security taxes later.

Example: A retiree who claims SS at 62 but takes a part-time job until 66.

  • Their PIA increases due to higher earnings.
  • They avoid the 30% reduction by delaying until FRA.

8. Plan for Healthcare Costs to Avoid Early Claiming

One of the biggest mistakes retirees make is claiming Social Security too early to cover healthcare costs. Instead:

  • Delay Social Security and use health savings accounts (HSAs), long-term care insurance, or Medicare premium subsidies.
  • Medicare starts at 65, so if you retire early, you’ll need private insurance, which can be expensive.

Example: A couple who claims SS at 62 but spends $20,000/year on healthcare.

  • By delaying until 66, they save $1,400/month in reduced benefits but avoid out-of-pocket medical costs.
