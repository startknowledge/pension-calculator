The calculator adjusts for:

  • Survivor benefits (if your spouse passes away, you may receive 100% of their benefit)

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security Benefits in 2026

Knowing your 2026 benefit estimate is just the first step. To boost your payout, consider these proven strategies:

1. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 for the Highest Possible Benefit

Why? For every year you delay beyond full retirement age (FRA), your benefit increases by 8% per year until age 70.

Example:

  • FRA Benefit: $2,000/month
  • Claim at 62: $1,400/month (30% reduction)
  • Claim at 70: $2,560/month (32% increase)

Best For: High earners who can afford to wait.

2. Use the "File and Suspend" Strategy (If Still Available)

(Note: This strategy was phased out in 2016, but variations may still apply.)

How It Worked:

  • One spouse files for benefits at FRA but suspends them.
  • The other spouse claims spousal benefits while the first spouse’s benefit grows until 70.

Alternative Today: Restricted Application (for those born before 1954) allows claiming spousal benefits only while delaying your own.

3. Claim Spousal Benefits Early, Then Switch to Your Own Later

Strategy:

  • If your spouse has a higher benefit, claim spousal benefits at 62 (50% of their FRA amount).
  • Once you reach FRA (66-67), switch to your own benefit (which will be higher).

Example:

  • Husband’s Benefit: $3,000 (FRA)
  • Wife’s Benefit: $1,500 (FRA)
  • Wife claims spousal at 62: $1,500 (50% of $3,000)
  • At 66, switches to her own $1,500 (no reduction)
  • If she waits until 70, her benefit grows to $1,920—still better than claiming at 62.

4. Work Longer to Increase Your Average Earnings

Social Security calculates benefits based on your highest 35 years of earnings. If you have years with low or no income, working longer can boost your average.

Example:

  • A teacher with 20 years of earnings (average: $40,000/year) + 15 years as a consultant (average: $80,000/year).
  • By working 5 more years, their average jumps to $60,000/year, increasing their benefit by ~20%.

5. Use the "Claim Now, Claim More Later" Strategy for Divorced Spouses

If you’re divorced but were married for 10+ years, you can claim ex-spouse’s benefits—even if they’ve remarried.

How to Maximize:

  • Claim ex-spouse’s benefit at 62 (50% of their FRA amount).
  • Once you reach FRA, switch to your own benefit (which may be higher).

Example:

  • Ex-Husband’s Benefit: $2,500 (FRA)
  • Your Benefit: $1,200 (FRA)
  • Claim ex-spouse’s benefit at 62: $1,250
  • At 66, switch to your $1,200 (no reduction)
  • If you wait until 70, your benefit grows to $1,560—still better than claiming at 62.

6. Consider the "Claim and Suspend" Alternative for Widows/Widowers

If your spouse passed away, you may be eligible for survivor benefits (up to 100% of their benefit).

Strategy:

  • If your own benefit is lower than your spouse’s, claim survivor benefits at 60 (71.5% of their benefit).
  • Once you reach FRA, switch to your own benefit (which may be higher).

Example:

  • Spouse’s Benefit (at death): $3,000
  • Your Benefit (at FRA): $1,800
  • Claim survivor at 60: $2,145 ($3,000 × 71.5%)
  • At 66, switch to your $1,800 (no reduction)
  • If you wait until 70, your benefit grows to $2,304—still less than survivor benefits.

7. Use a "Bridge Retirement" Strategy to Delay Social Security

If you retire early but can’t afford to live on savings alone, consider:

  • Part-time work to delay Social Security.
  • Renting out a home or monetizing skills (consulting, freelancing).
  • Delaying benefits by 2-3 years to increase payout.

Example:

  • A couple retires at 62 but can only afford $3,000/month from savings.
  • They delay Social Security until 66, increasing their $2,000/month benefit to $2,400/month.
  • Now, they can live on $5,400/month instead of $4,000.

8. Optimize for Tax Efficiency

Social Security benefits are taxable if your combined income exceeds $44,000 (single) or $55,000 (married).

How to Reduce Taxable Income: ✔ Claim benefits in lower-income years (e.g., after retirement). ✔ Use Roth IRA conversions to lower taxable income. ✔ Delay Medicare enrollment (if possible) to reduce premiums. ✔ Donate to charity (qualified charitable distributions reduce taxable income).

Example:

  • A couple with $50,000 in Social Security benefits and $30,000 in other income ($80,000 total).
  • Taxable portion: $4,800/month (85% of benefits).
  • By delaying benefits until 70, they reduce taxable income in early retirement.

Scenario:

  • Mary (66) has a $1,500/month benefit.
  • John (66) has a $3,000/month benefit.
  • Strategy:
    • Mary claims spousal benefits at 62 ($1,500, 50% of John’s FRA).
    • At 66, she switches to her own $1,500.
    • John delays until 70, increasing his benefit to $3,840/month.
  • Result:
    • Mary gets $1,500 (no reduction).
    • John gets $3,840 (instead of $3,000).
    • Total combined benefit: $5,340/month (vs. $4,500 if both claimed at 66).

Lesson: *Coordination between spouses can increase total benefits by 20%+.*
