Introduction: The Future of Social Security in 2026

Social Security was established in 1935 to provide financial security for retirees, survivors, and disabled individuals. Today, it remains the largest federal program in the U.S., serving over 64 million beneficiaries as of 2024 (Social Security Administration, 2024).

However, concerns about long-term solvency persist. The 2024 Trustees Report projected that the Social Security Disability Insurance (DI) Trust Fund will be depleted by 2034, while the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) Trust Fund will face a 23% funding shortfall by 2034 if no changes are made.

Despite these challenges, benefits will still be paid—just at a reduced rate. This means accurate planning is more important than ever.

Why Your 2026 Benefits Matter

  • Cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs) are expected to continue, but inflation’s impact varies year to year.
  • Claiming age (as early as 62 or as late as 70) drastically changes your monthly payout.
  • Work history and earnings determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA), which is the foundation of your benefits.
  • Marriage, divorce, and survivor benefits can significantly alter your payout.

If you’re approaching retirement, already retired, or just starting to plan, understanding your 2026 Social Security projections is essential.

How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before using our calculator, it’s crucial to understand how the SSA determines your benefits. Your monthly payout is based on:

1. Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA calculates your AIME by:

  • Adjusting your highest 35 years of earnings for inflation.
  • Indexing those earnings to current wage levels.
  • Taking the average of those indexed amounts.

2. The Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)

Your PIA is the benefit you’d receive if you retired at full retirement age (FRA), which is 66–67 depending on your birth year.

The formula for PIA (as of 2024, with adjustments expected in 2026) is: PIA = 90% of the first $1,174 + 32% of the next $6,922 (for 2024)

(Note: These numbers will change in 2026 due to COLA adjustments.)

3. Early vs. Delayed Retirement

  • Claiming at 62 (earliest possible age): Your benefit is reduced by ~30% compared to FRA.
  • Claiming at FRA (66–67): You receive 100% of your PIA.
  • Delaying until 70: Your benefit increases by 8% per year (up to 124% of PIA).

4. Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLAs)

Since 2026 is still a few years away, the exact COLA is unknown. However, the SSA uses the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners and Clerical Workers (CPI-W) to determine adjustments.

Historically, COLAs have ranged from 0% to 9.6% (2022 saw a 8.7% increase). If inflation remains high, your 2026 benefit could grow significantly.

Step-by-Step Guide to Getting Accurate Results

Step 1: Gather Your Earnings History

The SSA uses your highest 35 years of earnings. If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are counted—so maximizing earnings in your peak years is critical.

  • Example: If you worked 25 years but earned $100,000/year in your best years, the SSA will average those 25 years + 10 zeros, reducing your PIA.

Step 2: Input Your Expected Retirement Age

  • Claiming at 62? Your benefit will be ~70% of PIA.
  • Waiting until 70? You’ll get 124% of PIA (if you were born after 1943).

Step 3: Account for Spousal or Survivor Benefits

  • If married, spousal benefits can be 35–50% of your spouse’s PIA (if you claim at FRA).
  • Divorced spouses may still qualify if the marriage lasted 10+ years.
  • Survivor benefits (for widows/widowers) can be up to 100% of the deceased spouse’s PIA.

Step 4: Adjust for Future Earnings (If Still Working)

If you’re still working in 2026, the SSA applies the Retirement Earnings Test:

  • Under FRA: $1 in benefits lost for every $2 earned above $23,640 (2024 limit).
  • At FRA or older: No penalty.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your 2026 Social Security Benefits

1. Work Longer to Increase Your PIA

The SSA only considers your highest 35 years of earnings. If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are included, lowering your benefit.

Real-World Example: A 65-year-old with 30 years of work history earns $80,000/year in their best years. If they work 5 more years at $100,000, their PIA increases significantly because the SSA replaces the lower-earning years with higher wages.

Action Step:

  • Delay retirement if possible to add higher-earning years.
  • Consider part-time work in your 60s to boost your earnings record.

2. Claim Spousal Benefits Strategically

If you’re married, you can claim spousal benefits as early as 62, even if your spouse hasn’t retired yet.

Real-World Example: A couple where the husband has a higher PIA ($2,500/month) and the wife has a lower PIA ($1,200/month). If she claims spousal benefits at 66 (FRA), she gets 50% of his PIA ($1,250) + her own reduced benefit ($960) = $2,210/month—more than if she claimed her own benefit early.

Action Step:

  • Use our calculator to compare:
    • Claiming your own benefit early vs. spousal benefits.
    • Delaying your own claim to 100% at FRA while taking spousal benefits.

3. Delay Retirement Until Age 70 for Maximum Benefits

Every month you delay past FRA (66–67) up to 70 increases your benefit by 8%.

Real-World Example: A person with a PIA of $2,000 at FRA:

  • Claims at 62: $1,400/month (~30% reduction).
  • Claims at 70: $2,584/month (~29% increase).

Action Step:

  • If you can afford it, delay until 70—especially if you have other retirement income (401(k), pensions).
  • Use a "bridge job" in your 60s to cover expenses while delaying Social Security.

4. Use the "Restricted Application" Strategy (If Still Available)

(Note: This strategy is phasing out for those born after 1954, but may still apply to some.)

How it works:

  • You file for spousal benefits only (without claiming your own) at FRA.
  • Later, you can switch to your own benefit when it’s higher.

Real-World Example: A woman with a lower PIA ($1,500) marries a man with a higher PIA ($3,000). She can claim $1,500 spousal benefits at 66, then later switch to her own $1,500 benefit when it grows to $1,800 at 70.

Action Step:

  • Check your birth year to see if this strategy is still an option.
  • Consult a financial advisor if you’re unsure.

5. Avoid the Retirement Earnings Test (If Possible)

If you’re under FRA, the SSA penalizes earned income ($1 in benefits lost for every $2 over $23,640 in 2024).

Real-World Example: A 64-year-old earning $30,000/year (above the limit) would lose $3,360 in benefits for the year ($1 for every $2 over $23,640).

Action Step:

  • If you must work, consider:
    • Reducing hours to stay under the limit.
    • Delaying retirement until FRA to avoid penalties.

6. Consider Divorce or Marriage Timing for Benefits

  • Divorced spouses can claim benefits based on an ex-spouse’s record if the marriage lasted 10+ years.
  • Remarrying may affect survivor benefits—consult the SSA before remarrying after 60.

Real-World Example: A woman divorced at 50 after a 15-year marriage. Her ex-husband’s PIA is $3,500. She can claim 50% of his benefit ($1,750) at 66, even if she’s remarried.

Action Step:

  • Run scenarios with our calculator to see if divorce or remarriage timing affects your benefits.

7. Use Tax Strategies to Minimize Social Security Taxes

Up to 85% of your Social Security benefits may be taxable if your combined income exceeds $44,000 (single) or $55,000 (married filing jointly).

Real-World Example: A single retiree with $50,000 in other income and $2,000/month in Social Security ($24,000/year) has a combined income of $74,000. They owe taxes on $1,680/month ($20,160/year) of benefits (85% of $24,000).

Action Step:

  • Reduce taxable income by:
    • Roth conversions (to lower taxable income in retirement).
    • Charitable donations (to lower adjusted gross income).
    • Delaying claiming until you’re in a lower tax bracket.

8. Plan for Inflation and COLA Adjustments

Since 2026 is still a few years away, inflation could increase your benefits significantly if COLAs remain high.

Real-World Example:

  • 2022 COLA: +8.7%
  • 2023 COLA: +8.5%
  • If inflation stays high, your 2026 benefit could grow by 5–10%+.

Action Step:

  • Use our calculator’s COLA projection tool to estimate future benefits.
  • Adjust your retirement budget based on high vs. low COLA scenarios.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands in 2026

Mistake 1: Claiming Benefits Too Early Without Planning

Many retirees claim at 62 without realizing they lock in a permanently lower benefit.

Example: A person with a PIA of $2,000 at FRA claims at 62 and gets $1,400/month. If they waited until 70, they’d get $2,584/month—**$1,184 more per month* for life.*

Solution:

  • Use our calculator to compare early vs. delayed claiming.
  • Consult a financial advisor before making a decision.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Spousal Benefits

Many retirees don’t realize they can claim spousal benefits even if their own benefit is lower.

Example: A couple where the wife has a PIA of $1,200 and the husband has $3,000. If she claims spousal benefits at 66, she gets $1,500/month—more than her own $1,200.

Solution:

  • Run spousal benefit scenarios in our calculator.
  • Consider "claiming now, suspending later" (if still available).

Mistake 3: Working Past FRA Without Understanding the Rules

If you’re under FRA, the Retirement Earnings Test can reduce your benefits.

Example: A 64-year-old earning $35,000/year loses $5,920 in benefits for the year ($1 for every $2 over $23,640).

Solution:

  • Delay retirement until FRA if possible.
  • Reduce work hours if you must keep earning.

Mistake 4: Not Updating Your Earnings Record

The SSA may have incorrect earnings in your record, leading to lower benefits.

Example: A retiree discovers their earnings were recorded as $70,000 instead of $90,000 for a key year. Their PIA is $300/month lower than it should be.

Solution:

  • Request a free Social Security statement from SSA.gov.
  • Report errors to the SSA immediately.
