8 Key Factors That Affect Your Social Security Benefits in 2026

Understanding these factors will help you strategically plan for the best possible payout.

1. Your Full Retirement Age (FRA) – The Sweet Spot for Maximum Benefits

Your FRA (currently 66–67) is the age at which you receive 100% of your primary insurance amount (PIA). Claiming before FRA permanently reduces your benefit, while delaying increases it.

Example:

  • If your PIA is $2,000/month at FRA (67), claiming at 62 reduces it to $1,200/month (~30% less).
  • Delaying until 70 increases it to $2,560/month (~28% more).

Pro Tip: If you can afford to wait, delaying until 70 is often the best financial move for long-term security.

2. The Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) & Government Pension Offset (GPO) – Hidden Benefit Reductions

If you worked in government jobs with a pension, your Social Security benefits may be reduced due to:

  • WEP: Reduces benefits for those with no private-sector earnings history.
  • GPO: Reduces spousal/survivor benefits if your spouse receives a government pension.

Example: A teacher (covered by WEP) with 30 years of service might see their Social Security benefit cut by 50% if they didn’t work in the private sector.

Solution: Consult a Social Security attorney if you’re affected by WEP/GPO to explore alternative claiming strategies.

3. Inflation & COLA Adjustments – Will Your Benefits Keep Up?

The 2024 COLA was 8.7%, the highest in decades, but future adjustments depend on:

  • Inflation rate (measured by the CPI-W).
  • Wage growth (affects the bend points in benefit calculations).

2026 Projection:

  • If inflation stays moderate (~3%), expect a ~3–4% COLA.
  • If inflation spikes (5%+), benefits could increase 5–6%.

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Delay claiming if inflation is high (you’ll get larger monthly checks).
  • Consider a Roth IRA to reduce taxable Social Security income.

4. Spousal & Survivor Benefits – How to Double Your Income

If married, you can claim:

  • Spousal benefits (up to 50% of your spouse’s full benefit).
  • Survivor benefits (if your spouse passes away).

Example:

  • John (67) has a PIA of $2,500.
  • Jane (65) claims spousal benefits while waiting for her own benefit.
  • Jane receives $1,250/month (50% of John’s PIA) until she claims at 67.

Best Strategy:

  • File a restricted application (claim spousal benefits only) if you’re 5 years younger than your spouse.

5. Taxes on Social Security – How Much Could You Owe?

Social Security benefits are taxable if your combined income exceeds:

  • $32,000 (single)
  • $44,000 (married filing jointly)

Example:

  • Single retiree with $35,000 in other income + $2,000/month in benefits ($24,000/year).
  • Taxable income: $24,000 + $17,000 (half of benefits) = $41,000.
  • Taxable portion: $9,000 → ~$1,200 in federal taxes.

Solution:

  • Use a Roth conversion to reduce taxable income.
  • Claim benefits strategically to stay below tax thresholds.

6. Working After Retirement – The Earned Income Test

If you claim before FRA and work, the Earned Income Test applies:

  • $1 in benefits lost for every $2 earned (under $22,320 in 2024).
  • $1 in benefits lost for every $3 earned in the year you reach FRA.

Example:

  • Retiree claims at 62, earns $15,000 in 2026.
  • Loses $5,000 in benefits ($1 for every $2 earned).
  • Recovers lost benefits in full once FRA is reached.

Best Strategy:

  • Delay claiming if you plan to work in retirement.
  • Use a "pause-and-go" strategy (stop working temporarily to avoid penalties).

7. Divorce & Social Security – Can You Claim Ex-Spouse’s Benefits?

If you were married for 10+ years, you may qualify for ex-spouse benefits—even if divorced.

Example:

  • Sarah (65) divorced her husband (70) 15 years ago.
  • Ex-husband’s PIA is $3,000.
  • Sarah can claim 50% of his benefit ($1,500/month) if she hasn’t remarried.

Key Rule:

  • You don’t need to be unmarried to claim ex-spouse benefits.
  • The ex-spouse must be eligible for benefits (even if they haven’t claimed yet).

8. State Taxes on Social Security – Which States Tax Benefits?

Some states tax Social Security, reducing your net income. The worst offenders:

  • California (taxes benefits if income > $86,782).
  • New Jersey (taxes benefits if income > $75,000).
  • Minnesota (taxes benefits if income > $75,000).

Best States for Tax-Free Benefits:

  • Alaska, Florida, Nevada, Texas, Washington (no state income tax).

Solution:

  • Plan your retirement in a low-tax state if possible.
  • Use a trust or annuity to defer taxes on Social Security income.

Real-World Examples: How Different Claiming Strategies Affect Your 2026 Payout

Example 1: The Early Retiree (Claiming at 62)

Scenario:

  • John (born 1960, FRA = 67) has a PIA of $2,200/month.

Lesson: Claiming early costs thousands—even if you need the money now, delaying just a few years can dramatically increase your lifetime benefits.

Example 2: The Delayed Retiree (Claiming at 70)

Scenario:

  • Sarah (born 1958, FRA = 66) has a PIA of $2,000/month.
  • Delays until 70 (4 years past FRA).
  • Monthly benefit: $2,720 (32% increase).
  • Total over 20 years: $545,600 (vs. $480,000 if claimed at FRA).

Lesson: Delaying until 70 can boost your benefits by 25–30%, making it one of the best retirement strategies for high earners.

Example 3: The Spousal Benefit Strategy

Scenario:

  • Mike (67) has a PIA of $3,000.
  • Jane (65) claims spousal benefits while waiting for her own.
  • Jane receives $1,500/month (50% of Mike’s PIA).
  • Total combined income: $4,500/month (vs. $3,000 if Jane claimed her own benefit).

Lesson: Spousal benefits can double your income—especially if one spouse has a much higher PIA.

Example 4: The Government Worker (WEP Impact)

Scenario:

  • Teacher (born 1965, FRA = 67) has 30 years of service.
  • Private-sector earnings: $0 (only government pension).
  • Social Security benefit reduced by 50% due to WEP.
  • Final benefit: $800/month (instead of $1,600).

Lesson: If you’re a government employee, consult a Social Security attorney to explore alternative claiming strategies.

Example 5: The Part-Time Worker (Earned Income Test)

Scenario:

  • Retiree claims at 62, works part-time ($12,000/year).
  • Loses $6,000 in benefits ($1 for every $2 earned).
  • Recovers lost benefits in full at FRA (67).

Lesson: If you plan to work in retirement, delay claiming to avoid unnecessary penalties.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands in Social Security Benefits

Many retirees unintentionally leave money on the table due to these critical errors:

Mistake 1: Claiming Social Security Too Early (At 62)

  • Problem: Claiming at 62 reduces benefits by 25–30% permanently.
  • Solution: Delay until at least FRA (66–67), or 70 for maximum benefits.

Mistake 2: Not Considering Spousal Benefits

  • Problem: Many retirees don’t file for spousal benefits, leaving free money on the table.
  • Solution: If your spouse has a higher PIA, claim spousal benefits first while waiting for your own.

Mistake 3: Ignoring the Earned Income Test

  • Problem: Working while claiming before FRA can slash benefits.
  • Solution: Delay claiming if you plan to work, or pause work temporarily to avoid penalties.

Mistake 4: Not Factoring in Taxes

  • Problem: Many retirees don’t account for taxes on Social Security, leading to unexpected tax bills.
  • Solution: Use a Roth IRA to reduce taxable income, or claim benefits strategically to stay below tax thresholds.
