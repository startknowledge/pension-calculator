How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated in 2026

Before you can estimate your 2026 payout, you need to understand how Social Security benefits are determined. The SSA uses a three-step formula to calculate your monthly benefit:

1. Calculate Your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)

The SSA takes your highest 35 years of earnings (indexed for inflation) and averages them. If you worked fewer than 35 years, the SSA plugs in zeros for the missing years, which dramatically lowers your benefit.

Example:

  • If you earned $80,000/year for 35 years, your AIME is $80,000 × 12 months ÷ 35 years ≈ $2,714/month.
  • If you only worked 20 years, the remaining 15 years are $0, reducing your AIME to $40,800 ÷ 35 ≈ $1,166/month.

2. Apply the Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) Formula

The SSA uses a progressive formula to determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA), which is your full retirement age (FRA) benefit (currently 66–67, depending on birth year).

The formula is:

  • 90% of the first $1,174 (in 2024; adjusted for 2026)
  • + 32% of the next $6,996 (in 2024; adjusted for 2026)
  • + 15% of any amount over $8,170 (in 2024; adjusted for 2026)

Example (2024 PIA Calculation):

  • If your AIME is $3,000/month:
    • 90% of $1,174 = $1,056.60
    • 32% of ($3,000 - $1,174) = $6,328 × 0.32 = $2,025.00
    • Total PIA = $1,056.60 + $2,025.00 = $3,081.60/month

(Note: These numbers will change in 2026 due to COLA adjustments and wage indexation.)

3. Adjust for Early or Delayed Retirement

  • Claiming before FRA (e.g., at 62): Your benefit is permanently reduced by ~30% (varies by birth year).
  • Claiming after FRA (up to age 70): You earn delayed retirement credits (up to 8% per year).

Example:

  • If your FRA benefit is $2,500/month, claiming at 62 reduces it to ~$1,750/month.
  • Waiting until 70 increases it to ~$3,100/month.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security in 2026

Now that you know how to estimate your benefits, here are 8 proven strategies to increase your Social Security payout in 2026.

1. Work Longer to Boost Your Earnings

The SSA uses your highest 35 years of earnings. If you have years with low or no income, working longer can replace those zeros with higher earnings.

Example: John, 65, has 30 years of work history but 5 years with $0 earnings (due to caregiving).

  • His current AIME is $2,500/month.
  • If he works 2 more years at $70,000/year, his AIME could rise to $2,800/month, increasing his benefit by ~$300/month.

Action Step:

  • If you’re under 62, consider delaying retirement to add high-earning years.
  • If you’re already retired, check if part-time work could improve your benefit.

2. Delay Claiming Until Age 70 for Maximum Benefits

The biggest mistake most people make is claiming too early. For every year you delay past FRA (up to 70), you get an 8% bonus.

Example: Mike, born in 1960 (FRA = 67), has a PIA of $2,500.

  • Claiming at 67: $2,500/month
  • Claiming at 70: $2,500 × 1.08 × 1.08 × 1.08 = $3,100/month (~$600/month more)

Action Step:

  • If you can afford to wait, delaying until 70 is often the best financial move.

3. Use the "Restricted Application" Strategy (If Still Available)

Before April 2017, married couples could use the "file-and-suspend" strategy, where one spouse files for benefits but suspends them, allowing the other to claim a spousal benefit while the first spouse kept growing credits.

While this strategy is no longer available, some alternative tactics still work:

  • Claim spousal benefits at FRA while your spouse delays until 70.
  • Use the "claim now, claim more later" rule (if eligible).

Example: Sarah (67) and her husband (70) both have PIAs of $2,500.

  • If Sarah claims spousal benefits at 67, she gets $1,250/month (half of his PIA).
  • If her husband waits until 70, his benefit grows to $3,100, and she can switch to her own benefit later.

Action Step:

  • If you’re married, coordinate claiming strategies to maximize combined benefits.

4. Claim Spousal or Survivor Benefits Strategically

If your spouse has a higher benefit, claiming spousal benefits (up to 50% of their PIA) can boost your income.

Example: Lisa’s husband has a PIA of $3,500, but she only worked 10 years (AIME = $1,200).

  • If she claims spousal benefits at 67, she gets $1,750/month (50% of his PIA).
  • If she waits until 70, her own benefit grows to $1,500, but she loses the spousal bonus.

Best Approach:

  • Claim spousal benefits at FRA if your spouse is delaying until 70.
  • Switch to your own benefit later if it becomes higher.

5. Avoid Early Retirement If Possible

Claiming at 62 reduces your benefit by ~30% permanently. If you can wait even a few years, you’ll get thousands more over your lifetime.

Example: David, born in 1965 (FRA = 66), has a PIA of $2,200.

  • Claiming at 62: $1,540/month
  • Claiming at 66: $2,200/month
  • Claiming at 70: $2,736/month

Action Step:

  • If you must retire early, consider working part-time to delay Social Security and keep earning income.
