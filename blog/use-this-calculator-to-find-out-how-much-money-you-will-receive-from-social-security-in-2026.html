Social Security benefits are not fixed—they change based on:

  • Government policy changes (potential reforms could impact future benefits)

A 2022 study by the Social Security Trustees estimated that benefits could be reduced by 20% by 2034 if no changes are made. While that’s not immediate, planning for 2026 ensures you’re prepared for whatever adjustments come.

How to Calculate Your Social Security Benefits for 2026

The Social Security Administration (SSA) uses a complex formula to determine benefits, but we’ve simplified it for you. Here’s how it works:

1. The Social Security Benefit Formula (Simplified)

Your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)—the base benefit you’d receive at full retirement age (FRA)—is calculated using your 35 highest-earning years of work history.

The formula is: PIA = [ (Average Indexed Monthly Earnings × 96) + (Average Indexed Monthly Earnings × 32) ] ÷ 128

2. How Your Earnings Affect Your Benefits

The SSA indexes your earnings to account for wage growth. For example:

  • If you earned $80,000 in 2023, the SSA adjusts it to 2026 dollars based on inflation.
  • Years with no earnings (e.g., staying home to raise kids) count as $0—but you can replace them with higher-earning years if possible.

Real-World Example: Imagine you worked for 30 years, with your highest-earning years being:

  • $50,000 (2010)
  • $60,000 (2015)
  • $80,000 (2020)
  • $90,000 (2023)

The SSA averages these (after indexing) and applies the formula to determine your PIA.

3. Claiming Early vs. Delaying Benefits

Your benefit amount changes based on when you claim:

Claiming Age Monthly Benefit Adjustment
62 (Earliest) 70–75% of PIA (reduced)
66 (Full Retirement Age) 100% of PIA (no reduction)
70 (Latest) Up to 132% of PIA (delayed retirement credit)

Example: If your PIA at FRA (67) is $2,500/month, claiming at 62 would give you $1,875/month, while waiting until 70 could increase it to $3,250/month.

Why Delay?

  • Higher lifetime payout (especially if you expect a long retirement).
  • Spousal benefits (if your spouse is younger, delaying can maximize their survivor benefit).
  • Cost-of-living adjustments (COLA) compound over time.

Why Claim Early?

  • Financial need (if you can’t wait).
  • Health concerns (if you have a shorter life expectancy).
  • Lower tax burden (early benefits may be taxed less if income is low).

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security Benefits in 2026

Now that you understand the basics, here’s how to optimize your Social Security strategy for 2026 and beyond.

1. Work Longer to Increase Your PIA

The SSA only considers your 35 highest-earning years. If you have years with low or no earnings, working longer can boost your average.

Example: If you worked 30 years with an average of $40,000/year but have 5 years with $0 (e.g., raising kids), adding 5 more high-earning years could increase your PIA significantly.

Action Step:

  • Delay retirement if possible to add more high-earning years.
  • Maximize earnings in your peak years (if you’re self-employed, consider increasing income).

2. Delay Benefits Until Age 70 for Maximum Payout

The biggest lever you have is when you claim. For every year you delay beyond FRA (67), your benefit increases by 8%.

Example: If your PIA is $2,500 at 67, waiting until 70 could give you $3,250/month—a $750/month increase.

Action Step:

  • Run the numbers with our calculator to see if delaying is worth it.
  • Consider spousal benefits—if one of you dies, the survivor gets the higher benefit.

3. Coordinate Benefits with Your Spouse

Social Security offers spousal and survivor benefits, which can double your income if planned correctly.

Key Rules:

  • Spousal benefit = 50% of your spouse’s PIA (if you claim at FRA).
  • Survivor benefit = 100% of the higher-earning spouse’s benefit (if you outlive them).
  • File and Suspend (for those born before 1954) allowed one spouse to claim while the other delayed—but this was phased out in 2016.

Example: If you and your spouse both have a PIA of $2,500, claiming spousal benefits at 66 could give you $1,250 each, totaling $2,500/month—but if you wait until 70, your benefits could grow to $3,250 each, totaling $6,500/month.

Action Step:

  • Use our calculator to compare single vs. spousal benefits.
  • Consult a financial advisor to optimize filing strategies.

4. Avoid Earning Too Much While Claiming Early

If you’re under FRA (67) and still working, the SSA reduces your benefits based on earnings:

  • $1 in benefits for every $2 earned above $21,240 (2024 limit).
  • $1 in benefits for every $3 earned above $56,520 in the year you reach FRA.

Example: If you claim at 62 with a PIA of $2,500 but earn $30,000 in 2026, you could lose $4,360 in benefits that year.

Action Step:

  • Check the earnings limit for your year of claiming.
  • Consider reducing work hours if you’re close to the limit.

5. Use the "File and Suspend" Strategy (If Eligible)

For those born before 1954, the "File and Suspend" tactic allowed one spouse to claim benefits while the other delayed—but this was discontinued in 2016.

Alternative Strategy:

  • Claim spousal benefits early while your spouse delays.
  • Switch to your own benefit later when it grows.

Action Step:

  • Check your birth year to see if you qualify for any remaining strategies.
  • Consult the SSA or a benefits expert for personalized advice.

6. Factor in Taxes on Your Social Security Benefits

Up to 85% of your Social Security benefits may be taxable, depending on your income and filing status.

Tax Rules:

  • Single filers: Taxable if provisional income > $25,000.
  • Married filing jointly: Taxable if provisional income > $32,000.
  • Provisional income = Adjusted Gross Income + Tax-exempt interest + 50% of Social Security benefits.

Example: If you have $50,000 in other income and $2,500 in Social Security, your provisional income is $62,500, making 85% of your benefits taxable ($2,125).

Action Step:

  • Use a tax calculator to estimate how much you’ll owe.
  • Consider Roth IRA conversions to reduce taxable income.

7. Plan for Healthcare Costs in Retirement

Social Security does not cover Medicare premiums, which can eat into your benefits.

2024 Medicare Part B Premiums:

  • $174.70/month (standard premium).
  • $50–$500+/month for Part D (prescription drugs) and Medigap (supplemental insurance).

Action Step:

  • Budget for healthcare costs when estimating your Social Security payout.
  • Consider long-term care insurance to protect your benefits.

8. Stay Informed About Social Security Reforms

Congress has proposed changes to Social Security, such as:

  • Raising the retirement age (from 67 to 69 or 70).
  • Means-testing benefits (reducing payouts for high earners).
  • Increasing payroll taxes (to extend the trust fund).

Action Step:

  • Follow updates from the SSA and Congress.
  • Adjust your plan if major reforms are announced.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Claiming Social Security

Many retirees unintentionally reduce their benefits by making these mistakes:

1. Claiming Too Early Without Considering Spousal Benefits

Mistake: Claiming at 62 without checking spousal options. Fix: Use our calculator to compare single vs. spousal benefits before deciding.

2. Working While Claiming Early (Without Checking Earnings Limits)

Mistake: Continuing to work above the earnings limit while claiming early. Fix: Reduce work hours or delay claiming if you’re close to the limit.

3. Not Updating Your SSA Records

Mistake: Forgetting to report self-employment income or correct past earnings. Fix: Verify your earnings history on My Social Security and report any errors.

4. Assuming Benefits Are Fixed

Mistake: Not accounting for COLA increases or future policy changes. Fix: Re-run your estimate every few years to adjust for inflation.

5. Ignoring Divorce or Widow/Widower Benefits

Mistake: Missing out on ex-spouse or survivor benefits. Fix: Check eligibility for divorced spousal benefits (must be married 10+ years).

6. Not Considering State Taxes on Benefits

Mistake: Forgetting that some states tax Social Security (e.g., Minnesota, Vermont). Fix: Check your state’s tax laws before claiming.

7. Claiming Without a Financial Plan

Mistake: Relying only on Social Security without savings. Fix: Use our calculator to see if you need additional income sources (pensions, part-time work, investments).
