Part 1: How Congressional Pensions Work (And Why MTG’s Resignation Was a Financial Win)

The Three Types of Federal Pensions

Before we dissect MTG’s move, it’s essential to understand the three main pension systems federal employees (including Congress) are part of:

  1. Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS)

    • For most federal employees hired after 1986.
    • Three components:
      • Basic Benefit (defined benefit): Based on years of service and final average salary.
      • Social Security: Employees pay 7.65%, government matches.
      • Thrift Savings Plan (TSP): A 401(k)-style retirement account with government matching (up to 5% of salary).
    • Vesting: After 5 years of service, employees earn full pension benefits.

  2. Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS)

    • For employees hired before 1987.
    • No Social Security contribution (government covers 100% of retirement).
    • Higher pension benefits but no TSP match.

  3. Congressional Pension System (Separate from FERS/CSRS)

    • Members of Congress contribute 0.8% of their salary (vs. 7.65% for FERS).
    • No Social Security taxes (a major tax advantage).
    • Pension calculated differently:
      • 2% per year of service (max 20 years).
      • Final average salary (FAS) over the last 3 years (not just the last year).
    • No vesting requirement—pensions accrue immediately.

Why MTG’s Resignation Timing Was Critical

MTG’s January 2 resignation (just three days before the new term began) wasn’t arbitrary. Here’s why:

  • Congressional Terms Start on January 3

    • If MTG had resigned on December 31, she would have lost her seat effective January 3, meaning she wouldn’t have been paid for the final days of the term.
    • Resigning on January 2 meant she received her final salary payment (including the December salary) before leaving, maximizing her pension calculation.

  • Final Average Salary (FAS) Matters

    • Her pension is based on the average of her highest 3 years of salary.
    • By leaving just before the new term, she locked in her highest-earning years (as a sitting congresswoman) rather than risking a drop in pay if she had stayed longer.

  • Tax Implications of Early Retirement

    • If she had waited until after the term ended, she might have faced higher tax brackets or penalties for early withdrawal from her TSP.
    • Resigning early allowed her to phase out of Congress smoothly, avoiding unexpected tax liabilities.

Part 2: 8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Federal Pension (Like MTG Did)

Whether you’re a lawmaker, military officer, or federal civil servant, these proven strategies can help you secure the maximum pension benefits while minimizing tax burdens.

Strategy 1: Understand Your Vesting Timeline (And Act Before It’s Too Late)

Problem: Many federal employees don’t realize they’re vested until it’s almost too late. Solution:

  • FERS employees become fully vested after 5 years.
  • CSRS employees are always vested (but must work at least 3 years to receive a pension).
  • Congress members earn pension benefits immediately (no vesting requirement).

Action Step: ✅ Track your years of service using your OPM My Account or Congressional records. ✅ If you’re within 1-2 years of vesting, start planning your retirement window to avoid last-minute mistakes.

Real-World Example: A retired Navy SEAL who worked 28 years realized too late that he had missed the 5-year vesting mark for his FERS pension—costing him $20,000 in annual benefits. By monitoring his service clock, he could have adjusted his retirement date to qualify.

Strategy 2: Time Your Resignation for Maximum Final Average Salary (FAS)

Problem: Many federal workers assume their last year’s salary is used—but it’s actually the average of the highest 3 years. Solution:

  • If your salary fluctuates, plan your resignation during your highest-earning years.
  • For Congress, this means leaving just before a term ends (like MTG did).

Action Step: ✅ Review your salary history (via OPM records or pay stubs). ✅ If you’re in a variable-pay role (e.g., military promotions, congressional pay raises), time your exit to include your peak earnings.

Real-World Example: *A Department of Defense employee who waited until his salary dropped after a promotion lost $12,000 annually in his pension. By resigning at the peak of his pay grade, he increased his FAS by 15%.*

Strategy 3: Leverage the "Rule of 55" for Early Retirement Without Penalties

Problem: Most federal employees can’t retire before 62 without penalties. Solution:

  • The "Rule of 55" allows FERS employees to retire early (as early as age 55 with 25 years of service) without the 10% early withdrawal penalty on their TSP.
  • Congress members can retire at any age with full pension benefits.

Action Step: ✅ Check if you qualify for the Rule of 55 (age + years of service ≥ 55 + 25). ✅ If not, consider a "phased retirement" (working part-time while collecting partial benefits).

Real-World Example: A State Department official who retired at 57 with 28 years of service used the Rule of 55 to avoid TSP penalties, saving $8,000 in taxes in his first year of retirement.

Strategy 4: Minimize Taxes by Structuring Your Withdrawals Smartly

Problem: Federal pensions are taxed as ordinary income, and early withdrawals can trigger higher brackets. Solution:

  • Spread out withdrawals to stay in a lower tax bracket.
  • Use Roth conversions (if eligible) to reduce taxable income.
  • For Congress, avoid Social Security taxes by retiring before 70 (since benefits grow until then).

Strategy 5: Consider a "Phased Retirement" to Avoid Sudden Income Gaps

Problem: Many federal workers can’t afford to stop working entirely but don’t want to lose pension benefits. Solution:

  • FERS allows "phased retirement" (working part-time while collecting 50-100% of your pension).
  • Congress has no formal phased retirement, but leaving early (like MTG) allows for a smooth transition.

Action Step: ✅ If you’re in a role that allows it, negotiate a phased retirement. ✅ If not, plan a gradual exit (e.g., reducing hours before full retirement).

Problem: Many federal employees don’t contribute enough to get the full government match. Solution:

  • FERS employees get a 5% match (up to 5% of salary).
  • Congress gets no TSP match, but maximizing contributions still reduces taxable income.

Action Step: ✅ Contribute at least 5% of your salary to get the full match. ✅ For Congress, maximize Roth contributions (if in a lower tax bracket).
