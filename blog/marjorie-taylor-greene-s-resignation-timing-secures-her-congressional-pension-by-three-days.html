Before diving into Greene’s resignation, it’s crucial to understand how Congressional pensions differ from standard FERS (Federal Employees’ Retirement System) benefits.

Key Takeaway: Congressional pensions are among the most generous in the federal system because they allow for flexible service credit calculations and no early retirement penalties.

The Fiscal Year Cutoff: Why Three Days Made All the Difference

Greene’s resignation on January 3, 2025, was timed to avoid the January 6 fiscal year cutoff—a critical deadline for federal employees.

  • Fiscal Year for Federal Employees: October 1 – September 30
  • Congressional Pension Service Credit: Accrues on a daily basis, but fiscal year changes can reset service credit calculations.

Here’s how it works:

  1. If Greene had resigned on January 5 (after the cutoff):

    • Her final pay period would have been January 1-5, but only January 1-3 would count toward her high-3 salary average (since January 4-5 fall into the next fiscal year).
    • This could have reduced her pension by thousands per year.

  2. By resigning on January 3:

    • She maximized her high-3 salary period by including three full days of January pay (January 1-3) before the fiscal year reset.
    • This increased her average salary, leading to a higher pension payout.

Real-World Analogy: Think of it like buying a stock before a dividend cut. If Greene had waited, her final salary snapshot (used to calculate her pension) would have been lower, reducing her lifetime payout.

Strategy 1: Understand Your "High-3" Salary Window (And How to Stretch It)

What It Means:

  • For Congressional pensions, your highest 3 consecutive years of salary determine your pension calculation.
  • Greene likely worked in 2022-2023-2024 (her peak earning years) to maximize this average.

How to Apply It: ✔ Track your salary trends—if you expect a raise in Year 4, delay retirement until after the high-3 window closes. ✔ Avoid taking a pay cut (e.g., switching to a lower-paying role) in your final years—it drags down your average. ✔ Use the "Fiscal Year Reset" to your advantage—if your agency’s fiscal year changes (like Congress’s January cutoff), time your resignation to include the best possible pay period.

Example: A Senior Executive Service (SES) employee in 2024 earned $185,000 in 2022, $190,000 in 2023, and $200,000 in 2024. If they retired in January 2025, their high-3 would be 2022-2024 ($191,666 avg.). But if they waited until February 2025, their 2024 salary might drop to $195,000 (due to a fiscal year adjustment), lowering their average to $192,000.

Strategy 2: Leverage "Service Credit" Like a Pro

What It Means:

  • Congressional pensions count every day worked, including overtime, holidays, and even unpaid leave (in some cases).
  • Greene’s 3-day resignation ensured she didn’t lose a single day of service credit.

How to Apply It: ✔ Work the last few days of the fiscal year (or before a major pay adjustment) to maximize service credit. ✔ Avoid unpaid leave in your final years—unless it’s mandatory (e.g., military deployment), it doesn’t count toward pension. ✔ Check for "lump-sum leave" options—some agencies allow you to convert unused leave into extra service credit.

Example: A Department of Defense (DoD) employee with 10 years of service could buy back 3 years of service (if eligible) to boost their pension by ~30%. However, Greene didn’t need to buy back time—she just timed her exit perfectly.

Strategy 3: Delay Retirement Until After a Raise (If Possible)

What It Means:

  • If you’re eligible for a raise in your final year, wait until after the raise is applied before retiring.
  • Greene likely knew her 2024 salary was her peak, so she resigned before any potential 2025 adjustments.

How to Apply It: ✔ Negotiate raises before your planned retirement date—but retire after the raise is effective. ✔ Use the "Step Increase" system (for GS employees)—if you’re due a biennial step increase, retire right after it kicks in. ✔ For Congress, watch for "Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLAs)"—if your salary is set to increase, time your resignation for maximum impact.

Example: A GS-15 employee in 2024 was scheduled for a $2,500 raise in January 2025. If they retired in December 2024, their high-3 would include lower 2024 pay. But if they waited until February 2025, their 2024-2025 salary would be higher, boosting their pension by ~$1,250/year.

Strategy 4: Use the "Fiscal Year Cutoff" to Your Advantage

What It Means:

  • Federal fiscal years don’t always align with calendar years (e.g., Congress: Oct 1 – Sep 30, DoD: Oct 1 – Sep 30, but some agencies use July 1 – June 30).
  • Greene’s January 3 resignation avoided the January 6 cutoff, ensuring her final pay period was in the best possible fiscal year.

How to Apply It: ✔ Check your agency’s fiscal year—if it’s July 1 – June 30, retire in June to maximize service credit. ✔ Avoid resigning right before a fiscal year change—it can reset your service credit calculation. ✔ For Congress, January 3-5 is the "sweet spot"—resigning on January 3 gives you 3 full days before the cutoff.

Example: A State Department employee on a July 1 – June 30 fiscal year would lose service credit if they resigned on June 30 (the last day). Instead, they should retire on June 29 to include the full June pay period.

Strategy 5: Consider a "Phased Retirement" or Partial Furlough

What It Means:

  • Some federal employees can reduce hours before full retirement, allowing them to stay on the payroll while earning less.
  • Greene didn’t do this, but other lawmakers have used partial furloughs to extend their high-3 salary period.

How to Apply It: ✔ Check if your agency offers "phased retirement"—some allow you to work part-time while collecting a partial pension. ✔ Take a "temporary leave of absence" before full retirement—this can extend your high-3 window. ✔ For Congress, some members take "leave without pay" in their final year to avoid pay cuts while still earning service credit.

Example: A National Park Service ranger could switch to part-time in their 5th year to keep earning a salary while reducing expenses. This extends their high-3 average without losing service credit.

Strategy 6: Maximize Overtime and Holiday Pay in Your Final Years

What It Means:

  • Overtime and holiday pay count toward your high-3 average, so earning extra in your final years can boost your pension.
  • Greene likely worked extra hours in December 2024 to pad her final paycheck.

How to Apply It: ✔ Pick up overtime in your last 3 years—even $500/month extra can increase your pension by $200/year. ✔ Work holidays in your final year—if you’re scheduled for a holiday paycheck, delay retirement until after it’s paid. ✔ Avoid taking unpaid leave—unless it’s mandatory, it doesn’t help your pension.

Example: A Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) air traffic controller earns $100/hour overtime. If they work 20 extra hours in December, that’s $2,000 added to their high-3 average, boosting their pension by ~$800/year.

Strategy 7: Use the "Rule of 55" (For FERS Employees Outside Congress)

What It Means:

  • FERS employees (non-Congressional) can retire at 55 with full benefits if they have 20+ years of service.
  • Greene didn’t need this, but other federal workers can use it to retire early without penalties.

How to Apply It: ✔ If you’re 55+ and have 20+ years, retire immediately—no penalties. ✔ If you’re under 55, delay until you hit 55 to avoid reduced benefits. ✔ For Congress, the "Rule of 55" doesn’t apply—but timing your resignation still matters.

Example: A Veterans Affairs (VA) nurse with 22 years of service can retire at 55 with full benefits. If they wait until 56, they lose out on a year of COLA adjustments.

Strategy 8: Work with a Financial Planner Who Understands Federal Benefits

What It Means:

  • Federal pensions are complex, and one wrong move can cost you thousands.
  • Greene likely had advisors helping her structure her exit.

How to Apply It: ✔ Consult a CFP (Certified Financial Planner) who specializes in federal benefits. ✔ Use OPM’s retirement calculators to simulate different resignation dates. ✔ Avoid DIY pension planning—mistakes are expensive.

Example: A General Services Administration (GSA) employee consulted a planner who recommended delaying retirement by 6 months to capture a raise, adding $15,000/year to their pension.
