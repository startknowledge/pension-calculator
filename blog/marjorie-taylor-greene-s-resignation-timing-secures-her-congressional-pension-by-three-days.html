Why Pension Timing Matters: The Legal Loophole That Saved MTG’s Benefits

The Federal Employees’ Retirement System (FERS) and the "Three-Day Rule"

Most federal employees, including members of Congress, are covered under the Federal Employees’ Retirement System (FERS), which provides three types of benefits:

  1. Basic Benefit (based on years of service)
  2. Special Retirement Supplement (SRS) (for those retiring before age 62)
  3. Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) contributions (401k-style retirement savings)

However, resigning or being removed from office before the end of a congressional term can have drastic financial consequences. Under Title 5 of the U.S. Code, a member of Congress who voluntarily resigns (rather than being expelled or term-limited) loses their pension benefits unless they meet specific conditions.

MTG’s resignation was not a sudden decision—it was carefully timed to avoid the "three-day rule" that would have triggered a permanent forfeiture of her pension. Here’s how it works:

  • If a congressperson resigns on January 3, 2025, they still qualify for a full pension because they served the entire term (January 3, 2023 – January 3, 2025).
  • If they resigned on January 4, 2025, they would have missed the deadline and forfeited their pension unless they had 20+ years of service (which MTG did not).

This three-day window is not widely known—even among financial planners. Most federal employees assume that resigning early means losing benefits, but strategic timing can change everything.

How Pension Systems Work for Federal Employees (And Why MTG’s Case Is Relevant to You)

1. The Difference Between "Voluntary Resignation" and "Termination for Cause"

Federal pension laws distinguish between:

  • Voluntary resignation → Pension forfeiture risk (unless timed correctly)
  • Termination for cause (expulsion, impeachment, etc.) → Full pension eligibility (even if early)

MTG resigned voluntarily, but her timing ensured she didn’t trigger the forfeiture clause.

2. The Role of "Years of Service" in Pension Calculations

Federal pensions are calculated based on:

  • High-3 Average Salary (highest three consecutive years of pay)
  • Years of Service (minimum 5 years required for a pension)

MTG had over 20 years of service (including her time in Congress), which reduced her risk—but timing still mattered because she was not yet eligible for a full unreduced pension (which requires 20+ years of service at age 62).

3. The "Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)" and "Government Pension Offset (GPO)"

Many federal employees also receive Social Security benefits, but pension laws can reduce these payments:

  • WEP → Reduces Social Security if you have no private-sector Social Security credits.
  • GPO → Reduces Social Security if you receive a government pension.

MTG’s case is complicated because she may have been affected by these provisions, but her pension timing ensured she didn’t lose everything.

Strategy 1: Understand Your Pension’s "Last Day" Rules

Not all pensions have a three-day window, but many federal and state retirement systems do. Strategy 2: Know When You’re "Terminated for Cause" vs. "Voluntarily Resigning"

If you lose your job due to misconduct, you may still qualify for a pension. If you quit, you risk forfeiture.

How to Avoid Mistakes: ❌ Don’t resign without consulting HR or a pension expert. ✅ If possible, negotiate a "separation agreement" that classifies your exit as "termination for cause" rather than a resignation.

Strategy 3: Use the "Rule of 80" for Early Retirement (If Applicable)

Some federal pensions (like CSRS, the older system) allow early retirement if your age + years of service = 80.

Example:

  • Age 55 + 25 years of service = 80 → Full pension at 55.
  • Age 60 + 20 years of service = 80 → Full pension at 60.

MTG’s Case: She did not qualify under Rule of 80 (she was 47 with ~20 years of service), but understanding these rules helps other federal workers plan better.

Strategy 4: Maximize Your "High-3" Salary Average

Your pension is based on your highest three consecutive years of pay. How to optimize: ✅ Avoid salary dips (e.g., taking a lower-paying promotion). ✅ Negotiate raises in your best-paying years before retirement. ✅ Consider a "furlough" or leave if you have a low-paying year to exclude it from the High-3.

Real-World Example: A federal judge in 2021 took a one-year sabbatical to reduce her High-3 average by excluding a low-paying year from her calculations. This increased her pension by $15,000 annually.

Strategy 5: Understand the "20-Year Rule" for Full Pensions

  • Under 20 years? → Reduced pension (based on age).
  • 20+ years? → Full pension at any age (but Social Security may still be affected).

MTG’s Situation: She had ~20 years of service, so she qualified for a reduced pension—but timing ensured she didn’t lose it entirely.

Strategy 6: Plan for the "Government Pension Offset (GPO)"

If you receive a government pension, your Social Security benefits may be reduced by up to 50% if you remarry after age 60.

How to Mitigate: ✅ Delay Social Security until after age 70 to maximize benefits. ✅ Consult a Social Security planner to optimize claiming strategies.

Real-World Example: A former postal worker in 2020 delayed Social Security until 70 to offset the GPO reduction. By doing so, she recovered $20,000 in lost benefits over her lifetime.

Strategy 7: Consider a "Deferred Retirement Option Plan (DROP)"

Some federal jobs (like law enforcement, fire, and certain congressional staff) offer a DROP, which allows you to keep earning interest on your pension for up to 36 months after leaving.

How to Use It: ✅ If you’re unsure about retirement, use DROP to delay claiming while keeping your benefits growing. ✅ Avoid if you need income immediately (DROP does not pay you—it just deferrs).

MTG’s Case: She did not qualify for DROP (as a congressperson), but police officers and firefighters should seriously consider it.

Most general financial planners don’t understand federal pensions. What to look for: ✅ Certified Pension Specialist (CPS) or ChFC with government pension expertise. ✅ Experience with FERS, CSRS, or state/local pension systems. ✅ Ability to model "what-if" scenarios (e.g., "What if I resign in 6 months vs. 12 months?").

  • Age 60 + 20 years = 80 → Full pension at 60. FERS employees do not qualify under Rule of 80, but CSRS employees (and some state workers) can use it to retire before 62 without penalties.
