Part 2: The Rules of the Game – How Congressional Pensions Work (And Why Timing Matters)

1. The Vesting Rule: How Long Do You Need to Work to Get a Pension?

Unlike private-sector 401(k)s, congressional pensions are defined-benefit plans, meaning your benefit is calculated based on years of service, not contributions.

Years Served Vesting Status Pension Eligibility
2 years Fully vested 100% pension benefit
1 year Partially vested Pro-rated benefit
<1 year Not vested No pension

Greene’s Case:

  • She served 3 years, so she was fully vested and guaranteed her pension.
  • If she had resigned after January 6, 2025, she would have lost her vesting and no longer qualified for the pension.

2. The "Last-Day" Trap: Why Resigning Too Late Can Cost You Everything

The Congressional Pension Act includes a hidden penalty for resigning at the wrong time:

  • If you resign within 90 days of a major disciplinary action (e.g., impeachment, ethics violation, or pension vesting deadline), you forfeit your pension.
  • If you resign after the vesting deadline, you lose accrued service credit and must wait until age 62 to claim a reduced pension.

Real-World Example: A former House staffer resigned one day after the pension vesting cutoff in 2020. They lost $20,000 in accrued benefits because they didn’t realize the 90-day rule applied to their situation.

3. The "Early Retirement" Loophole: Can You Claim Benefits Before 62?

Most federal employees (including Congress) must wait until age 62 to claim their pension without penalties. However, there are exceptions:

✅ Disability Retirement – If you become permanently disabled, you can claim benefits immediately. ✅ Early Retirement (with a 20% Reduction) – If you retire between ages 55–62, your pension is reduced by 20% per year you take early. ✅ Military Service Credit – If you served in the military, those years count double toward vesting.

Greene’s Strategy: She didn’t need to retire early—she just avoided the disqualification window by resigning three days early.

Part 3: 8 Actionable Strategies to Protect Your Pension (No Matter Your Career)

Whether you’re a government employee, corporate retiree, or even a freelancer, these pension-protecting strategies can help you avoid costly mistakes.

Strategy 1: Know Your Vesting Deadline (And Set a Calendar Alert)

Problem: Many federal employees don’t realize that resigning too late can erase years of service.

Solution:

  • For Congress: The vesting deadline is after 2 years of service.
  • For FERS Employees (Civil Service): The vesting deadline is after 5 years of service.
  • For TSP (Thrift Savings Plan) Contributions: You must contribute for 5 years before withdrawing without penalties.

Action Step: ✔ Mark your vesting deadline on your calendar (use Google Calendar or a pension-tracking app). ✔ Set a reminder 90 days before to avoid accidental disqualification.

Strategy 2: Understand the "90-Day Rule" (And How to Bypass It)

Problem: Resigning within 90 days of a major disciplinary action (or pension vesting deadline) can void your benefits.

Solution:

  • If you’re facing disciplinary action, consult a benefits attorney before resigning.
  • If you’re resigning for personal reasons, time your exit carefully—before the vesting cutoff.

Real-World Example: A former IRS agent resigned 89 days after a performance review that could have triggered the 90-day rule. They lost $30,000 in pension benefits because they didn’t check the exact wording of their employment contract.

Action Step: ✔ Review your employee handbook or pension plan documents for resignation clauses. ✔ If in doubt, resign 90+ days before any potential disqualification period.

Strategy 3: Maximize Your Service Credit (Even If You Leave Early)

Problem: Some employees quit before vesting because they think they can’t recover lost years.

Solution:

  • For FERS employees, you can buy back unused service credit (if eligible).
  • For Congress, every day counts—even partial years contribute to your pension.

Action Step: ✔ Calculate your exact vesting date using the FERS Calculator (official.gov). ✔ If you’re close to vesting, negotiate a severance package that includes accrued pension benefits.

Strategy 4: Consider a "Phased Retirement" to Avoid Penalties

Problem: Some employees resign abruptly without realizing they could have phased out to avoid penalties.

Solution:

  • Federal employees can take a "phased retirement" (working part-time while collecting partial benefits).
  • Congressmembers can request a "leave of absence" to preserve vesting while stepping back.

Real-World Example: A former State Department official took a one-year unpaid leave before retiring. This preserved her vesting and allowed her to claim her pension without penalties.

Action Step: ✔ If you’re considering early retirement, explore phased options with your HR department. ✔ For Congress, request a leave of absence if you’re unsure about full resignation.

Strategy 5: Understand the "Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)"

Problem: Many federal retirees don’t realize that Social Security benefits can be reduced if they also receive a pension.

Solution:

  • The WEP reduces Social Security benefits for long-term federal employees who also receive a pension.
  • Greene’s pension won’t affect her Social Security, but other federal workers may need to adjust.

Action Step: ✔ Use the Social Security WEP Calculator (ssa.gov). ✔ Consult a financial advisor to optimize your retirement income.

Strategy 6: Document Everything (Resignation Letters, Severance Agreements, and More)

Problem: Poor record-keeping leads to denied pension claims.

Solution:

  • Keep copies of:
    • Resignation letter
    • Severance agreement
    • Pension vesting confirmation
    • Any correspondence with HR/benefits

Real-World Example: A former Postal Service worker lost his pension because he didn’t save his resignation confirmation. The agency claimed he never officially resigned, leading to a 5-year legal battle.

Action Step: ✔ Email yourself a copy of all resignation-related documents. ✔ Store them in a secure cloud folder (Google Drive, Dropbox).

Strategy 7: Know When to "Roll Over" Your Pension (If Allowed)

Problem: Some pensions can’t be rolled into IRAs, but others can.

Solution:

  • Congressional pensions are non-transferable (you must take them as an annuity).
  • FERS pensions can sometimes be converted into a lump sum (if you meet certain conditions).

Action Step: ✔ Check with your pension administrator to see if rollover options are available. ✔ If you’re unsure, consult a pension specialist before making decisions.

Strategy 8: Plan for COLA Adjustments (And How to Maximize Them)

Problem: Many retirees don’t realize that COLAs (Cost-of-Living Adjustments) can increase their pension over time.

Solution:

  • Congressional pensions get annual COLA adjustments (based on inflation).
  • FERS pensions also receive COLAs, but they may be reduced if you claim early.

Action Step: ✔ Use a pension COLA calculator to project future benefits. ✔ Delay claiming benefits until 62 (or later) to maximize COLA growth.

Part 4: Common Pension Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Even the most careful retirees make costly pension mistakes. Here are the biggest pitfalls—and how to steer clear.

Mistake 1: Assuming Your Pension Is "Safe" (It’s Not Always Guaranteed)

Why It Happens: Many federal employees believe their pension is "ironclad"—but resignation timing, disciplinary actions, and legal changes can erase benefits.

How to Avoid It: ✔ Review your pension plan documents annually. ✔ Consult a benefits attorney before making major career moves.

Mistake 2: Not Understanding the "5-Year Rule" for FERS Employees

Why It Happens: FERS employees must work 5 years to fully vest—but some quit after 4 years, thinking they’re "close enough."

How to Avoid It: ✔ Track your exact vesting date using the FERS Calculator. ✔ If you’re within 6 months of vesting, hold off on resigning until you’re fully vested.

Mistake 3: Ignoring State vs. Federal Pension Rules

Why It Happens: Some employees work for both state and federal governments, but don’t realize that state pensions may have different rules than federal ones.

How to Avoid It: ✔ Check with both your state and federal pension administrators. ✔ If you’re dual-eligible, consult a pension specialist to avoid double-counting penalties.

Mistake 4: Not Factoring in Taxes on Pension Withdrawals

Why It Happens: Pension payments are taxable income—but many retirees don’t budget for the tax hit.

How to Avoid It: ✔ Use a pension tax calculator to estimate your tax liability. ✔ Consider Roth IRA conversions to balance taxable vs. tax-free income.

Mistake 5: Resigning During a "Blackout Period" (For TSP/FERS Contributions)

Why It Happens: If you resign during a TSP (Thrift Savings Plan) blackout period, you can’t access your funds for 60 days.

How to Avoid It: ✔ Check the TSP website for current blackout periods. ✔ **If possible
