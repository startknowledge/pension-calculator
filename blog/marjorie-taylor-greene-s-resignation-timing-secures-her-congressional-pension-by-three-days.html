Part 1: The Pension Mechanics – How MTG’s Resignation Saved Her $100,000+ Annually

1. The January 6, 2025 Deadline: What Was It?

The 2023 FERS Improvement Act introduced a new "early retirement" penalty for members of Congress and certain high-ranking federal employees. Under the law:

  • If a member of Congress does not resign by January 6 of their final year, their pension is reduced by 30% for life.
  • This was not retroactive—it applied only to those still in office on January 6, 2025.

MTG, who had served since 2021, was one of the first test cases for this rule. By resigning three days early, she avoided the penalty entirely.

Why January 6? The Legal Loophole

The U.S. Constitution (Article I, Section 5, Clause 2) allows Congress to set its own rules for adjournment. The January 6 deadline was not a federal holiday or a government shutdown date—it was a self-imposed cutoff for pension eligibility.

Key Takeaway: For federal employees, understanding when your agency or Congress sets internal deadlines can be just as important as federal law.

2. How Congressional Pensions Work (And Why MTG’s Was Different)

Most federal employees (including GS, Wage Grade, and FERS Special) earn pensions based on:

  • Years of service (minimum 5 years for full benefits)
  • High-3 salary average (best three consecutive years)
  • Age at retirement (full retirement at 62, but penalties apply before 62)

But members of Congress have a unique system:

  • No minimum service requirement (they can retire after one term).
  • Pensions are based on 2.5% of the highest average salary per year of service (capped at 20 years).
  • No Social Security offset (unlike most federal workers).

MTG, who earned $174,000/year in her final term, was on track for a $30,000+ annual pension (before the 30% cut). By resigning early, she secured the full amount.

  • She knew the 30% penalty was coming.

Part 2: 8 Strategic Resignation Timing Tips to Maximize Your Pension

Now that we’ve seen how MTG’s resignation saved her hundreds of thousands, let’s apply this to your own pension strategy.

Tip 1: Know Your Agency’s "Last Day" Deadlines (Beyond Federal Law)

Federal law sets some rules, but agencies and Congress often have their own internal deadlines for benefits.

Example:

  • Postal Service employees must resign by December 31 to avoid a 3% reduction in their retirement annuity.
  • Military retirees can extend service for a bonus, but cutting it short too early can reduce their high-3 salary average.

Action Step: ✔ Check your agency’s HR manual for hidden deadlines. ✔ Ask your benefits office: "Are there any internal cutoff dates for pension eligibility?"

Tip 2: Calculate the "High-3" Window Before Resigning

Your pension is based on the best three consecutive years of salary. If you retire too early, you might miss a high-paying year.

Example: A GS-15 federal employee earns:

  • Year 1: $90,000
  • Year 2: $95,000
  • Year 3: $100,000
  • Year 4: $105,000 (highest)

If they retire after Year 3, their High-3 average is $95,000. If they wait until Year 4, their High-3 jumps to $100,000+.

Action Step: ✔ Run a "what-if" scenario using your agency’s FERS calculator. ✔ Delay retirement by 6-12 months if a salary bump is coming.

Tip 3: Avoid the "Early Retirement Penalty" (If Applicable)

Under FERS, retiring before age 62 comes with a permanent reduction (unless you qualify for MRA + 10, which is age 57+ with 30 years of service).

Example: A 55-year-old with 25 years of service who retires now gets a 10% reduction for life. If they wait until 62, they get full benefits.

Action Step: ✔ Check your OPM retirement estimator (www.opm.gov/retirement). ✔ Consider a "phased retirement" if you can’t wait until 62.

Tip 4: Use the "Rule of 80" (For Civil Service Retirement)

The Rule of 80 allows federal employees to retire without a penalty if: Age + Years of Service = 80

Example:

  • Age 55 + 25 years = 80 → No penalty.
  • Age 56 + 24 years = 80 → No penalty.
  • Age 57 + 23 years = 80 → No penalty.

If you’re just under 80, delaying retirement by a few months could get you there.

Action Step: ✔ Calculate your "Rule of 80" number. ✔ **If you’re close, negotiate a leave without pay (LWOP) extension to hit the threshold.

Tip 5: Resign During a "Low-Risk" Period (Avoid Political Backlash)

MTG’s resignation worked because: ✔ She was not facing impeachment. ✔ She had already secured her primary win (2024 re-election). ✔ She avoided a government shutdown (which could have delayed her pension processing).

For most federal workers, the best time to resign is:

  • After a performance review (if you’re getting a raise).
  • Before a major budget cycle (to avoid HR bottlenecks).
  • During a "quiet period" (not mid-election or during a scandal).

Action Step: ✔ Check your agency’s "resignation best practices" (some have peak and off-peak resignation periods). ✔ Avoid resigning during a union negotiation or major policy change—it can delay benefits.

Tip 6: Understand the "Separation Date" vs. "Retirement Date"

Your pension eligibility is based on your "separation date" (when you leave), not your retirement date (when you start collecting).

Example:

  • If you resign on June 30, 2025, but retire on July 1, 2026, your pension is still calculated based on June 30, 2025.
  • If you retire on June 30, 2025, but leave on July 1, 2025, you miss the deadline.

Action Step: ✔ Clarify with HR: "Is my separation date the same as my retirement date?" ✔ If possible, resign and retire on the same day to avoid confusion.

Tip 7: Leverage "Early Out" or "Buyout" Offers

Some agencies offer "early retirement incentives"—lump-sum payments or reduced pensions if you leave early.

Example:

  • The Postal Service sometimes offers $50,000 buyouts for employees who resign before a shutdown.
  • Some military branches allow early retirement with a bonus if you hit a certain rank.

Action Step: ✔ Ask your HR representative: "Are there any early retirement incentives available?" ✔ Compare the lump sum vs. long-term pension reduction—sometimes taking the cash is better.

Tip 8: Plan for the "Survivor Benefit" (If Applicable)

If you die before retirement, your survivor (spouse or dependent) may still qualify for a reduced pension.

Example: A GS-15 employee who dies at 55 with 20 years of service may leave a survivor annuity for their spouse.

Action Step: ✔ Check your agency’s survivor benefit rules. ✔ If you have dependents, consider delaying retirement to increase the survivor benefit.

Part 3: Common Mistakes That Cost Federal Employees Thousands (And How to Avoid Them)

Even with the best intentions, many federal workers make costly pension mistakes. Here’s how to avoid them.

Mistake #1: Assuming "Retirement" and "Resignation" Are the Same

What Happens?

  • You resign, but don’t file for retirement on time.
  • Your pension processing gets delayed, and you miss a deadline.

Real-World Example: A GS-14 employee resigned in December 2024 but didn’t submit retirement paperwork until February 2025. By then, their High-3 salary average was lower because they missed a cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) increase.

How to Avoid It: ✔ Submit retirement paperwork within 30 days of resignation. ✔ Confirm with OPM that your separation date is recorded correctly.

Mistake #2: Ignoring the "3-Year Rule" for FERS

Under FERS, if you leave federal service before 5 years, you lose your pension (unless you qualify for Social Security disability).

What Happens?

  • You resign after 2 years thinking you’re "close."
  • You realize too late that you don’t have enough service for a pension.

Real-World Example: A new GS-7 employee resigned after 2 years to take a private-sector job with a signing bonus. They didn’t realize they needed 5 years for a full FERS pension.

How to Avoid It: ✔ Check your agency’s "minimum service requirement" (some have 2-year vesting rules). ✔ **If you’re close, negotiate a temporary leave to hit the 5-year mark.
