Why Three Days? The Congressional Pension Formula

Congressional pensions are governed by the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS), which includes:

  • A defined benefit pension (based on years of service and salary)
  • A Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) match (similar to a 401(k))
  • Special retirement benefits for members who serve at least 20 years

The key variable in MTG’s case was how her resignation affected her "years of service" calculation.

Under FERS rules:

  • Full-time service counts as 1 year per 52 weeks (or prorated for part-time).
  • A resignation before the end of a term can shorten the service calculation, potentially reducing pension eligibility—or, in MTG’s case, optimizing it.

However, resigning after the term ends (but before the new Congress starts) does not count as a "break in service"—meaning her full 2023–2025 term (2 years) was recognized for pension purposes, even though she left early.

The three-day window was critical because:

  1. January 3–5, 2025: MTG was still an active member, accruing service credit.
  2. January 6, 2025: Resignation took effect, but not before the term’s service credit was locked in.
  3. January 9, 2025: Congress adjourned, but MTG’s pension eligibility was already secured.

This micro-timing ensured she qualified for the full 2-year service credit without triggering early retirement penalties or reduced benefits.

Part 2: The Legal & Financial Mechanics of Congressional Pensions

How Congressional Pensions Work (And Why Timing Matters)

Congressional pensions are not like private-sector retirement plans. They are government-backed, defined-benefit systems with unique rules that can be exploited—or mishandled—with severe financial consequences.

1. The Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS)

Most federal employees (including Congress) are under FERS, which has three components:

  • Basic Benefit Plan (BBP) – The defined pension.
  • Special Retirement Supplement (SRS) – For those retiring before age 62.
  • Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) – A 401(k)-style match.

2. The Pension Formula: High-3 Salary × Years of Service

The core pension calculation is: Annual Pension = (High-3 Salary × 1.5% × Years of Service) × (1 + 1% for each year over 20)

  • High-3 Salary: The average of your highest three consecutive years of pay.
  • Years of Service: Counts only full years (or prorated for part-time).
  • Multiplier: 1.5% per year for the first 20 years, then increases by 1% per year beyond 20.

Example:

  • A 30-year congressional staffer earning $180,000 in their last year:
    • High-3 = $180,000 (assuming consistent pay).
    • 30 years × 1.5% = 45% of $180,000 = $81,000/year.
    • + 10 extra years (30–40) × 1% = +10% → $91,800/year.

3. The "Break in Service" Rule (And Why MTG Avoided It)

If an employee leaves federal service for more than 52 weeks, their pension service clock resets.

  • MTG’s resignation on January 6, 2025, was within the same term, so no break in service.
  • If she had resigned after January 9, 2025, her service credit might have been interrupted, forcing her to restart her pension clock if she returned later.

Real-World Impact:

  • A senior staffer with 15 years who resigns for 6 months could lose those 15 years if they don’t return within 52 weeks.
  • MTG’s three-day window ensured no reset, locking in her 2023–2025 service.

4. The "Early Retirement" Penalty (And Why MTG Didn’t Trigger It)

Under FERS, if you retire before age 62, you may face:

  • Reduced benefits (if retiring before 20 years of service).
  • No Special Retirement Supplement (SRS) (unless you qualify for disability).

MTG did not retire early—she resigned, meaning she could return to Congress later without losing pension benefits.

Part 3: 8 Actionable Strategies to Optimize Your Pension Benefits

Whether you’re a federal employee, military officer, or corporate executive with a defined benefit plan, these proven strategies can help you maximize your pension—just like MTG did with her resignation timing.

Strategy 1: Understand Your "High-3" Salary Window

Problem: Many workers assume their last year’s salary is their High-3, but earlier years can boost your average.

Solution:

  • Track your highest three consecutive years (not just the last three).
  • If you had a high-earning year early in your career, include it in your High-3.

Example:

  • A federal employee with salaries:
    • Year 1: $60,000
    • Year 2: $70,000
    • Year 3: $80,000
    • Year 4: $90,000
    • Year 5: $100,000
  • High-3 = $80K + $90K + $100K = $270K average → $90K High-3
  • If they only took Years 3–5 ($80K + $90K + $100K), same result.
  • But if they had a $120K year earlier, they could include that for a higher average.

Action Step:

  • Review your pay stubs and calculate your High-3 manually.
  • If possible, negotiate a raise in a high-earning year to boost your average.

Strategy 2: Time Your Resignation to Avoid "Break in Service"

Problem: Leaving federal service for more than 52 weeks resets your pension clock.

Solution:

  • If you’re close to retirement, resign at the end of a term (like MTG did).
  • If you’re not ready to retire, avoid long gaps—return within 52 weeks to preserve service credit.

Example:

  • A military officer with 18 years who resigns for 6 months could lose those 18 years if they don’t return.
  • Instead, take a short leave (under 52 weeks) to avoid the reset.

Action Step:

  • Check your agency’s rules on "break in service."
  • If resigning, time it to the end of a fiscal year to lock in service credit.

Strategy 3: Consider a "Phased Retirement" to Boost Years of Service

Problem: Some workers retire early but miss out on extra years that could increase their pension multiplier.

Solution:

  • If you’re close to 20 years, work part-time or consult to reach 20+ years before full retirement.
  • Example: A federal employee with 19 years who takes a 1-year consulting job could reach 20 years and get the higher multiplier.

Action Step:

  • Calculate how many more years you need to hit 20+.
  • Negotiate a flexible work arrangement (e.g., reduced hours, contract work) to extend service.

Strategy 4: Maximize Your TSP (Thrift Savings Plan) Match

Problem: Many federal employees don’t contribute enough to get the full employer match.

Solution:

  • The federal match is 1% of salary (up to 5% if you contribute).
  • Contribute at least 5% to get the full match (up to $8,700 in 2024 for a $174,000 salary).

Example:

  • MTG’s salary: $174,000
  • 5% contribution = $8,700
  • Federal match = 1% = $1,740
  • Total TSP contribution = $10,440/year

Action Step:

  • Set up automatic TSP contributions at the minimum to get the match.
  • Increase contributions if possible to boost retirement savings.

Strategy 5: Use the "Rule of 85" for Early Retirement (If Applicable)

Problem: Some federal employees can retire early under the "Rule of 85" (age + years of service ≥ 85).

Solution:

  • If you’re close to 85, retire early to avoid penalties.
  • Example: A 55-year-old with 30 years (55 + 30 = 85) can retire at 55 with full benefits.

Action Step:

  • Check if you qualify for the Rule of 85.
  • Consult a financial advisor to optimize your retirement timing.
