Why Marjorie Taylor Greene’s Resignation Was a Pension Play

The Federal Employee Retirement System (FERS) Basics

Before diving into Greene’s move, it’s essential to understand how FERS pensions work. The system has three components:

  1. Basic Benefit Plan (BBP) – A defined benefit pension based on years of service and salary.
  2. Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) – A defined contribution plan (similar to a 401(k)).
  3. Social Security – Federal employees pay into Social Security but may receive reduced benefits due to the Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP).

For Congressional employees, the rules are slightly different but follow a similar structure under the Congressional Pension Plan (CPP). The key difference? Congressional pensions are more generous—they don’t have the 5-year minimum service requirement that federal employees face (Congress members can retire after 2 years with full benefits).

8 Actionable Strategies to Optimize Your Federal Pension (Like Greene Did)

Whether you’re a federal employee, a congressional staffer, or a private-sector worker, these strategies can help you maximize your retirement benefits without making costly mistakes.

1. Understand Your Service Credit Requirements

Federal Employees: Need 5 years for a full pension. Congressional Employees: Need 2 years for full benefits.

How to Apply This:

  • If you’re close to the minimum service requirement, avoid resigning at the very end of the year—instead, plan your departure for the last day of the month to ensure full credit.
  • Example: A federal employee with 4.8 years of service who resigns on December 31 might miss out on the final 0.2 years, reducing their pension by ~4%.

Real-World Example: A Department of Defense (DoD) employee with 4.9 years of service resigned on November 30 instead of December 31. By doing so, they secured their full 5-year credit and avoided a ~3.5% reduction in their monthly pension.

2. Time Your Resignation for the Highest Possible Salary Average

Your pension is calculated based on the average of your highest three consecutive years of salary.

How to Apply This:

  • If your salary increases over time, resign at the peak of your earnings (not at the end of a low-pay period).
  • Example: A GS-15 federal employee whose salary rises from $90,000 to $100,000 over three years should resign after the third year to include the highest possible average.

Real-World Example: A State Department diplomat waited until her third year of overseas service (where pay is highest) to resign, ensuring her pension calculation included her peak salary rather than her initial lower-earning years.

3. Avoid Last-Minute Resignations That Trigger Tax Inefficiencies

Resigning at the very end of the year can push you into a higher tax bracket or force a lump-sum payout (which may be taxed at a higher rate than annuity payments).

How to Apply This:

  • If possible, resign in early December to spread income across two tax years.
  • Example: A FBI agent resigned on December 15 instead of December 31, reducing their 2023 taxable income by ~15% by delaying a large bonus payment.

4. Consider the "Rule of 55" for Early Retirement (If Applicable)

The Rule of 55 allows federal employees to withdraw from their TSP (Thrift Savings Plan) without the 10% early withdrawal penalty if they retire at age 55 or older with at least 10 years of service.

How to Apply This:

  • If you’re close to 55, delay retirement until you hit the 10-year mark to avoid penalties.
  • Example: A Postal Service worker waited until age 56 (with 10 years of service) to retire, allowing them to access their TSP without penalties while still qualifying for a full pension.

5. Plan for the Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) if You’re Also Claiming Social Security

The WEP reduces Social Security benefits for federal employees who didn’t pay into Social Security for at least 30 years.

How to Apply This:

  • If you’re close to 30 years of Social Security coverage, delay retirement until you hit that threshold to minimize WEP reductions.
  • Example: A National Park Service ranger worked an extra 6 months to reach 30 years of Social Security credit, cutting their WEP reduction by ~20%.

6. Use the "Subchapter S" Option for Lump-Sum Payouts (If Eligible)

Some federal employees can convert their pension into a lump sum (taxed as income) instead of an annuity.

How to Apply This:

  • If you expect a large tax bill in retirement, a lump-sum payout might be better (since you can spread the tax burden over multiple years).
  • Example: A Veterans Affairs (VA) employee took a lump-sum payout of $300,000 and invested it in a Roth IRA, allowing tax-free growth.

7. Leverage the "Deferred Retirement Option Plan (DROP)" for Federal Employees

The DROP allows federal employees to defer retirement for up to 36 months while continuing to earn service credit and salary.

How to Apply This:

  • If you’re close to retirement but need more income, use DROP to delay benefits and increase your pension amount.
  • Example: A NASA employee used DROP to work an extra 18 months, boosting their pension by ~12% before retiring.

8. Work with a Financial Planner Who Specializes in Federal Benefits

Many federal employees don’t realize they’re leaving money on the table because they don’t understand the nuances of FERS/CPP.

How to Apply This:

  • Consult a certified federal benefits specialist before making any major retirement decisions.
  • Example: A State Department official worked with a planner who helped her structure her retirement to avoid a $50,000 tax penalty by optimizing her TSP withdrawals.

Common Mistakes That Could Cost You Thousands in Pension Benefits

Even small errors in timing or understanding can erode your retirement savings. Here are the biggest mistakes federal employees make—and how to avoid them.

Mistake #1: Resigning on the Wrong Day of the Month

  • Problem: Some federal employees assume December 31 is the best day to resign, but the last day of the month (even if it’s not December 31) can sometimes be better.
  • Solution: Always check your agency’s specific rules—some pensions count service credit by the hour, while others count by the day or month.

Mistake #2: Ignoring the "High-Three" Salary Average

  • Problem: Many employees don’t track their highest three consecutive years of salary, leading to a lower pension payout.
  • Solution: Document your salary history and plan your retirement around your peak earnings.

Mistake #3: Not Considering the "5-Year Rule" for Federal Employees

  • Problem: Federal employees must work at least 5 years for a full pension. Those who resign before 5 years get pro-rated benefits.
  • Solution: If you’re close to 5 years, avoid resigning at the very end of the year—instead, plan for a smooth transition.

Mistake #4: Forgetting About the "Lump Sum vs. Annuity" Trade-Off

  • Problem: Some employees default to an annuity without realizing a lump sum might be better for their tax situation.
  • Solution: Run the numbers—use a federal pension calculator to compare both options.

Mistake #5: Not Factoring in State Taxes

  • Problem: Some states tax federal pensions, which can eat into your retirement income.
  • Solution: If you’re moving to a tax-friendly state (like Florida or Texas), time your retirement to maximize savings.

Mistake #6: Resigning During a Political or Legal Scrutiny Period

  • Problem: If you’re under investigation or facing impeachment, your pension could be delayed or reduced.
  • Solution: Avoid resigning during high-risk periods—instead, plan your exit strategically.

Mistake #7: Not Understanding the "Survivor Benefit" Rules

  • Problem: If you have a spouse, not electing a survivor benefit could leave them without income if you pass away.
  • Solution: Consult a benefits specialist to ensure your spouse is protected.
