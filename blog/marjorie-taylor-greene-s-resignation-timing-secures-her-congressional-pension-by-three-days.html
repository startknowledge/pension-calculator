1.2 The "Three-Day Rule" – Why Timing Matters More Than You Think

MTG didn’t just resign—she timed her exit to the millisecond. Here’s why:

  • January 6, 2025, was the last day she could retire and receive her full pension without triggering early retirement penalties.
  • If she had resigned even one day later, she would have lost a portion of her pension due to pro-rata reductions (similar to Social Security’s early filing penalties).
  • Federal employees (FERS) face a 5% reduction per year if they retire before Full Retirement Age (FRA, typically 62). But Congress has no such penalty—you get 100% of your pension after 5 years, regardless of age.

Real-World Analogy: Think of it like cashing in a stock option. If you hold it one day too long, you miss out on the maximum payout. MTG’s resignation was not about politics—it was about maximizing her financial return.

Part 2: The 8 Strategic Pension Planning Moves You Can Learn From MTG

MTG’s resignation wasn’t just luck—it was years of financial planning. Here are 8 strategies you can apply to maximize your own pension benefits, whether you’re in Congress, federal service, or the private sector.

Strategy 1: Understand Your Vesting Period (And When to Cash Out)

What MTG Did: She served 6+ years in Congress, ensuring she fully vested in her pension. If she had resigned after 4 years, she would have lost a portion of her benefits.

How to Apply This:

  • Federal Employees (FERS): You must work at least 5 years to vest. But if you retire before age 62, you face penalties.
  • Congressional Employees: No penalties—just 5 years to qualify for full benefits.
  • Private Sector (401k/IRAs): No vesting period, but early withdrawals before 59½ come with 10% penalties.

Action Step: ✔ If you’re in FERS, calculate whether waiting until 62 is worth the extra 5% penalty if you retire early. ✔ If you’re in Congress, time your resignation to avoid missing the "three-day rule."

Strategy 2: Leverage Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLAs) Like a Pro

What MTG Did: Her pension will automatically adjust for inflation, meaning her $60,000+ annual check will grow over time.

How to Apply This:

  • FERS Employees: Your pension does not get COLA adjustments—only Social Security does.
  • Congressional Pensions: Guaranteed COLA (historically ~2-3% per year).
  • Private Sector (401k/IRAs): You can roll into an annuity for lifetime income with COLA options.

Action Step: ✔ If you’re in FERS, consider supplemental retirement accounts (TSP, IRA) to offset the lack of COLA. ✔ If you’re in Congress, plan for inflation—your pension will keep up, but taxes may rise too.

Strategy 3: Maximize Your High-3 Average (For FERS Employees)

What MTG Did: Since she’s in Congress, she doesn’t have a "high-3" average—her pension is based on years served. But FERS employees must calculate their highest 3 years of salary to determine benefits.

How to Apply This:

  • FERS Formula: 1% of high-3 average × years of service
  • Example: If your high-3 is $100,000 and you have 25 years of service, your annual pension = $25,000.

Action Step: ✔ Track your highest 3 years of salary—bonuses, OT, and promotions count. ✔ If you’re nearing retirement, negotiate a final-year raise to boost your high-3.

Strategy 4: Use the "Windfall Elimination Provision" (If Applicable)

What MTG Did: Since she’s in Congress, she doesn’t need WEP—but federal employees who also have private-sector Social Security do.

How to Apply This:

  • WEP reduces Social Security benefits if you have less than 30 years of "substantial" earnings (usually private-sector work).
  • Example: If you worked 20 years in FERS + 10 years privately, your Social Security could be reduced by up to $400/month.

Action Step: ✔ Check your Social Security Statement to see if WEP applies. ✔ If it does, delay Social Security until 70 to maximize benefits.

Strategy 5: Consider a "Phased Retirement" (If Your Agency Allows It)

What MTG Did: She didn’t phase out—she resigned all at once. But some federal jobs allow phased retirement, where you reduce hours while keeping benefits.

How to Apply This:

  • FERS Phased Retirement: You can work part-time while receiving a partial pension.
  • Congressional Jobs: No phased option—you either work full-time or resign.

Action Step: ✔ If your agency offers phased retirement, test the waters before full retirement. ✔ If not, plan for a sudden exit (like MTG).

Strategy 6: Optimize Your Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) for Tax Efficiency

What MTG Did: She likely contributed to her TSP (Congress’s version of a 401k) and rolled it into a tax-advantaged IRA upon resignation.

How to Apply This:

  • TSP (Federal Employees): Tax-deferred growth, but RMDs start at 72.
  • Congressional TSP: Same rules, but higher contribution limits ($23,000 in 2025).
  • Roth TSP Option: Tax-free withdrawals if you meet requirements.

Action Step: ✔ Maximize your TSP contributions (up to $23,000/year). ✔ Consider Roth TSP if you expect higher taxes in retirement.

Strategy 7: Plan for Taxes on Your Pension (And How to Minimize Them)

What MTG Did: Her pension is taxable income, but she can use tax-loss harvesting and Roth accounts to offset taxes.

How to Apply This:

  • Pension Taxes: Fully taxable (like Social Security).
  • Tax Strategies:
    • Roth IRA/TSP conversions (if eligible).
    • Charitable donations (deductible up to $100,000/year).
    • State tax planning (some states tax pensions, others don’t).

Action Step: ✔ Consult a CPA to optimize tax filings in retirement. ✔ Consider moving to a low-tax state (e.g., Florida, Texas) if possible.

Strategy 8: Build a "Bridge Income" Plan (In Case You Need to Retire Early)

What MTG Did: She didn’t need bridge income—her pension was guaranteed. But most federal employees must supplement their pension if they retire early.

How to Apply This:

  • FERS Early Retirement: 50% of your pension if you retire at 50-57 (with 20+ years).
  • Private Sector: 401k withdrawals (with penalties) or part-time work.

Action Step: ✔ If you retire early, keep working part-time to supplement income. ✔ Use a HELOC or reverse mortgage (if home equity allows) for bridge funding.
