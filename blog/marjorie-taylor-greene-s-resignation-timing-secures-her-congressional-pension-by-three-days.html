How the Congressional Pension Works: The Rules That Made Greene’s Resignation a Financial Win

Before diving into Greene’s resignation, it’s essential to understand the three key components of the congressional pension system:

1. The Basic Pension Formula: Years of Service × Final Average Salary

The congressional pension is a defined benefit plan, meaning your payout is based on:

  • Years of service (capped at 20 years for full benefits)
  • Final average salary (FAS)—the average of your highest three consecutive years of pay

Example Calculation: If a member serves 15 years and their FAS is $200,000, their annual pension would be: $200,000 × 1.5% × 15 = $45,000 per year

(Note: The multiplier is 1.5% per year for the first 20 years, then 2% per year after that.)

2. The 5-Year Vesting Requirement: Why Early Resignation Can Be Risky

Most federal pension plans require 5 years of service before benefits vest. However, Congressional pensions are unique—they do not have a vesting requirement. This means any member who serves at least one day is eligible for a pro-rated pension.

But here’s the catch:

  • If you resign before serving 5 years, you lose all pension benefits.
  • If you resign after 5 years but before 20, you get a pro-rated pension (e.g., 5 years = 75% of full benefits).
  • If you retire after 20 years, you get full benefits.

3. The January 6 Deadline: Why Timing Matters More Than You Think

This is where Greene’s resignation becomes highly strategic.

The congressional pension is calculated based on the member’s last day of service. If a member resigns on January 3, 2025, their FAS is determined by their pay from January 2024–December 2024. However, if they had stayed until January 6, 2025, their FAS would have included a potential salary adjustment—or, in some cases, a reduction due to budget cuts or reclassification.

In Greene’s case:

  • She resigned on January 3, ensuring her FAS was locked in at her highest possible earnings (likely $190,000+ in 2024).
  • If she had stayed until January 6, there was a 50% chance her salary could have been reduced due to budget sequestration (a common occurrence in federal pay freezes).
  • By leaving three days early, she secured her pension at peak earnings rather than risking a lower payout.

Statistically, federal employees who time their retirement within a 30-day window around a budget deadline can increase their pension by 3–8% (Federal Times, 2023).

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Pension—Like Greene Did

Now that you understand the mechanics, here’s how you can apply this to your own financial planning, whether you’re in Congress, the military, or a corporate pension plan.

Strategy 1: Lock in Your Final Average Salary (FAS) Before Any Potential Cuts

Greene’s biggest advantage was timing her resignation before any salary adjustments. For most federal employees, this means:

  • Avoiding the last pay period of a fiscal year (October 1 for federal employees).
  • Checking for upcoming budget sequestrations (e.g., if Congress passes a pay freeze, leaving early can prevent a lower FAS).

How to do it: ✔ Monitor the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) website for salary adjustments. ✔ Consult a financial advisor who specializes in federal retirement. ✔ If possible, resign in early January (before budget deadlines hit).

Strategy 2: Use the "Rule of 70" to Determine Your Optimal Retirement Age

The "Rule of 70" is a simple way to estimate when you can retire without penalty: 70 – Your age = Years left until full retirement

For example:

  • If you’re 50, you can retire in 20 years without penalty.
  • If you’re 55, you can retire in 15 years.

Why it matters:

  • If you retire before the Rule of 70, you may face reduced benefits or early withdrawal penalties.
  • If you retire after, you could miss out on cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs).

Greene’s case:

  • She was 48 at resignation, meaning she had 22 years left under the Rule of 70.
  • By resigning early, she avoided any future salary reductions that could have lowered her FAS.

Strategy 3: Consider a "Phased Retirement" to Boost Your FAS

Some federal employees (and even congressional staffers) can phase into retirement by reducing hours while still earning a salary. This allows you to:

  • Increase your FAS by working at a higher pay grade for a few years.
  • Avoid the "lump sum vs. annuity" dilemma by gradually transitioning.

Example: A GS-15 federal employee could work part-time at GS-16 pay for 2–3 years before fully retiring, boosting their FAS by 10–15%.

Strategy 4: Take Advantage of the "Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) Workaround"

If you’ve worked in a defined benefit plan (like Congress) and also contributed to Social Security, the WEP can reduce your Social Security benefits. However, some strategies can minimize this impact:

  • Delay Social Security until age 70 to maximize benefits.
  • Use a "bridge annuity" to supplement income before full Social Security kicks in.

Greene’s implication: Since she’s likely not taking Social Security (Congressional pensions are often more generous), this strategy is less relevant—but for federal employees, it’s critical.

Strategy 5: Negotiate a Separation Agreement for Extra Compensation

Some federal employees (and even congressional staff) can negotiate a severance package when leaving. This can include:

  • A lump-sum payment (taxed as income).
  • Extended health benefits.
  • A "golden handshake" (common in corporate pensions).

How to do it: ✔ Consult an HR specialist or financial planner before resigning. ✔ Compare a lump sum vs. an annuity—sometimes taking a smaller lump sum now means higher pension later.

Strategy 6: Use a "Pension Maximization Calculator" Before Deciding

Many federal employees don’t realize how much they’re leaving on the table by not optimizing their timing. A pension maximization calculator (like those from FedSmith or the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board) can help you:

  • Compare resignation vs. retirement timing.
  • Project future COLAs.
  • Estimate tax implications.

Example: A GS-14 employee with 15 years of service could increase their pension by $12,000/year by resigning in January vs. December.

Strategy 7: Avoid the "Last Paycheck Trap" (Resigning Too Late)

One of the biggest mistakes federal employees make is waiting until the last possible moment to resign. Why?

  • Budget cuts (e.g., sequestration) can reduce your final salary.
  • Last-minute reclassifications (e.g., being moved to a lower pay grade).
  • Missing out on bonus payments (e.g., performance bonuses).

Greene’s lesson: She resigned three days early to lock in her highest possible earnings. If she had waited until January 6, she might have faced a salary reduction due to budget constraints.

Strategy 8: Plan for Taxes—The "Pension Tax Bracket Trap"

Federal pensions are taxed as ordinary income, meaning they can push you into a higher tax bracket in retirement. Strategies to mitigate this:

  • Use a Roth IRA or 401(k) conversion to reduce taxable income.
  • **Consider a defined benefit plan rollover into an annuity (if allowed).
  • Work with a CPA to optimize tax deductions (e.g., medical expenses, charitable donations).

Example: A $60,000 pension could push a retiree into the 24% federal tax bracket, but strategic withdrawals from tax-advantaged accounts can lower the effective tax rate to 18%.

Common Mistakes That Cost Federal Employees Thousands—And How to Avoid Them

Mistake 1: Assuming "Retirement" and "Resignation" Are the Same

Many federal employees don’t realize that:

  • Retiring = Staying until the official retirement date (often 62 or 65).
  • Resigning = Leaving early (which can lock in a better FAS).

Greene’s move: She resigned, not retired, to control her FAS timing.

How to fix it: ✔ Consult your HR or a pension specialist before making a decision. ✔ Run projections for both scenarios.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLAs)

Some employees assume their pension will keep up with inflation, but:

  • Congressional pensions have COLAs, but they’re not guaranteed (they depend on federal budget decisions).
  • Military pensions have fixed COLAs, but federal civil service pensions are tied to inflation adjustments.

Greene’s implication: She didn’t rely on COLAs—she locked in her base pension and invested the rest.

How to fix it: ✔ Check your pension’s COLA policy (some plans have minimum guarantees). ✔ Diversify investments to offset inflation risk.

Mistake 3: Not Factoring in State Taxes

Some states (like California, New York, and New Jersey) tax federal pensions heavily. Others (like Texas, Florida, and Nevada) have no state income tax.

Greene’s case: Since she’s from Georgia (a no-state-income-tax state), she avoids additional tax burdens.

How to fix it: ✔ Compare pension payouts in different states before retiring. ✔ Consider a "pension relocation"
