How Congressional Pensions Work: The Basics

Before diving into MTG’s resignation strategy, it’s essential to understand the fundamental structure of the Congressional Pension System (CPS).

1. Who Qualifies for a Congressional Pension?

The CPS is a defined-benefit plan for members of Congress, similar to federal employee pensions but with unique rules. To qualify, an individual must:

  • Serve at least 5 years in Congress (House or Senate).
  • Retire at or after age 62 (or meet other service-based thresholds).
  • Not have been removed from office due to impeachment or conviction.

2. How Are Pensions Calculated?

The pension formula is based on:

  • Years of service (maximum credit is 20 years, even if serving longer).
  • Final average salary (FAS)—the average of the highest three consecutive years of pay.
  • A multiplier (currently 1.5% per year of service for those retiring after 2018).

Example Calculation: If a lawmaker serves 15 years and earns a final average salary of $200,000, their annual pension would be: $200,000 × 1.5% × 15 = $45,000 per year

3. Why Does Resignation Timing Matter?

Unlike private-sector pensions, congressional pensions have strict eligibility windows. Missing the exact deadline can result in:

  • Reduced benefits (if you resign too early).
  • Loss of eligibility (if you resign too late).
  • Tax complications (if benefits are not properly structured).

MTG’s resignation on January 3, 2025, was not arbitrary—it was calculated to the day to ensure she met the final pay period requirement before her term ended.
