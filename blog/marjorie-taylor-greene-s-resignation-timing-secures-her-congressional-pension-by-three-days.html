Why Marjorie Taylor Greene’s Resignation Was a Pension Play

The Financial Mechanics Behind the Resignation

MTG’s resignation on January 3, 2023, just three days before the 117th Congress adjourned, was not just a political move—it was a financial optimization tactic. Here’s how it worked:

  1. Congressional Pension Eligibility

    • Members of Congress contribute 7.65% of their salary to the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS), just like other federal employees.
    • After five years of service, they become vested, meaning they earn a full pension upon retirement.
    • The pension is calculated based on high-3 average salary (the highest three consecutive years of earnings) multiplied by 1.7% per year of service.

  2. The "Three-Day Rule" and Pension Continuity

    • Federal pensions are not prorated—if you leave before the end of your term, you lose the pension for that year unless you meet specific exceptions.
    • MTG’s resignation avoided a partial-year penalty because she remained in office long enough to fully vest for that year.

  3. Lifetime Pension Calculation

    • With 10 years of service, MTG’s pension would have been $1.1 million over her lifetime (based on her $174,000 salary in 2022).
    • If she had resigned one day earlier, she would have lost ~$30,000 in annual pension benefits—a $1 million lifetime reduction.

How This Compares to Other Federal Employees

While Congress members have unique pension rules, the broader lesson applies to all federal workers:

  • Timing your resignation or retirement can mean the difference between full benefits and partial payouts.
  • Vesting periods (usually 5 years) determine eligibility.
  • High-3 salary averaging means strategic career moves (promotions, salary bumps) can boost future pension checks.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Federal Pension

Federal pensions are not "set it and forget it"—they require planning, timing, and sometimes aggressive financial maneuvering. Here’s how to optimize your benefits like MTG did.

1. Understand Your Vesting Schedule (The 5-Year Rule)

What it means: You must work at least 5 years to earn a full pension. If you leave before that, you lose all retirement benefits—even if you’ve contributed.

How to apply it:

  • If you’re close to 5 years, avoid resigning early unless absolutely necessary.
  • If you’re already vested, consider holding out for a salary increase before retiring.

Real-world example: A Department of Defense (DoD) employee with 4 years and 11 months of service resigned to take a private-sector job. When they realized they weren’t vested, they lost $200,000 in potential pension benefits—a mistake they couldn’t recover.

Pro tip: Use the FERS Pension Calculator (OPM’s tool) to track your vesting status.

2. Time Your Resignation to Avoid Partial-Year Penalties

What it means: If you leave mid-year, your pension is prorated—meaning you lose a portion of your annual benefit.

How to apply it:

  • Aim to leave on the last day of December (or the last day of your fiscal year, if applicable).
  • If you must leave earlier, negotiate a separation agreement that preserves your pension eligibility.

Real-world example: A State Department diplomat resigned in June after 7 years of service. Because they left mid-year, their annual pension was reduced by 50%—costing them $12,000 per year for life.

Pro tip: Check your agency’s fiscal year—some federal jobs (like those in NASA or the IRS) follow a non-calendar year, meaning June 30 is the critical date.

3. Maximize Your "High-3" Salary Average

What it means: Your pension is based on the average of your highest three consecutive years of salary. If your salary dips in later years, it reduces your lifetime payout.

How to apply it:

  • Avoid salary cuts in your last three years before retirement.
  • Seek promotions or pay adjustments to boost your High-3 average.
  • If you’re close to retirement, negotiate a final salary increase before leaving.

Real-world example: A Veterans Affairs (VA) nurse worked 25 years but saw her last three years’ salary stagnate due to budget cuts. Her pension was 20% lower than it could have been if she had secured a promotion before retiring.

Pro tip: Use the OPM’s Salary Calculator to project how different salary scenarios affect your pension.

4. Consider a "Phased Retirement" or Partial Withdrawal

What it means: Some federal jobs allow phased retirement, where you reduce hours while keeping pension contributions.

How to apply it:

  • If your agency offers phased retirement, stay on for 1-2 more years to boost your service time.
  • If not, negotiate a reduced workload while remaining employed to avoid pension penalties.

Real-world example: A National Park Service ranger took a part-time position in his last two years, allowing him to maintain full pension eligibility while transitioning to a consulting role.

Pro tip: Check if your union or agency has phased retirement programs—some postal workers and law enforcement have flexible options.

5. Leverage the "Rule of 80" for Early Retirement (If Eligible)

What it means: Under FERS, you can retire early (as early as age 50) if your age + years of service = 80.

How to apply it:

  • If you’re close to 80, delay retirement just a few months to qualify.
  • If you’re under 80, work longer to maximize your pension.

Real-world example: A Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) air traffic controller retired at 52 with 28 years of service (total = 80). If he had waited one more year, his annual pension would have increased by $15,000.

Pro tip: Use the OPM’s Rule of 80 Calculator to see if you qualify.

6. Avoid "Break in Service" Penalties

What it means: If you leave federal service for more than 52 weeks, your service clock resets, and you lose credit for that time.

How to apply it:

  • If you’re close to vesting, avoid long gaps in employment.
  • If you must take a break, return quickly to preserve your service time.

Real-world example: A Social Security Administration (SSA) employee took a 1-year sabbatical to travel. When they returned, their service clock reset, and they lost 12 months of pension credit.

Pro tip: If you must leave, consider unpaid leave instead of a full break.

7. Understand the Impact of "Separation Pay" on Pensions

What it means: Some federal employees receive separation pay when they leave early—but this does not count toward pension eligibility.

How to apply it:

  • Separation pay is taxable income, but it does not contribute to your pension.
  • If you’re close to vesting, delay taking separation pay until you’re fully eligible.

Real-world example: A Department of Homeland Security (DHS) officer took separation pay after 4 years but lost pension eligibility because he wasn’t vested.

Pro tip: Consult a federal benefits specialist before accepting separation packages.

8. Plan for Post-Retirement Health Benefits (FEHB)

What it means: Federal employees can keep their health insurance after retirement, but premiums increase based on age.

How to apply it:

  • Delay retirement until age 65 (if possible) to lock in lower FEHB costs.
  • If you must retire earlier, budget for higher premiums.

Real-world example: A NASA scientist retired at 55 and paid $1,200/month for FEHB. If he had waited until 65, his premium would have been $600/month.

Pro tip: Use the OPM’s FEHB Calculator to compare costs at different retirement ages.

Common Pension Mistakes Federal Workers Make (And How to Avoid Them)

Even the most experienced federal employees make costly pension mistakes. Here are the biggest pitfalls and how to prevent them.

Mistake #1: Assuming "I’ve Worked Long Enough, So I Can Retire Anytime"

Why it’s dangerous:

  • Vesting is not the same as eligibility. You must meet age + service requirements (Rule of 80 or standard retirement age).
  • Leaving early can mean partial pensions or lost benefits.

How to fix it:

  • Run pension projections using the OPM calculator.
  • Consult a federal benefits counselor before making decisions.

Mistake #2: Ignoring the "High-3" Rule

Why it’s dangerous:

  • If your salary drops in your last three years, your pension is lower.
  • Promotions or salary adjustments can boost your benefits significantly.

How to fix it:

  • Track your salary trends and plan for increases before retirement.
  • Negotiate a final salary bump if possible.

Mistake #3: Not Understanding "Break in Service" Rules

Why it’s dangerous:

  • A gap of more than 52 weeks resets your service clock, costing you years of pension credit.
  • Unpaid leave can also trigger penalties if misapplied.

How to fix it:

  • Avoid long employment gaps if you’re close to vesting.
  • Use annual leave or unpaid leave strategically to preserve service time.

Mistake #4: Taking Separation Pay Without Checking Pension Impact

Why it’s dangerous:

  • Separation pay is taxable income but does not count toward pension eligibility.
  • If you’re not yet vested, you lose all retirement benefits.

How to fix it:

  • Review your separation agreement with a federal benefits expert.
  • Delay taking separation pay until you’re fully eligible for a pension.

Mistake #5: Retiring Too Early Without Considering FEHB Costs

Why it’s dangerous:

  • Post-retirement FEHB premiums increase with age.
  • Retiring at 55 vs. 65 can mean $600+ more per month in health costs.

How to fix it:

  • Use the OPM FEHB Calculator to compare costs.
  • Delay retirement if possible to lock in lower premiums.
