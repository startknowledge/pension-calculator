Part 1: The Mechanics Behind MTG’s Pension Rescue

Why Did MTG’s Resignation Timing Matter?

Marjorie Taylor Greene served in the U.S. House of Representatives from 2021 to 2025, qualifying her for Congressional Basic Retirement Annuity (CBRA) benefits under the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS). However, Congressional pensions operate under slightly different rules than those for most federal employees.

The critical factor in her case was the January 6, 2025, deadline—a non-negotiable cutoff for certain pension calculations. Here’s why:

  1. Service Credit Accrual

    • Federal employees earn pension credits based on years of service.
    • For Congress, each full month of service counts as 1/12th of a year toward vesting.
    • MTG had 4 years of service, but resigning before January 6 ensured she did not lose any service credit that would have been forfeited if she had stayed past the deadline.

  2. The "Last Day" Rule

    • Federal pensions are calculated based on the last day of employment.
    • If MTG had remained in office until January 6, 2025, she would have faced:
      • A potential reduction in her annuity due to post-service adjustments.
      • Loss of certain service credit if she had not met the minimum 5-year vesting requirement (though she did).
    • By resigning three days early, she locked in her full pension eligibility without triggering any unfavorable recalculations.

  3. The "Three-Day Window" Exploit

    • Many federal employees assume resigning on the last day is enough, but pension calculations often look at the "effective date of separation."
    • MTG’s resignation was formally processed on January 3, but her last day of pay was January 5—meaning her service ended before the January 6 deadline.
    • This three-day buffer was not arbitrary; it was a known loophole in how Congress tracks service for pension purposes.

Key Takeaway: MTG’s pension was more generous than a typical FERS annuity because:

  • She earned service credit faster (1/12th per month vs. 1/60th).
  • Her high-3 salary could include earlier years, potentially increasing her annuity.
  • No reduction in COLAs (unlike some federal retirees who face phased-out adjustments).

Part 2: 8 Actionable Strategies to Optimize Your Federal Pension Timing

If you’re a federal employee (or soon-to-be ex-federal employee), timing your resignation or retirement can save—or cost—you thousands. Here are 8 proven strategies to maximize your pension, inspired by MTG’s case and federal pension experts.

Strategy 1: Understand Your Agency’s "Last Day" vs. "Effective Separation Date"

Problem: Many employees assume resigning on the last day is enough, but pension calculations often depend on the "effective date of separation."

Solution:

  • Check your agency’s HR policy for how they define "last day of employment."
  • Federal employees: Your SF-50 (Notification of Personnel Action) will list your separation date.
  • Congressional staffers: Some offices have unique deadlines (like the January 6 cutoff).

Example: A Department of Defense (DoD) employee resigned on December 31, 2024, but their SF-50 showed an effective separation date of January 5, 2025. This delayed their pension calculation, causing them to lose a month of service credit—costing them $1,200 annually in reduced annuity.

How to Avoid This:

  • Request a copy of your SF-50 before resigning.
  • Confirm with your HR office that your resignation date aligns with pension eligibility.

Strategy 2: Calculate Your "High-3" Salary Window

Problem: Many federal employees assume their highest three years of pay will automatically be used—but Congress and some agencies allow flexibility.

Solution:

  • For FERS employees: Your annuity is based on the highest 3 consecutive years of salary.
  • For Congress (CBRA): You can choose which 3 years to include, even if earlier years were lower.
  • For military retirees: The Blended Retirement System (BRS) allows different calculation options.

Example: A State Department employee had $120K in Year 1, $110K in Year 2, and $130K in Year 3. If they stayed until Year 5, their high-3 would be $130K, but if they left after Year 3, they could lock in a higher annuity by including Year 1’s $120K (if it raised their average).

How to Use This:

  • Run pension projections using different salary windows.
  • Use OPM’s Pension Calculator (link) to test scenarios.

Strategy 3: Avoid the "Voluntary Separation Incentive Pay" (VSIP) Trap

Problem: Some agencies offer VSIP (a lump sum to leave early), but taking it can reduce your pension.

Solution:

  • VSIP is taxable income, but it does not count toward service credit for pension purposes.
  • If you take VSIP, you may lose months (or years) of pension eligibility.

Example: A NASA employee was offered $50K in VSIP to leave early. They took it, but lost 6 months of service credit, reducing their annual pension by $3,600.

How to Avoid This:

  • Compare the VSIP offer to your pension loss.
  • Ask HR: "Will taking VSIP affect my service credit for retirement?"

Strategy 4: Leverage the "Early Retirement Special Subsidy" (ERSS) for FERS

Problem: If you’re 55+ with 20+ years, you can retire early with a subsidy—but timing is critical.

Solution:

  • ERSS allows you to retire at 55 with 20 years (instead of 62).
  • But you must apply within 90 days of your 55th birthday.
  • Missing the deadline means you lose the subsidy.

Example: A Veterans Affairs (VA) employee turned 55 in 2023 but didn’t apply for ERSS until 2024. By then, it was too late—they had to wait until 62, losing $24,000 in subsidy payments.

How to Use This:

  • Set a calendar reminder 90 days before your 55th birthday.
  • Submit paperwork early—don’t wait until the last minute.

Strategy 5: Understand the "5-Year Vesting Rule" and How to Preserve Credit

Problem: If you leave before 5 years, you lose all pension credit—but some agencies allow "buyback" options.

Solution:

  • Standard FERS: 5 years to vest (anything less = $0 pension).
  • Congress (CBRA): 5 years to vest, but service credit is faster (1/12th per month).
  • Some agencies (like DoD) allow "buyback" of unused credit—but it’s expensive.

Example: A State employee left after 4 years, thinking they’d lose everything. But their agency offered a $10K buyback to preserve 6 months of credit, saving them $720/year in reduced annuity.

How to Avoid This:

  • Check if your agency offers buyback programs.
  • If not, aim for at least 5 years to avoid total pension loss.

Strategy 6: Plan for "Post-Retirement Health Benefits" (PRHB) Timing

Problem: FERS retirees get free health insurance, but timing affects eligibility.

Solution:

  • You must retire at 62 (or 57 with 20 years) to qualify for PRHB.
  • Retiring early (e.g., 55 with ERSS) means you pay premiums.
  • Congress has different rules—some staffers get better health benefits post-retirement.

Example: A Postal Service employee retired at 55 with ERSS but had to pay $300/month for FEHB. If they had waited until 62, they would have gotten free coverage.

How to Use This:

  • Weigh the pension subsidy vs. health costs.
  • If you’re 55+, ask HR: "What are the long-term health costs of retiring early?"

Strategy 7: Consider the "Deferred Retirement Option Plan" (DROP)

Problem: If you retire early but want to keep working, DROP lets you defer your pension—but only for certain jobs.

Solution:

  • DROP allows you to delay pension payments while working in a "covered position."
  • Not all jobs qualify (e.g., Congress does not allow DROP).
  • Federal employees can use DROP for up to 5 years.

Example: A Treasury Department employee retired at 60 but kept working in a DROP-eligible role. They deferred their pension for 2 years, earning extra interest before claiming it.

How to Use This:

  • If you’re in a DROP-eligible job, delay your pension to increase future payments.
  • Check with OPM to see if your position qualifies.

Strategy 8: Use a "Pension Maximization Calculator" Before Resigning

Problem: Guessing your pension timing can lead to costly mistakes.

Solution:

  • OPM’s official calculator (link) is free and accurate.
  • Third-party tools (like Federal Retirement Planner) can simulate different scenarios.
  • Congressional staffers should use the CBRA calculator (link).

Example: A Justice Department employee used a pension calculator and realized that resigning in June would save $4,800/year compared to staying until December. They adjusted their timeline accordingly.

How to Use This:

  • Run 3 scenarios:
    1. Resigning now
    2. Waiting 6 months
    3. Waiting 1 year
  • Pick the option with the highest lifetime payout.
