Part 1: The MTG Pension Case Study – A Deep Dive into the Numbers

Why Did MTG Resign on January 3, 2025? The Pension Trigger Explained

Marjorie Taylor Greene’s resignation was not just political—it was financial. Here’s how the numbers played out:

1. The Congressional Pension Formula: High-High-Low

Federal congressional pensions are calculated under the High-Three Average Salary (H-3) method, where:

  • The highest three consecutive years of salary are averaged.
  • This average is then multiplied by 1.7% for each year of service (up to 30 years).

However, there’s a critical catch: If you leave Congress before January 3 of any given year, you lose the salary from that year in your pension calculation.

2. MTG’s Salary & Service Timeline

  • 2021–2024 Salaries: MTG earned $174,000 per year (standard congressional pay).
  • 2025 Salary (if she stayed): She would have earned $174,000 again, but resigning on January 3, 2025, meant she didn’t earn it.

Why did this matter?

  • If MTG had stayed until January 4, 2025, her 2025 salary would have been included in her High-Three average, potentially increasing her pension by thousands per year.
  • By resigning three days early, she excluded 2025 from her H-3 calculation, ensuring her highest three years remained 2022–2024—likely her peak earnings.

3. The Pension Impact: How Much Did She Save?

Using conservative estimates (assuming 25 years of service):

  • Including 2025 salary (if she stayed): Her H-3 average might have been $174,000 × 1.7% × 25 = ~$73,100/year.
  • Excluding 2025 (by resigning early): Her H-3 average could have been $174,000 × 1.7% × 24 = ~$68,680/year (a $4,420 annual reduction).

But wait—it’s even more nuanced.

  • If 2025 was not one of her top three years, then no change would occur.
  • However, if 2025 was her third-highest year, then resigning early saved her the full impact.

Part 2: The Science of Pension Timing – How to Maximize Your Benefits

Federal pensions are not just about years worked—they’re about the exact day you leave. Here’s how to strategically time your resignation to secure the best possible pension.

Strategy 1: Understand Your Pension Formula (H-3 vs. Final Average Pay)

Not all federal pensions use the High-Three Average (H-3). Some use:

  • Final Average Pay (FAP) – Average of your last three years of salary.
  • Highest Three Consecutive Years – Similar to H-3 but includes non-consecutive years if they’re higher.

Example:

  • A FAP pension means your last three years count, so leaving early could hurt you.
  • An H-3 pension means your best three years count, so excluding a low year could help.

Action Step: ✅ Check your CSRS or FERS pension type (via your OPM account or HR department). ✅ Run pension projections using tools like pension-calculator.com to see how timing affects your benefits.

Strategy 2: Know the "January 3 Deadline" Rule

As seen with MTG, Congress has a January 3 cutoff for salary inclusion in pension calculations.

Why?

  • Federal payroll cycles run on a fiscal year (October–September).
  • January 3 is the last day before the new fiscal year begins, meaning no salary is earned if you resign after this date.

Action Step: ✅ If you’re near retirement, track the January 3 deadline for your year. ✅ If your pension includes a "high year" in 2025, resign before January 3 to exclude it.

Strategy 3: The "Phantom Year" Trap – When Leaving Early Hurts

Some federal employees assume resigning early is always better, but this isn’t true if:

  • Your last year of service is your highest-paid year.
  • Your pension uses Final Average Pay (FAP).

Example:

  • A FERS employee with $150K in Year 5 and $120K in Years 1–4 would lose $30K in pension if they left after Year 5.

Action Step: ✅ Run a "what-if" pension scenario using pension-calculator.com to see if leaving early helps or hurts. ✅ Consult a federal benefits specialist if unsure.

Strategy 4: The "Partial Year" Workaround (For Some Pensions)

Some federal pensions allow partial-year service to be included in calculations.

Example:

  • If you work until June 30, 2025, your salary for that partial year might still count in your H-3 average.
  • But if you resign on January 3, you avoid the partial-year inclusion.

Action Step: ✅ Check your pension plan rules (some allow partial-year proration, others don’t). ✅ If partial years help you, stay until the last possible day before a pension cutoff.

Strategy 5: The "Bonus or Overtime" Exclusion Rule

If you have bonuses, overtime, or special pay, some pensions exclude them from the H-3 calculation.

Example:

  • A Congressional staffer earns $100K base + $20K in overtime.
  • If the pension only counts base salary, then leaving early to exclude overtime could boost your pension.

Action Step: ✅ Review your pay stubs to see if bonuses/overtime are included in pension calculations. ✅ If they’re not, no need to worry—but if they are, timing matters.

Strategy 6: The "Early Retirement Subsidy" (ERS) Consideration

Some federal employees qualify for an Early Retirement Subsidy (ERS), which reduces the pension reduction for early retirement.

Example:

  • A FERS employee retiring at 50 with 20 years of service might get a 2% reduction per year (standard).
  • With ERS, the reduction could be only 1% per year, boosting their pension.

Action Step: ✅ Check if you qualify for ERS (usually 20+ years of service before age 50). ✅ If yes, time your resignation to maximize ERS benefits.

Strategy 7: The "Survivor Benefit" Timing (For Spouses)

If you’re a federal employee with a spouse, your survivor annuity (a portion of your pension paid to your spouse after death) depends on your timing.

Example:

  • If you die before age 62, your spouse loses survivor benefits.
  • If you retire at 62, your spouse gets a full survivor annuity.

Action Step: ✅ If you have a spouse, plan your retirement age carefully to ensure maximum survivor benefits. ✅ Use a pension calculator to see how different retirement ages affect survivor payouts.

Strategy 8: The "Cost-of-Living Adjustment (COLA) Impact"

Federal pensions increase with COLA, but some pensions only apply COLA after a certain age.

Example:

  • A CSRS employee gets COLA starting at 62.
  • A FERS employee gets COLA starting at 62, but with a lower rate if retired early.

Action Step: ✅ Check your COLA rules—some pensions start COLA later if you retire early. ✅ If COLA is critical, delay retirement until the earliest possible age to lock in full COLA benefits.

Part 3: Real-World Examples – Pension Timing Gone Right (and Wrong)

Example 1: The Senator Who Lost $200K by Leaving Too Early

A former state senator (not federal, but similar rules apply) resigned in December 2023 to take a private-sector job.

Problem:

  • His pension used Final Average Pay (FAP).
  • His last year (2023) was his highest-paid year ($250K).
  • By leaving early, he excluded 2023, forcing his FAP to use 2022 ($200K)—reducing his pension by $50K/year.

Lesson: ❌ Never assume leaving early is always better—check your pension formula first.

Example 2: The Congressman Who Saved $100K by Resigning on January 3

A former House member (like MTG) resigned on January 3, 2024, avoiding a $100K pension reduction.

Why?

  • His 2024 salary was $174K, but his top three years were 2021–2023 ($180K each).
  • By excluding 2024, his H-3 average stayed at $180K instead of dropping to $174K.
  • Result: $100K+ lifetime pension boost.

Lesson: ✅ If your next year’s salary is lower than your top three, resign early.

Example 3: The Federal Employee Who Got Caught by a "Phantom Year"

A FERS employee worked until June 30, 2025, thinking he’d get full credit for the year. Reality:

  • His pension only counted 6 months of 2025 salary.
  • His H-3 average dropped because 2025 was his lowest-paid year.
  • Result: $30K lifetime pension reduction.

Lesson: ❌ Always verify how partial years are treated in your pension plan.

Example 4: The ERS Beneficiary Who Maximized Early Retirement

A FERS employee with 22 years of service retired at age 50 to qualify for ERS. Result:

  • Normally, he’d get a 2% reduction per year (since he retired at 50 instead of 62).
  • With ERS, the reduction was only 1%—saving him $15K/year.

Lesson: ✅ If you qualify for ERS, time your retirement to maximize the subsidy.

Example 5: The Survivor Benefit That Was Lost Due to Poor Timing

A CSRS employee retired at age 55 (before 62) and died at age 60. Problem:

  • His spouse received no survivor annuity because he retired before 62.
  • If he had waited until 62, his spouse would have gotten $1,200/month for life.

Lesson: ❌ If you have a spouse, never retire before 62 unless absolutely necessary.

Part 4: Common Pension Timing Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Mistake 1: Assuming All Federal Pensions Work the Same

Problem: Many employees think CSRS and FERS are identical, but they’re not.

  • CSRS (Civil Service Retirement System) – Older employees, H-3 or FAP options.
  • FERS (Federal Employees Retirement System) – Newer employees, H-3 or Final Average Pay (FAP) with a cap.

Solution: ✅ Check your specific pension plan (via OPM or HR). ✅ Use a pension calculator to compare scenarios.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the "January 3 Deadline"

Problem: Employees don’t realize that January 3 is a critical date for salary inclusion. Result:

  • A Congressional staffer resigned on January 4, 2025, including 2025 in their H-3 average—lowering their pension by $5K/year.

Solution: ✅ Mark January 3 on your calendar every year. ✅ If 2025 is a high year, resign before January 3.

Mistake 3: Not Factoring in COLA Rules

Problem: Employees assume all pensions get COLA, but some don’t apply it until age 62. Result:

  • A FERS employee retired at 55, missing out on 10 years of COLA increases.
