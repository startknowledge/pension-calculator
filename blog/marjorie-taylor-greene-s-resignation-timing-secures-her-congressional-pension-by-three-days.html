Understanding Congressional Pensions: The Rules That Govern Your Benefits

Before diving into MTG’s resignation strategy, it’s essential to grasp how congressional pensions differ from private-sector retirement plans.

1. The Congressional Pension System (CPS) vs. FERS

Most federal employees (outside Congress) are covered by the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS), which combines:

  • Basic Benefit Plan (BBP) – A defined-benefit pension
  • Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) – A defined-contribution 401(k)-style account
  • Social Security – Supplemental coverage

Congress, however, operates under its own system—the Congressional Pension System (CPS)—which is older, more generous, and subject to different rules.

2. Key Features of the Congressional Pension

  • No Social Security Integration: Unlike FERS, the CPS does not coordinate with Social Security. Your pension is standalone.
  • Vesting & Service Requirements: You must serve at least 5 years to be vested. Full retirement benefits require 20 years of service.
  • Final Average Salary (FAS): Your pension is based on your highest 3 years of salary (adjusted for inflation).
  • Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLAs): Pensions receive annual increases based on inflation (though recent years have seen debates over this).
  • Early Retirement Penalties: Leaving before age 62 (or after 20 years of service) can trigger reductions in your pension.

3. The Critical Deadline: Why MTG’s Resignation Timing Was Perfect

MTG’s resignation on January 3, 2023, wasn’t arbitrary. It was aligned with the federal fiscal year cutoff—specifically, the January 3 deadline that determines:

  • Pension eligibility (if you leave before this date, you may lose vesting rights).
  • Final pay calculations (your last three years of salary are frozen as of this date).
  • Separation benefits (some pensions require a minimum service period before benefits kick in).

For MTG, this meant: ✅ Avoiding a "break in service" that could have disqualified her from pension benefits. ✅ Locking in her highest-earning years (2021-2022) for her Final Average Salary (FAS) calculation. ✅ Preventing a "permanent loss"—had she resigned after January 3, she might have faced penalties or forfeiture of accrued benefits.
