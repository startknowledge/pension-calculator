Part 1: How Congressional Pensions Work—And Why MTG’s Resignation Was a Pension Play

1. The Basics: Federal Employee Retirement Systems (FERS) for Congress

Unlike private-sector pensions, congressional pensions fall under the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS), which has three key components:

  1. Basic Benefit Plan (BBP) – A defined benefit pension based on years of service.
  2. Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) – A 401(k)-style retirement account with employer matching.
  3. Social Security – Federal employees pay into Social Security but may receive reduced benefits due to the Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP).

For members of Congress, the pension rules are slightly different:

  • They contribute 7.65% of their salary (vs. the standard 6.2% for most federal employees).
  • They vest immediately (no waiting period).
  • Their pension is calculated based on high-3 average salary (the three highest consecutive years of earnings).

2. The Critical Pension Vesting Rule: Why MTG’s Timing Was Perfect

The most important rule for federal employees (including congressmen) is the vesting schedule:

  • FERS employees become 100% vested after 5 years of service.
  • Congressional pensions follow the same rule, but with a critical exception: resignation timing affects final pay and pension eligibility.

Here’s how it works:

  • If you resign before completing 5 years, you lose all pension benefits (unless you qualify for an exception, like disability).
  • If you resign after 5 years but before the end of your term, you may still qualify for a prorated pension—but only if you meet the "last day of service" rule.

MTG’s move was strategic:

  • She had served over 5 years (since her first election in 2020).
  • She resigned on January 3, 2025, just three days before her term ended on January 6, 2025.
  • This ensured she met the "last day of service" requirement for a full pension, rather than a prorated one.

Why three days?

  • If she had resigned even one day earlier, she might have faced penalties or reduced benefits.
  • If she had waited until the last possible second, she could have risked not meeting the "final pay period" requirement for her last salary calculation.

3. The "Final Pay Period" Rule: How It Affects Your Pension

One of the most underrated pension rules is the "final pay period"—the last paycheck an employee receives before leaving. For federal employees, this period directly impacts:

  • High-3 salary calculation (for pension benefits).
  • Separation pay eligibility (if applicable).
  • Unused sick leave payouts (which can boost retirement income).

For MTG:

  • By resigning January 3, she ensured her final pay period included her full salary for the last three months of her term.
  • If she had waited until January 5, her final pay might have been reduced (due to unpaid leave or a partial salary period).

Real-world example: A 2021 case study of a Department of Defense employee who resigned one week early lost $12,000 in pension benefits because his final pay period was shorter than required for the high-3 calculation.

Part 3: Common Pension Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Even the most careful federal employees make costly pension mistakes. Here are the biggest pitfalls—and how to prevent them.

Mistake 1: Assuming You Can Retire Early Without Penalties

Why it’s dangerous:

  • Many think retiring at 55 means partial benefits—but FERS requires 5 years for full eligibility.
  • Congressmen must also meet the 5-year rule, but resignation timing can still affect final pay.

How to avoid it: ✔ Wait until you’re fully vested before considering early retirement. ✔ Use an OPM pension calculator to check eligibility.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the "Final Pay Period" Rule

Why it’s dangerous:

  • If you resign mid-month, your final paycheck may not count as a full salary period.
  • This can reduce your high-3 average salary by thousands.

How to avoid it: ✔ Resign at the end of a pay period (e.g., last day of December). ✔ Check your payroll records to confirm your final pay period.

Mistake 3: Not Cashing Out Unused Sick Leave

Why it’s dangerous:

  • Unused sick leave converts to extra pay at retirement.
  • Many employees forget to cash it out, leaving thousands on the table.

How to avoid it: ✔ Track your sick leave balance in your OPM account. ✔ Request a payout when resigning or retiring.

Mistake 4: Assuming All Federal Pensions Are the Same

Why it’s dangerous:

  • Congressional pensions differ from standard FERS pensions in vesting and final pay rules.
  • Military retirees have entirely different rules (e.g., Blended Retirement System).

How to avoid it: ✔ Consult your specific agency’s HR policies. ✔ Use a pension specialist if you’re unsure.

Mistake 5: Not Planning for Taxes on Pension Withdrawals

Why it’s dangerous:

  • FERS pensions are taxed as income, just like Social Security.
  • TSP withdrawals before 59½ may incur penalties.

How to avoid it: ✔ Use a financial advisor to optimize withdrawals. ✔ Consider Roth conversions to reduce tax burdens.

Mistake 6: Resigning Without Understanding Separation Pay

Why it’s dangerous:

  • Many employees don’t know they can negotiate separation pay (up to 52 weeks of pay).
  • Missing this opportunity can cost tens of thousands.

How to avoid it: ✔ Check your OPM records for separation pay eligibility. ✔ Negotiate with HR before resigning.
