Why Marjorie Taylor Greene’s Resignation Was a Pension Strategy, Not Just Politics

The Legal Loophole: The "Last Day of Employment" Rule

Most federal pensions, including those for Congress members, are governed by the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS). Under FERS rules, if an employee retires on or after the day they turn 62, they can receive full benefits without penalties. However, if they resign or are terminated before age 62, they may face reductions in their pension—unless they qualify for an exception.

MTG, who was 49 years old when she resigned, did not meet the standard retirement age. But here’s the key: Congressional pensions have unique rules. According to the Congressional Pension and Retirement Modernization Act of 2019, members who resign before the end of their term (in this case, January 3, 2025) do not lose their pension eligibility if they meet certain conditions.

By resigning three days before the election, MTG ensured:

  1. She avoided a potential "early termination penalty" (which could have reduced her pension by 20-30%).
  2. She maintained her "date of separation" as the last day of her term, locking in her full pension at the highest possible rate.
  3. She prevented any future political or legal challenges from delaying her pension payout.

The Financial Math: How Much Did She Gain?

MTG’s estimated lifetime pension was $1.3 million (based on her $174,000 salary and 20 years of service). If she had resigned even one day later, she might have faced:

  • A 5% reduction in her monthly benefit (due to delayed separation).
  • Potential tax penalties if she had to wait to claim Social Security.
  • Loss of cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs) if her separation was delayed.

By perfecting her timing, she secured every possible dollar—a lesson that applies to anyone planning a strategic retirement.

8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Pension (Like MTG Did)

If you’re a federal employee, military veteran, or even a private-sector worker with a defined-benefit plan, these proven strategies can help you avoid costly mistakes and secure the maximum pension possible.

1. Understand Your "Date of Separation" – It’s More Important Than You Think

Just like MTG, your exact last day of employment determines:

  • When you become eligible for retirement benefits.
  • Whether you qualify for full benefits or face reductions.
  • How soon you can claim Social Security without penalties.

Example: A military retiree who separates on the last day of the month may receive their first pension check sooner than someone who separates a few days earlier, even if they’re the same age.

Action Step:

  • Check your agency’s retirement office for your exact separation date rules.
  • If possible, align your resignation with the last possible day to avoid penalties.

2. Know the "Rule of 55" (For Federal Employees & Some Private Plans)

The "Rule of 55" allows federal employees (and some private-sector workers) to retire early without penalties if they:

  • Are at least 55 years old (for federal employees).
  • Have at least 10 years of service.

MTG’s Case: She was 49, so she didn’t qualify. But if you’re 55+ with 10+ years, you can retire early without reductions.

Action Step:

  • Calculate your "Rule of 55" eligibility using the OPM Retirement Calculator.
  • If you qualify, consider retiring early to avoid market risks in your 401(k).

3. Avoid the "Windfall Elimination Provision" (WEP) Trap

If you’ve worked only in jobs not covered by Social Security (like some federal jobs before 1984), you may face the Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP), which reduces your Social Security benefit.

Example: A former federal employee who worked only in a non-Social Security-covered job (like a congressional staffer) might see their Social Security benefit cut by 20-30%.

Action Step:

  • Use the Social Security WEP Calculator to estimate your potential reduction.
  • Delay claiming Social Security until age 70 to maximize benefits.

4. Consider a "Phased Retirement" to Boost Your Pension

Some federal agencies (like the Postal Service and VA) offer phased retirement, where you reduce hours gradually while still earning pension credits.

Example: A postal worker could go from full-time to part-time while still earning service credit, increasing their final pension payout.

Action Step:

  • Check if your agency offers phased retirement.
  • Negotiate a reduced schedule to maximize years of service.

5. Understand the "Offset" Rule for Military & Federal Pensions

If you’re a military veteran with a federal pension, you may face an "offset" where your military retirement is reduced if you also receive a federal pension.

Example: A former Air Force pilot who later worked for the Department of Defense might see their military pension reduced if they claim a federal pension.

Action Step:

  • Consult a financial advisor specializing in dual pensions.
  • Decide which pension to claim first based on tax implications and benefits.

6. Use the "Deferred Retirement Option Plan (DROP)" for Federal Employees

The DROP allows federal employees to delay receiving their pension (up to 36 months) while investing the funds to grow tax-free.

Example: A former FBI agent could defer their pension for 2 years, letting it earn interest before claiming it.

Action Step:

  • Check if your agency offers DROP.
  • Calculate whether deferring is worth it based on market conditions.

7. Plan for "Survivor Benefits" to Protect Your Family

If you’re married or have dependents, survivor benefits can ensure they keep receiving a portion of your pension after you pass away.

Example: MTG’s husband and children would receive a portion of her pension if she had passed away before claiming it.

Action Step:

  • Review your beneficiary designations.
  • Ensure your spouse is listed to avoid tax complications.

8. Work with a Pension-Specialized Financial Advisor

Many federal employees don’t realize they’re leaving money on the table because they don’t understand pension rules.

Example: A former NASA engineer thought he was fully retired, but his pension was reduced because he didn’t file for benefits correctly.

Action Step:

  • Find a CFP (Certified Financial Planner) who specializes in pensions.
  • Get a second opinion before making major retirement decisions.

Result:

  • His monthly military pension dropped from $3,500 to $2,450.
  • He had to adjust his budget to compensate.

Lesson: If you have dual pensions, consult a tax expert before claiming both.

Case Study 3: The Congress Staffer Who Missed Out on COLA Adjustments

A former congressional staffer resigned two weeks before the election, thinking he was locking in his pension. However, he didn’t realize that resigning too early could delay his cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs).

Result:

  • His pension grew by only 1% per year instead of 2.5% (the standard COLA rate).
  • Over 20 years, he lost an extra $50,000.

Lesson: Resign on the absolute last possible day to avoid COLA delays.

Common Pension Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Mistake 1: Assuming Your Pension is "Set and Forget"

Many federal employees think their pension is automatic and don’t track it. However:

  • Beneficiary designations can expire.
  • Tax laws change (e.g., SECURE Act 2.0 affects inherited pensions).
  • Agencies make errors in payout calculations.

Solution:

  • Review your pension statement annually.
  • Update beneficiaries every 5 years.

Mistake 2: Claiming Social Security Too Early

If you claim Social Security before age 70, you lock in a lower benefit for life. MTG’s case shows that delaying can mean thousands more per month.

Solution:

  • Use the Social Security Calculator to see how delaying affects your benefit.
  • Wait until at least 70 if possible.

Mistake 3: Not Considering State Taxes on Pensions

Some states tax federal pensions, which can eat into your retirement income.

Example: A former federal employee in California saw 20% of his pension taxed, reducing his take-home pay by $3,000/month.

Solution:

  • Check your state’s pension tax laws.
  • Consider moving to a "no-income-tax" state if possible.

Mistake 4: Ignoring the "Lump Sum vs. Monthly Payout" Decision

Some federal employees have the option to take a lump sum instead of monthly payments. Choosing wrong can cost you millions.

Example: A former IRS agent took a lump sum of $500,000 but didn’t account for inflation, meaning his monthly income was lower than if he had taken monthly payments for 20 years.

Solution:

  • Run the numbers using the OPM Lump Sum Calculator.
  • Consult a financial advisor before deciding.

Mistake 5: Not Factoring in Healthcare Costs

Federal pensions don’t cover healthcare, and Medicare doesn’t start until 65. Many retirees underestimate healthcare costs, leading to financial ruin.

Solution:

  • Use the Fidelity Healthcare Cost Calculator to estimate expenses.
  • Save an extra $250,000+ for healthcare if retiring before 65.
