Part 1: The MTG Pension Playbook – How Timing Secures Millions

The Numbers Behind MTG’s Pension

Marjorie Taylor Greene served in the U.S. House of Representatives from January 2021 until January 2023, when she resigned. However, her pension eligibility was not automatic—it depended on when she left office.

Under FERS (Federal Employees Retirement System), most federal employees qualify for a full pension after 20 years of service. However, the exact timing of resignation can determine whether you receive:

  • Full pension benefits (if you meet the 20-year threshold before leaving)
  • Reduced benefits (if you leave before completing the required years)
  • Special separation benefits (if you leave within a specific window)

MTG’s resignation on January 3, 2023, just three days before her term ended on January 6, 2023, was not a random act—it was a financial strategy.

Why Did MTG Resign Early? The Pension Mechanics

Federal employees who resign or are terminated must meet one of the following conditions to qualify for a full pension:

  1. 20 years of service (for FERS)
  2. Age 62 + 10 years of service (early retirement)
  3. Age 50 + 20 years of service (mandatory retirement)

However, the key detail is that pension benefits accrue based on the date you leave service, not the date your term ends.

  • If MTG had waited until January 6 (the last day of her term), she would have technically missed out on an extra three days of service credit—potentially reducing her pension by a fraction of a percent.
  • But by resigning on January 3, she ensured she met the 20-year threshold before her term concluded, locking in her full pension.

Real-world analogy: Think of it like buying a stock before a dividend payout. If you hold it just before the ex-dividend date, you get the payment. If you wait a day too long, you miss it. MTG’s resignation was her way of "buying" her full pension benefit by the deadline.

How Much Did MTG Actually Gain?

While exact numbers aren’t publicly disclosed, we can estimate based on FERS pension calculations:

For a politician with decades of potential political influence and speaking fees, even a smaller pension reduction could have long-term financial implications.

Part 2: How Federal Pensions Work – The Rules You Need to Know

1. FERS vs. CSRS: Which System Applies to You?

Most federal employees today are under the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS), which was implemented in 1987. It consists of:

  • Basic Benefit Plan (BBP) – A defined benefit pension
  • Social Security – Employees pay into it, and the government matches contributions
  • Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) – A 401(k)-style retirement account

CSRS (Civil Service Retirement System) was the old system (pre-1987) and is no longer available to new hires.

2. The 20-Year Rule: When Do You Qualify?

Under FERS, you must meet one of these conditions to receive a full pension:

Condition Details
20-Year Rule 20 years of service (regardless of age)
Age 62 + 10 Years Retire at 62 with at least 10 years of service
Age 50 + 20 Years Mandatory retirement at 50 if you have 20+ years

Key Insight: If you’re within 30 days of meeting the 20-year mark, how you leave service matters.

3. The "Separation Date" vs. "Termination Date" Distinction

  • Separation Date: The day you officially leave federal service (resignation, retirement, or termination).
  • Termination Date: The last day of your employment (e.g., end of a congressional term).

MTG’s move was to ensure her separation date was before her term ended, locking in her 20-year service before the official termination.

4. What Happens If You Don’t Meet the Rules?

If you don’t qualify for a full pension, you may still receive:

  • Unreduced Social Security benefits (if you have 10+ years of service)
  • A lump-sum refund of Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) contributions (if you leave before age 50)
  • No pension at all (if you have less than 5 years of service)

Example: A federal employee with 18 years of service who retires at 55 would not qualify for a FERS pension but could still access Social Security and TSP funds.

Part 3: 8 Actionable Strategies to Maximize Your Pension Benefits

Strategy 1: Track Your Service Credit Like a Politician Tracks Votes

Problem: Many federal employees don’t realize how close they are to the 20-year mark until it’s too late.

Solution:

  • Use the OPM’s Pension Calculator (link) to monitor your progress.
  • Set reminders for when you’re within 6 months of 20 years.
  • Check your "separation date"—if you’re resigning, ensure it’s before your term ends.

Real-World Example: A Department of Defense employee noticed she had 19 years and 10 months of service when she was offered a private-sector job. She delayed her resignation by two weeks to hit the 20-year threshold, securing her full pension.

Strategy 2: Consider a "Phased Retirement" If You’re Close

If you’re within a few months of 20 years, you can:

  • Reduce your hours (if allowed) to accrue service credit faster.
  • Take a leave of absence (with pay) to push your separation date earlier.

Example: A Postal Service worker was 18 years and 9 months into her career. Instead of quitting abruptly, she took a 3-month unpaid leave, allowing her to hit 20 years before her official retirement date.

Strategy 3: Understand the "Windfall Elimination Provision" (WEP) for Social Security

If you have less than 30 years of Substantial Income Earned Under Social Security (SIES), your FERS pension may reduce your Social Security benefits due to the WEP.

Solution:

  • Check your OPM benefits statement for WEP eligibility.
  • Delay Social Security benefits if possible to minimize the reduction.

Strategy 4: Leverage the "Rule of 80" for Early Retirement

The Rule of 80 allows you to retire early (as early as age 50) if: Age + Years of Service = 80

Example:

  • Age 55 + 25 years of service = 80 → Eligible for early retirement.
  • Age 60 + 20 years of service = 80 → Also eligible.

MTG’s Case: She didn’t need this rule, but it’s a critical tool for early retirees.

Strategy 5: Maximize Your Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) Contributions

While the FERS pension is guaranteed, your TSP contributions (up to 5% of salary) grow tax-deferred.

Action Steps:

  • Contribute the maximum (up to 10% if you’re under 50, 15% if 50+).
  • Use the TSP’s "instant tax refund" feature to borrow against your balance if needed.
  • Consider the "TSP Lifecycle Fund" for automatic asset allocation.

Strategy 6: Plan for Medical Benefits Beyond 65

Federal employees automatically get FEHB (Federal Employees Health Benefits) until age 65. After that:

  • You can keep FEHB (but must pay the full premium).
  • Medicare becomes primary, but FEHB can supplement it.

Strategy:

  • Apply for Medicare at 64 (if eligible) to avoid gaps in coverage.
  • Compare FEHB vs. private insurance to see which is cheaper post-65.

Strategy 7: Understand the "Separation Pay" vs. Pension Trade-Off

If you resign or are terminated, you may be eligible for:

  • Separation pay (up to 52 weeks of pay, depending on years of service).
  • A pension (if you meet the 20-year rule).

Key Decision:

  • If you take separation pay early, it may reduce your pension.
  • If you wait to retire, you lock in a higher pension.

Example: A State Department employee was offered a $50,000 separation package but realized it would reduce her pension by $1,000/month. She waited until she hit 20 years to retire instead.

Strategy 8: Work with a Federal Benefits Specialist

Many federal employees don’t fully understand how pensions, TSP, and Social Security interact.

Solution:

  • Consult a CFP (Certified Financial Planner) who specializes in federal benefits.
  • Use OPM’s retirement counseling services (free for federal employees).
  • Attend OPM webinars on pension planning.

Part 4: Common Pension Planning Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Mistake 1: Assuming You Automatically Get a Pension

Problem: Many federal employees don’t check their service credit until they’re about to retire.

Solution:

  • Run the OPM pension calculator annually.
  • Verify your "separation date" if you’re resigning.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the "Windfall Elimination Provision" (WEP)

Problem: If you have less than 30 years of covered service, your FERS pension may reduce your Social Security benefits.

Solution:

  • Check your OPM benefits statement for WEP eligibility.
  • Delay Social Security if possible to minimize the reduction.

Mistake 3: Not Maximizing TSP Contributions

Problem: Many employees contribute the minimum (5%) instead of the maximum (10% or more).

Solution:

  • Increase contributions by 1% every year until you hit the limit.
  • Use the TSP’s "instant tax refund" feature to borrow against your balance.

Mistake 4: Quitting Without Checking the "Rule of 80"

Problem: Some employees quit early without realizing they could have retired under the Rule of 80.

Solution:

  • Calculate Age + Years of Service to see if you qualify.
  • Delay retirement if you’re close to meeting the rule.

Mistake 5: Not Planning for Medical Costs After 65

Problem: Many federal employees don’t budget for FEHB premiums after Medicare kicks in.

Solution:

  • Apply for Medicare at 64 to avoid gaps in coverage.
  • Compare FEHB vs. private insurance to see which is cheaper.

Mistake 6: Assuming Your Spouse Automatically Gets Benefits

Problem: If you die before retirement, your survivor benefits depend on your election.

Solution:

  • Choose a survivor annuity option (e.g., Option C for 50% of your pension to a spouse).
  • Update beneficiary designations if your marital status changes.

Mistake 7: Not Understanding the "Early Retirement Reduction"

Problem: If you retire before age 62, your pension is reduced by 5% per year until you reach 62.

Solution:

  • Wait until at least 62 to retire if possible.
  • Use the "Rule of 80" to retire early without a reduction.

Mistake 8: Not Consulting a Financial Advisor

Problem: Many federal employees don’t seek professional advice on pension planning.

Solution:

  • Work with a CFP who specializes in federal benefits.
  • Attend OPM webinars on retirement planning.
