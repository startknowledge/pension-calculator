How Long Will My Money Last in Retirement? The Ultimate Guide to Stretching Your Savings

Retirement is one of life’s most exciting milestones—but also one of its most daunting financial challenges. With rising healthcare costs, inflation, and longer life expectancies, many retirees wonder: Will my money truly last? According to the 2023 Retirement Confidence Survey by the Employee Benefit Research Institute (EBRI), only 58% of workers feel very or somewhat confident about having enough savings for retirement. Meanwhile, the Social Security Administration (SSA) reports that the average retiree receives just $1,905 per month in Social Security benefits—a far cry from covering all living expenses.

If you’re asking yourself, “How long will my money last in retirement?”—you’re not alone. The good news? With the right strategies, careful planning, and a retirement calculator, you can stretch your savings further than you think. This guide will break down:

  • How to use a retirement calculator to estimate your financial runway
  • 8 actionable strategies to make your money last longer
  • Real-world examples of retirees who stretched their savings successfully
  • Common mistakes that drain retirement funds prematurely
  • FAQs with expert-backed answers

By the end, you’ll have a clear roadmap to ensure your golden years remain golden—not a financial nightmare.

Why Your Retirement Money Might Not Last as Long as You Think

Before diving into solutions, it’s essential to understand the key factors that determine how long your money will last in retirement:

1. Rising Costs Outpace Savings

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) for all urban consumers rose 3.4% in 2023 (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics), meaning your dollar buys less each year. Healthcare costs alone have increased by over 50% since 2010, according to the Health Research & Educational Trust (HRET). If you retire at 65, you could spend $300,000 or more on healthcare in retirement—without long-term care.

2. Sequence of Returns Risk

The order in which you earn investment returns can drastically affect your portfolio. A bad market year early in retirement (e.g., 2008 or 2022) can deplete your savings faster than expected. A 2021 study by Vanguard found that retirees who experienced a 10% drop in their portfolio within the first five years of retirement had a 30% higher chance of running out of money compared to those with steady growth.

3. Underestimating Longevity

People are living longer than ever. The average life expectancy at age 65 is now 84.3 years (CDC, 2023). But 1 in 4 Americans will live past 90, and 1 in 10 will reach 95. If you retire at 65, you may need savings to last 25-30 years—or even longer.

4. Social Security Isn’t Enough

The average monthly Social Security benefit in 2024 is $1,905—far below the $1,700–$2,500 many retirees need for basic living expenses (AARP, 2023). Without additional income, most retirees face a shortfall of $10,000–$30,000 per year.

5. The 4% Rule May Not Be Enough Anymore

The traditional 4% rule (withdrawing 4% annually and adjusting for inflation) was based on historical data. However, lower interest rates and higher inflation mean some financial planners now recommend 3.5% or even 3% to ensure longevity. A 2023 study by Morningstar found that only 60% of retirees following the 4% rule would have enough left after 30 years.

How to Calculate: How Long Will My Money Last in Retirement?

Before implementing strategies, you need a clear baseline. A retirement calculator is your best tool—but not all calculators are created equal. Here’s how to use one effectively.

Step 1: Choose the Right Retirement Calculator

Not all calculators account for inflation, healthcare costs, or market volatility. Look for one that includes: ✅ Inflation adjustments (critical for long-term sustainability) ✅ Healthcare cost estimates (Medicare premiums, long-term care) ✅ Sequence of returns risk (simulates worst-case market scenarios) ✅ Tax implications (especially for withdrawals from tax-advantaged accounts)

  • Fidelity’s Retirement Score (AI-driven analysis)
  • T. Rowe Price Retirement Income Calculator (accounts for variable spending)

Step 2: Input Your Key Financial Numbers

A good calculator will ask for:

  1. Current savings & investments (401(k), IRA, brokerage accounts, real estate)
  2. Annual income needed in retirement (break it down: housing, food, healthcare, travel, etc.)
  3. Expected Social Security benefits (use the SSA calculator)
  4. Pension income (if applicable)
  5. Annual spending adjustments (will you spend less in retirement? More?)
  6. Life expectancy & spouse’s age (if applicable)
  7. Expected investment returns (historically ~7% for stocks, but adjust for risk tolerance)
  8. Healthcare costs (Medicare Part B premiums, supplemental insurance, long-term care)

Step 3: Run Multiple Scenarios

Don’t just rely on one estimate. Test:

  • Best-case scenario (strong market returns, low inflation)
  • Worst-case scenario (market crash in Year 1, high inflation)
  • Moderate scenario (average returns, moderate spending)

Example: A couple with $1.2 million in savings, $60,000 annual spending, and a 65-year-old wife with a life expectancy of 90:

  • Best case (7% returns): Money lasts 35+ years
  • Worst case (4% returns, high inflation): Money lasts 22 years
  • Moderate case (5.5% returns): Money lasts 28 years

This shows why having a buffer (extra savings) is crucial.

Step 4: Adjust Based on Your Results

If your calculator shows a shortfall, don’t panic. Instead: ✔ Increase savings rate (if still working) ✔ Delay retirement (even a year can add $100K+ to Social Security) ✔ Reduce expenses (downsize, cut discretionary spending) ✔ Optimize withdrawals (use the 4% rule or dynamic withdrawal strategy)

8 Actionable Strategies to Make Your Retirement Money Last Longer

Now that you’ve run the numbers, here’s how to stretch your savings so they last decades.

1. Follow the 4% Rule (But Adjust for Your Situation)

The 4% rule (first proposed by Trinity Study, 1998) suggests withdrawing 4% of your portfolio annually, adjusted for inflation. However:

  • If you have high healthcare costs, consider 3.5%.
  • If you’re risk-averse, 3% may be safer.
  • If you have a long-term care policy, you might afford 4.5%.

Real-World Example: John and Mary, both 67, retired with $1.5 million. They used the 4% rule:

  • Year 1: Withdraw $60,000
  • Year 5: Inflation-adjusted withdrawal = $67,200
  • Year 15: Withdrawal = $85,000
  • Year 30: Portfolio still has $700,000 left (assuming 6% average returns)

Pro Tip: Use a dynamic withdrawal strategy—if the market does well, take less; if it struggles, take more (but cap at 5% max).

2. Delay Social Security to Maximize Benefits

Every year you delay Social Security beyond full retirement age (FRA, usually 66-67) increases your benefit by 8% per year until age 70.

Example:

  • FRA benefit: $2,000/month
  • Delay to 70: $2,560/month (32% increase)
  • Extra annual income: $6,720—enough to cover $80,000+ over 12 years

When to Claim?

  • Claim at 62? Only if you must (benefits drop ~30%).
  • Claim at FRA? Good if you need income but want balance.
  • Claim at 70? Best if you have other income sources (pension, investments).

3. Downsize or Move to a Lower-Cost Area

Housing is often the biggest expense in retirement. AARP estimates that homeowners spend 25-30% of their budget on housing—far more than renters.

Strategies: ✅ Downsize to a smaller home (free up equity for income) ✅ Move to a lower-cost state (Florida, Texas, Arizona offer no state income tax) ✅ Consider a reverse mortgage (if you own your home outright, this provides tax-free income) ✅ Rent out a room (or part of your home) for extra cash

Real-World Example: *Sarah and Tom lived in a $700K home in California, paying $5,000/month in mortgage + taxes. After downsizing to a $300K condo in Arizona (no state tax), their housing cost dropped to $1,800/month. The extra $3,200/month ($38,400/year) stretched their $1.2M portfolio by 5+ years.

4. Use Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Strategies

Withdrawing from the wrong accounts first can waste thousands in taxes. Here’s the optimal order:

  1. Taxable brokerage accounts (first, since you’ve already paid taxes)
  2. Roth IRA (tax-free growth—best for high earners)
  3. Traditional IRA/401(k) (taxed as ordinary income)
  4. Social Security (strategic claiming can reduce tax burden)

Pro Tip: If you’re in a low tax bracket, consider converting traditional IRA to Roth (if eligible) to avoid future tax hikes.

5. Generate Passive Income Streams

Relying solely on withdrawals can be risky. Passive income (earned without active work) can reduce required withdrawals and protect against market downturns.

Best Passive Income Ideas for Retirees: 🔹 Dividend stocks (S&P 500 stocks yield ~1.5-2%, but high-dividend stocks like Realty Income pay 5-6%). 🔹 Rental income (if you own property, consider renting it out). 🔹 Annuities (guaranteed income for life, but check fees carefully). 🔹 Peer-to-peer lending (platforms like LendingClub pay 4-8%). 🔹 Affiliate marketing or royalties (if you have a skill or intellectual property).

Example: Dave retired with $800K and built a dividend portfolio yielding 3.5% annually ($28,000/year). Instead of withdrawing $40,000 (4% of $1M), he only needed to withdraw $12,000, preserving his principal longer.

6. Cut Expenses Without Sacrificing Quality

You don’t need to live like a pauper—just optimize spending to make your money go further.

Smart Cuts: ✔ Healthcare:

  • Use Medicare Advantage plans (often include dental/vision).
  • Buy generic drugs (save 30-50% vs. brand names).
  • Consider HSA contributions (triple tax-advantaged if you have a high-deductible plan). ✔ Housing:
  • House hack (rent out a room or basement).
  • Age in place (modify home for accessibility instead of moving). ✔ Travel & Lifestyle:
  • Use senior discounts (AARP offers 1,000+ discounts).
  • Travel off-season (flights, hotels, and cruises are 30-50% cheaper).
  • Volunteer (some organizations provide free housing/food in exchange for work).

Example: *Ellen and Jim spent $5,000/month in retirement. By switching to a Medicare Advantage plan, downsizing, and using AARP discounts, they reduced costs to $3,500/month—adding $20,000/year to their runway.

7. Consider a Part-Time Job or Side Hustle

Many retirees underestimate how much they enjoy working. A part-time job or side hustle can:

  • Delay Social Security claiming (if you’re still earning).
  • Provide taxable income (helping you avoid high tax brackets on withdrawals).
  • Keep you mentally sharp (linked to lower dementia risk).

Best Side Hustles for Retirees: 🔸 Freelancing (writing, consulting, graphic design). 🔸 Teaching or tutoring (online or in-person). 🔸 Renting out a car (Turo, Getaround). 🔸 Selling handmade goods (Etsy, local markets). 🔸 Pet sitting/dog walking (Rover, Wag).

Example: *Bob retired at 65 with $900K but felt bored. He started freelance writing and earned $20,000/year, reducing his required withdrawals from $36,000 to $16,000. This extended his money by 8+ years.
