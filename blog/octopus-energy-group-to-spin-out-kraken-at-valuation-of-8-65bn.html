Why Octopus Energy Is Spinning Out Kraken: The Strategic Breakdown

1. The Rise of Octopus Energy: From Startup to Energy Giant

Octopus Energy, founded in 2015, has grown into a UK-based renewable energy leader, serving over 2 million customers and generating 100% renewable electricity for its domestic clients. However, its ambitions extend far beyond the UK—with expansions into Europe, the US, and Asia, the company has positioned itself as a global player in the energy transition.

Yet, despite its rapid growth, Octopus faced a critical challenge: diversification vs. focus. Its portfolio included:

  • Retail energy supply (Octopus Energy)
  • Wholesale energy trading (Kraken Energy)
  • Renewable energy development (solar, wind, battery storage)
  • EV charging infrastructure (Octopus Electric Vehicles)

This multi-faceted approach meant that while some divisions thrived, others struggled to scale efficiently. Enter Kraken Energy—a wholesale energy trading arm that handles large-scale power purchases, carbon markets, and energy trading for corporates and utilities.

2. The Kraken Spin-Off: A $8.65bn Valuation Explained

The decision to spin out Kraken isn’t just about financial optimization—it’s about strategic clarity. Here’s why:

A. Unlocking Shareholder Value Through Focus

Octopus Energy’s core retail business (serving households and SMEs) and its renewable energy projects (wind, solar, batteries) are high-growth, long-term plays. However, wholesale energy trading (Kraken) operates in a different rhythm—driven by short-term market fluctuations, geopolitical risks, and carbon pricing volatility.

By spinning out Kraken, Octopus can: ✔ Concentrate on its retail and renewable core, improving operational efficiency. ✔ Allow Kraken to raise capital independently, funding its high-risk, high-reward trading strategies. ✔ Create a standalone entity that can attract private equity, hedge funds, and institutional investors who specialize in energy markets.

B. The $8.65bn Valuation: What Does It Really Mean?

At first glance, $8.65 billion seems like an enormous number—but let’s dissect it:

  • Kraken’s revenue in 2023: Estimated at £1.2bn (~$1.5bn) (Financial Times, 2024).
  • Enterprise value (EV) multiple: ~5.5x revenue—a premium to traditional energy traders but justified by:
    • Dominance in UK wholesale markets (Kraken controls ~30% of the UK’s energy trading volume).
    • Strong ESG credentials (Kraken is a leader in carbon offsetting and renewable energy procurement).
    • Scalability in Europe and beyond (expansion into Germany, Netherlands, and US markets).

Comparison to Peers:

  • Shell’s trading arm (Shell Energy Trading): Valued at ~£10bn but operates in a more mature market.
  • EDF’s wholesale division: Valued at €5bn ($5.5bn), but with less focus on renewables.

Why the Premium? Kraken isn’t just a traditional energy trader—it’s a green energy enabler. Its ability to procure renewable power at scale, manage carbon markets, and support corporate sustainability goals makes it more attractive to ESG-focused investors.

C. The Timing: Why Now?

Several factors make this spin-off perfectly timed:

  1. Post-Ukraine War Energy Market Stability – After the 2022 energy crisis, markets have stabilized, making Kraken’s trading operations less volatile.
  2. Rising Renewable Energy Cost Parity – Solar and wind are now cheaper than fossil fuels in many regions, reducing Kraken’s reliance on volatile gas prices.
  3. Institutional Demand for Green Energy Assets – Pension funds and sovereign wealth funds are rushing to allocate capital to ESG-linked assets.
  4. Regulatory Tailwinds – The UK’s Net Zero Strategy and EU Green Deal provide long-term clarity for energy traders.

  • Octopus Energy (Retail + Renewables) will retain its high-growth assets, allowing it to focus on customer acquisition and renewable expansion.
  • Kraken’s spin-off will generate fresh capital for Octopus, which can be reinvested in battery storage, EV charging, and international markets.
  • Dual-class share structure? Not yet confirmed, but if implemented, existing Octopus shareholders may receive Kraken shares, creating dual exposure.

Potential Upside:

  • Octopus Energy’s stock could see a short-term boost as investors recognize the strategic clarity.
  • Long-term growth in retail renewables and EV infrastructure remains intact.

2. For Pension Funds & Institutional Investors: A New ESG Play

Pension funds (especially in the UK, Netherlands, and Scandinavia) are heavily invested in ESG assets. Kraken’s spin-off presents: ✅ Direct exposure to wholesale renewable energy markets (a growing sector). ✅ Carbon credit trading opportunities (Kraken is a leader in voluntary carbon markets). ✅ Diversification away from traditional utilities (which are facing declining margins).

3. For Retail Energy Consumers: Will Prices Drop?

One of the biggest questions is: Will this spin-off lead to lower energy bills for households?

Short Answer: Possibly, but not immediately.

Why?

  • Kraken’s wholesale trading efficiency could reduce procurement costs for Octopus Energy’s retail arm.
  • However, regulatory and market conditions (e.g., capacity charges, network costs) still play a major role in pricing.

Long-Term Impact:

  • If Kraken successfully scales renewable energy procurement, Octopus could offer more competitive green tariffs.
  • Battery storage integration (another Octopus strength) could further smooth out price volatility.
