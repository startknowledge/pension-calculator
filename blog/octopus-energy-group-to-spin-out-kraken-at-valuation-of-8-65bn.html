Why Octopus Energy is Spinning Out Kraken: A Strategic Breakdown

1. The Growth of Kraken: A Battery Storage Powerhouse

Kraken, a subsidiary of Octopus Energy, has emerged as one of the fastest-growing battery storage companies in Europe. Since its inception, Kraken has focused on developing, owning, and operating grid-scale battery projects, which play a critical role in storing excess renewable energy (from wind and solar) and releasing it during peak demand periods.

Key statistics on Kraken’s growth:

  • 2021: Kraken secured £1.5bn in funding for its first major battery storage projects.
  • 2022: The company tripled its capacity to 1.5GW, making it one of the largest battery storage developers in the UK.
  • 2023: Kraken announced plans to expand into Europe, with projects in Germany, Spain, and the Netherlands.
  • Valuation: The $8.65bn spin-off valuation suggests Kraken is now worth more than Octopus Energy’s original valuation before the separation.

Why is Kraken so valuable?

  • Government support: The UK’s Contract for Difference (CfD) scheme provides long-term revenue guarantees for battery storage projects.
  • Grid flexibility: As renewable energy penetration increases, battery storage is essential for grid stability, reducing reliance on fossil fuel peaker plants.
  • Scalability: Kraken’s modular approach allows for rapid deployment of new projects, making it a high-growth asset class.

2. Octopus Energy’s Strategic Shift: Focusing on Retail and Innovation

While Kraken is a high-growth, capital-intensive asset, Octopus Energy’s core business has always been retail energy provision, smart meters, and EV charging infrastructure. By spinning out Kraken, Octopus Energy can:

  • Reduce debt burden: Battery storage projects require heavy upfront capital, which may have been straining Octopus’s balance sheet.
  • Improve shareholder returns: Kraken’s independent valuation allows investors to focus on Octopus’s retail growth without the volatility of energy asset fluctuations.
  • Accelerate innovation: With Kraken operating autonomously, Octopus can prioritize digital transformation, customer experience, and EV infrastructure without being bogged down by energy asset management.

Analyst Perspective: "This spin-off is a masterstroke. Kraken is a pure-play battery storage company, while Octopus remains a diversified energy retailer. Investors who want exposure to grid-scale storage can now do so without the risks of Octopus’s broader business." — James Watson, Energy Analyst at Bloomberg

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Evaluating Energy Spin-Offs

1. Ignoring Regulatory Risks

Mistake: Assuming government subsidies (like CfDs) will remain permanent. Reality:

  • UK CfD auctions have seen declining strike prices (e.g., 2022 CfD auction had 50% fewer bids than 2021).
  • Political shifts (e.g., new UK government policies) could reduce support for renewables.

How to Avoid:

  • Analyze auction trends (e.g., BEIS CfD data).
  • Check for "strike price guarantees" in contracts.

2. Overestimating Growth Without Due Diligence

Mistake: Assuming battery storage will grow at 25% CAGR forever. Reality:

  • Grid constraints (e.g., UK National Grid capacity limits) could slow deployment.
  • Supply chain bottlenecks (lithium, cobalt) may increase costs.

How to Avoid:

  • Review Kraken’s pipeline (how many projects are shovel-ready vs. planned).
  • Compare to peers (e.g., Vattenfall, Iberdrola) to see realistic growth rates.

3. Failing to Diversify Within the Spin-Off

Mistake: Putting all pension funds into Kraken without diversification. Reality:

  • Battery storage is cyclical—prices can volatility with energy markets.
  • Geographic risk (e.g., UK vs. EU policies) could impact returns.

How to Avoid:

  • Allocate across multiple energy sectors (solar, wind, storage).
  • Consider international exposure (e.g., German battery storage vs. UK CfDs).

4. Not Understanding the Tax Implications

Mistake: Assuming all energy investments are tax-efficient. Reality:

  • Corporate bonds (e.g., Kraken’s debt) may have higher tax rates than equities.
  • Carried interest rules (for private equity investors) can reduce pension fund returns.

How to Avoid:

  • Consult a tax advisor before investing in private equity or infrastructure funds.
  • Compare VCTs/EIS for maximum tax relief.

5. Missing the Long-Term Liability Matching

Mistake: Investing in high-growth energy stocks without aligning with pension fund liabilities. Reality:

  • Battery storage projects have 20-30 year contracts, but pension funds need stable cash flows.
  • Volatile energy stocks may not match long-term pension obligations.

How to Avoid:

  • Use duration matching (align investment horizons with pension payouts).
  • Blend energy stocks with bonds for risk-adjusted returns.
