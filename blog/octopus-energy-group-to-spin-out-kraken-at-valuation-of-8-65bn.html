Octopus Energy Group to Spin Out Kraken at $8.65bn Valuation: What This Means for Investors, Pension Funds, and the Future of Energy

Introduction: A Major Shift in the Energy Sector

The energy landscape is undergoing one of its most dramatic transformations in decades, driven by the rapid adoption of renewable energy, shifting regulatory environments, and the growing demand for sustainable solutions. At the forefront of this evolution is Octopus Energy Group, the UK’s largest renewable energy provider, which has announced plans to spin out its battery storage arm, Kraken Energy, in a transaction valued at $8.65 billion.

This move marks a pivotal moment—not just for Octopus Energy but for the broader pension funds, institutional investors, and energy transition stakeholders who are closely watching how this restructuring could reshape the future of clean energy investments. With global renewable energy investments exceeding $500 billion in 2023 (IRENA) and battery storage capacity growing at a CAGR of 28% (BloombergNEF), the spin-off of Kraken could signal a new era of specialization within the energy sector.

Why Octopus Energy is Spinning Out Kraken: The Strategic Rationale

Octopus Energy’s decision to unbundle Kraken Energy—its battery storage and grid services subsidiary—is not an isolated move but part of a broader trend in corporate restructuring within the energy sector. Here’s why this spin-off is happening:

1. Focus on Core Renewable Energy Business

Octopus Energy has built its reputation on solar, wind, and green gas solutions, serving over 1.5 million customers in the UK and beyond. However, battery storage and grid services require a different operational model—one that demands higher capital expenditure, regulatory expertise, and real-time market agility.

By spinning out Kraken, Octopus can:

  • Concentrate on its core strengths (retail energy, renewables, and EV charging).
  • Reduce operational complexity by separating high-growth, high-risk assets from its stable consumer-facing business.
  • Attract investors who specialize in grid-scale storage, rather than those focused on retail energy.

2. Capitalizing on the Battery Storage Boom

The global battery storage market is projected to reach $1.3 trillion by 2030 (MarketsandMarkets), with UK battery storage capacity expected to grow from 1.5 GW in 2023 to 20 GW by 2030 (Department for Energy Security & Net Zero).

Kraken’s portfolio includes:

  • Grid-scale battery projects (e.g., the 100 MW Hornsea Project One battery, one of the UK’s largest).
  • Demand flexibility services (helping businesses and industries reduce energy costs through smart grid solutions).
  • Vehicle-to-grid (V2G) technology (enabling electric vehicles to feed power back into the grid).

A standalone Kraken could raise dedicated funding for these high-growth areas, accelerating deployment in line with the UK’s net-zero targets.

3. Aligning with Regulatory and Market Trends

The UK government’s Energy Security Strategy and Net Zero Roadmap emphasize battery storage as a critical enabler for integrating renewable energy. By spinning out Kraken, Octopus can:

  • Leverage government incentives (e.g., Contract for Difference (CfD) auctions, Enhanced Frequency Response (EFR) schemes).
  • Partner with national grid operators to optimize storage deployment.
  • Attract institutional investors who are increasingly allocating capital to infrastructure and grid-scale solutions.

4. Creating Shareholder Value Through Specialization

Unbundling allows both Octopus and Kraken to optimize their valuations:

  • Octopus Energy can retain its retail and renewables business, which has strong cash flows and customer loyalty.
  • Kraken Energy can pursue higher-growth, capital-intensive projects with a focused investor base (e.g., pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and infrastructure specialists).

This aligns with the "unbundling" trend seen in utilities, where companies like EDF and National Grid have separated their generation, transmission, and retail arms to enhance efficiency and attract niche investors.

  • Kraken’s valuation is significantly higher than most recent battery storage deals, reflecting Octopus Energy’s strong brand, regulatory relationships, and pipeline of projects.
  • The $8.65bn figure is roughly 10x its 2023 revenue, which is comparable to other high-growth infrastructure assets (e.g., UK North Sea wind farms trade at 8-12x EBITDA).

2. Why the Premium Valuation?

Several factors contribute to Kraken’s high valuation multiple: ✅ Government Support – The UK’s £1bn battery storage fund and EFR contracts provide long-term revenue visibility. ✅ Scalability – Kraken has multiple projects in the pipeline, including 1 GW of new storage by 2025. ✅ Tech Leadership – Octopus is a pioneer in V2G and AI-driven grid optimization, reducing operational risks. ✅ Investor Demand – Pension funds and infrastructure investors are actively seeking exposure to battery storage as a defensive asset class against energy price volatility.

3. Risks That Could Affect the Valuation

While the valuation is strong, market risks remain:

  • Regulatory Uncertainty – Changes in subsidy schemes (e.g., CfD auctions) could impact cash flows.
  • Grid Connection Delays – National Grid’s backlog of connection requests could slow project deployment.
  • Competition from Cheaper Renewables – If solar and wind costs drop further, storage may become less critical in some markets.
  • Interest Rate Sensitivity – High borrowing costs could delay or reduce project financing.

Actionable Insight: If you’re evaluating pension fund allocations to energy infrastructure, diversify across multiple sectors (renewables, storage, grid services) rather than overconcentrating in one area.

What This Means for Investors & Pension Funds

The Kraken spin-off has broad implications for different types of investors:

1. For Pension Funds & Institutional Investors

Pension schemes with long-term horizons (e.g., public sector pensions, corporate defined benefit schemes) may see this as an opportunity to: ✔ Gain exposure to a high-growth, low-correlation asset class (battery storage moves differently than stocks or bonds). ✔ Access a diversified portfolio of grid services, V2G, and demand flexibility—all of which are resilient to energy price shocks. ✔ Leverage tax advantages (e.g., UK’s Infrastructure Investment Allowance for qualifying assets).

Real-World Example: The UK’s National Pension Savings Scheme (NPSS) has been actively allocating to renewable energy and grid infrastructure since 2020. If Kraken were to go public or be acquired by a pension-backed fund, it could become a core holding for schemes looking to reduce equity exposure.

2. For Self-Directed Investors & ETF Holders

Individual investors may not have direct access to Kraken, but they can indirectly benefit through:

  • Renewable Energy ETFs (e.g., iShares Global Clean Energy ETF (ICLN), First Trust NASDAQ Clean Edge Green Energy ETF (QCLN)).
  • Infrastructure ETFs (e.g., Global X Infrastructure Thematic ETF (INFR)).
  • UK Energy Stocks (e.g., Orsted (ORSTED), Ørsted (OTC:ORSTED), ScottishPower (SPP)).

Actionable Strategy: If you’re invested in UK energy stocks, monitor whether Octopus Energy’s share price reacts positively to the Kraken spin-off—a strong reaction could signal confidence in the separation.

3. For Energy Transition & ESG Investors

The spin-off aligns with ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) investment trends:

  • Kraken’s focus on grid stability supports net-zero goals by reducing reliance on fossil fuel peaker plants.
  • Octopus’s retail business continues to expand EV charging and green gas, reinforcing its ESG credentials.
  • Pension funds with net-zero mandates (e.g., Legal & General, Aviva) may increase allocations to storage assets as part of their decarbonization strategies.

Common Mistake to Avoid: Assuming that all renewable energy investments are equally low-risk. Battery storage involves higher upfront costs and regulatory risks compared to operational solar or wind farms.

Once Kraken stands alone, watch for:

Red Flag: If Kraken’s debt levels spike post-spin-off, it may signal over-reliance on leverage—a risk for pension funds with liquidity constraints.
