Why Octopus Energy Is Spinning Out Kraken: The Strategic Rationale

Octopus Energy Group, founded in 2015, has grown from a small British energy supplier into a global leader in renewable energy, serving over 2 million customers across the UK, Europe, and beyond. However, the company’s ambitions have outgrown its current structure. The decision to spin out Kraken, its European wholesale energy trading arm, is a bold strategic move with several key motivations:

1. Unlocking Shareholder Value Through Specialization

Octopus Energy has been undervalued in the market due to its diverse but complex operations, ranging from retail energy supply to renewable energy projects and now wholesale trading. By spinning out Kraken—a high-growth, high-margin business—Octopus can:

  • Focus on its core retail and renewable energy business while allowing Kraken to operate as an independent entity.
  • Attract institutional investors who prefer specialized energy trading firms over conglomerates.
  • Boost Octopus’ stock valuation by separating its most valuable asset.

Real-world example: In 2021, Iberdrola (a Spanish renewable energy giant) spun off its distribution arm (Red Eléctrica) to focus on renewables, resulting in a 20% increase in its stock price within six months.

2. Capitalizing on Europe’s Energy Market Reforms

The European energy market is in flux due to:

  • The post-Ukraine war energy crisis, which has accelerated the shift away from Russian gas.
  • New EU regulations (e.g., REPowerEU plan) aiming to reduce fossil fuel dependence by 2030.
  • Increased demand for flexible energy trading as renewables (wind, solar) become more dominant.

Kraken, with its advanced trading platforms and energy market expertise, is perfectly positioned to capitalize on these changes. By spinning it out, Octopus ensures that Kraken can scale rapidly without being constrained by the slower-moving retail side of the business.

3. Attracting Private Equity and Strategic Investors

The $8.65bn valuation suggests that private equity firms and strategic buyers (such as Engie, E.ON, or even sovereign wealth funds) are eyeing Kraken as a high-growth acquisition target. This could lead to:

  • A secondary market listing for Kraken, providing liquidity for Octopus shareholders.
  • Potential buyout offers that could further boost Octopus’ stock price.

Real-world example: In 2022, Orsted (a Danish wind energy leader) was acquired by Brookfield Renewable Partners for $15.3bn, demonstrating how specialized renewable energy firms command premium valuations.

4. Reducing Operational Complexity

Octopus Energy has grown through acquisitions (e.g., Bulb Energy, Ovo Energy) and organic expansion, leading to a fragmented operational structure. By spinning out Kraken:

  • Octopus can streamline its management and focus on customer-facing and renewable energy projects.
  • It avoids regulatory and compliance risks that come with running both a retail supplier and a wholesale trader.

5. Aligning with Investor Demand for ESG-Focused Firms

Institutional investors—particularly pension funds and asset managers—are increasingly prioritizing ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) criteria when making investment decisions. While Kraken is a wholesale trading business, its role in facilitating renewable energy transitions makes it an attractive asset for ESG-conscious buyers.

Statistic: According to McKinsey (2023), 60% of institutional investors now consider ESG factors in their portfolio decisions, with renewable energy being the top-performing asset class in sustainable investing.

  • Flexibility services (helping grid operators balance supply and demand).

Why is this valuable?
