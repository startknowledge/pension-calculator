Understanding the Kraken Spin-Off: A Deep Dive

What Is Kraken, and Why Is It Being Spun Out?

Kraken is Octopus Energy Group’s renewable energy subsidiary, specializing in wind farms, solar projects, and battery storage. Founded in 2015, Kraken has since grown into one of the UK’s largest independent renewable energy developers, with a portfolio valued at over £10 billion.

The decision to spin out Kraken is part of Octopus Energy’s strategy to unlock shareholder value while allowing Kraken to operate as an independent, publicly listed entity. This move aligns with a broader trend in corporate restructuring, where asset-heavy companies separate their core operations from high-growth subsidiaries to attract private equity or institutional investors.

The $8.65 Billion Valuation: How Was It Determined?

The $8.65 billion valuation of Kraken is based on several key factors:

  1. Asset Value – Kraken’s operational wind and solar assets, including projects in the UK, Europe, and the US, contribute significantly to its valuation.
  2. Pipeline Potential – The company has over 10 GW of renewable energy projects in development, which could further drive its market cap.
  3. Regulatory and Policy Support – Governments are offering subsidies, tax incentives, and long-term contracts for renewable energy, making Kraken’s assets more attractive.
  4. Comparable Deals – Recent spin-offs in the energy sector, such as NextEra Energy’s spin-off of NextEra Energy Partners (2023, $12.5bn valuation), provide benchmarks for Kraken’s valuation.

Who Will Own Kraken After the Spin-Off?

While the exact ownership structure is still being finalized, industry insiders suggest:

  • Octopus Energy will retain a minority stake (likely 10-20%).
  • Private equity firms, sovereign wealth funds, and institutional investors will likely acquire the majority.
  • Potential IPO (Initial Public Offering) in the near future, allowing retail investors to gain exposure.

8 Actionable Strategies for Investors and Pension Holders

The Kraken spin-off presents unique opportunities for those looking to invest in renewable energy. Here’s how you can capitalize on this trend while minimizing risk.

1. Diversify Your Pension Portfolio with Renewable Energy ETFs

If you’re a pension holder, directly investing in Kraken may not be feasible due to its private nature post-spin-off. Instead, consider exchange-traded funds (ETFs) that focus on clean energy:

  • iShares Global Clean Energy ETF (ICLN) – Tracks global renewable energy companies.
  • Invesco Solar ETF (TAN) – Focuses on solar-specific stocks.
  • L&G Clean Energy Generation UCITS ETF (CLEN) – A UK-friendly option with strong ESG compliance.

Why it works: These ETFs provide instant diversification and exposure to the broader renewable energy sector without the need for direct ownership.

2. Explore Green Bonds for Passive Income

Pension funds and individual investors can also invest in green bonds, which fund renewable energy projects. Platforms like:

  • Green Bond Market (GBM)
  • Climate Bonds Initiative (CBI)

Offer fixed-income returns while supporting sustainable infrastructure.

3. Invest in Renewable Energy Infrastructure Funds

For those with higher-risk tolerance, private infrastructure funds specializing in wind and solar can yield strong long-term returns. Examples include:

  • Octopus Renewables Fund (pre-spin-off exposure)
  • Schroders Global Renewable Power Fund

Pro Tip: Check if your pension provider offers access to these funds through pension drawdown or self-invested personal pensions (SIPPs).

4. Monitor Kraken’s Potential IPO for Direct Exposure

If Kraken goes public, institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals will have the first opportunity. To prepare:

  • Set up a brokerage account with platforms like Hargreaves Lansdown, AJ Bell, or Interactive Investor.
  • Follow Kraken’s financial updates via Bloomberg Terminal, Reuters, or the Financial Times.
  • Consider a "waitlist" strategy—if Kraken’s IPO is oversubscribed, you may miss out if you don’t act quickly.

5. Leverage Pension Loans for Strategic Investments

If you’re 55+ and in pension drawdown, you can borrow against your pension (via a pension loan scheme) to invest in renewable energy assets. This allows you to:

  • Access capital for direct investments in wind/solar projects.
  • Diversify beyond traditional stocks and bonds.

Warning: Pension loan schemes come with tax and debt risks, so consult a financial advisor before proceeding.

6. Invest in Renewable Energy REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)

REITs like Brookfield Renewable Partners (BEP) and NextEra Energy Partners (NEP) allow investors to earn dividends from renewable energy infrastructure without direct ownership. These are liquid investments that can be held within a SIPP or pension wrapper.

7. Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts for Renewable Energy Investments

If you’re in the US, consider a Health Savings Account (HSA) or IRA for renewable energy investments. In the UK, a SIPP or Self-Assessed Pension can provide tax-free growth on green investments.

8. Stay Informed on Policy Changes Affecting Renewable Energy

Governments are constantly introducing new subsidies and regulations that impact renewable energy valuations. Key areas to watch:

  • UK’s Contracts for Difference (CfD) scheme
  • US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) tax credits
  • EU Green Deal funding

Action Step: Subscribe to Bloomberg Green, Reuters Sustainability, and the IEA’s renewable energy reports for real-time updates.

Real-World Examples of Successful Energy Spin-Offs

Understanding past successes can help investors anticipate future trends in the renewable energy sector.

Example 1: NextEra Energy Partners (NEP) – A $12.5bn Spin-Off

In 2023, NextEra Energy (the world’s largest renewable energy company) spun off NextEra Energy Partners (NEP), a private equity-like fund focused on wind and solar assets. The move:

  • Unlocked $12.5 billion in value for NextEra shareholders.
  • Allowed NEP to raise $10 billion in new capital for future projects.
  • Resulted in a 20%+ return for early investors within a year.

Lesson: Spin-offs can accelerate growth by separating high-growth assets from core operations.

Example 2: Ørsted’s Transition from Oil to Renewables

Ørsted, a Danish energy giant, completely pivoted from oil to offshore wind in the 2010s. By 2020, it became the world’s largest offshore wind operator, with a market cap of $20 billion.

Key Takeaway: Companies that diversify into renewables early often outperform traditional energy firms in the long run.

Example 3: Iberdrola’s Wind and Solar Expansion

Iberdrola, a Spanish utility, has invested $100 billion in renewables since 2010. Its wind and solar assets now generate 70% of its revenue, making it one of the most ESG-compliant energy companies globally.

Investor Benefit: Iberdrola’s stock has outperformed the S&P 500 by 150% over the past decade.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Investing in Renewable Energy

While renewable energy is a high-growth sector, investors often make costly mistakes. Here’s how to avoid them:

Mistake 1: Overpaying for Hype Without Due Diligence

  • Problem: Some investors rush into renewable energy stocks without analyzing fundamentals.
  • Solution: Always check:
    • Debt levels (high leverage can hurt returns).
    • Project completion rates (delays can reduce valuation).
    • Regulatory risks (policy changes can impact profitability).

Mistake 2: Ignoring Geographic Diversification

  • Problem: Overconcentration in one region (e.g., UK wind farms) can expose you to local policy risks.
  • Solution: Spread investments across Europe, the US, and Asia for stability.

Mistake 3: Assuming All Renewable Energy Stocks Are Equal

  • Problem: Some companies are pure-play renewables, while others are hybrid (oil + renewables).
  • Solution: Research ESG ratings (MSCI, Sustainalytics) to ensure true green exposure.

Mistake 4: Not Factoring in Inflation and Currency Risks

  • Problem: Renewable energy projects in emerging markets may face currency fluctuations.
  • Solution: Use hedging strategies or invest in stable-currency-denominated assets.

Mistake 5: Missing Out on Tax Incentives

  • Problem: Many investors don’t claim available tax breaks for green investments.
  • Solution: Consult a tax advisor to maximize REIT deductions, solar tax credits, or pension-friendly green funds.
