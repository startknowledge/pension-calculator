Why Octopus Energy is Spinning Out Kraken: The Strategic Breakdown

1. The Rise of Octopus Energy: From Startup to Energy Giant

Octopus Energy, founded in 2015, has grown into one of the UK’s most innovative renewable energy providers, serving over 1.5 million customers across the UK and Europe. Its success stems from:

  • Aggressive expansion in solar, wind, and battery storage
  • A customer-first approach, including dynamic tariffs that adjust in real-time based on energy prices
  • Strong ESG credentials, with 90% of its energy supply coming from renewables (as of 2023)

However, as the company scaled, it faced operational complexity—managing both retail energy services (Octopus Energy) and large-scale energy infrastructure (Kraken) under one umbrella became increasingly difficult.

2. What is Kraken Energy? A Deep Dive

Kraken, acquired by Octopus in 2021 for £1.3 billion, is a wholesale energy trading and infrastructure company specializing in:

  • Battery storage (with 1.5 GW of capacity under development)
  • Grid flexibility services, helping balance supply and demand in real-time
  • Carbon capture and hydrogen projects (critical for the UK’s net-zero goals)
  • Aggregation of renewable energy assets, including wind and solar farms

Unlike Octopus Energy’s retail-focused model, Kraken operates in the B2B (business-to-business) energy market, dealing with national grid operators, industrial clients, and energy traders.

3. The Business Case for the Spin-Off

Octopus Energy’s CEO, Greg Jackson, has stated that the spin-off is "a strategic move to unlock value for shareholders" while allowing both entities to focus on their core strengths.

Key Reasons for the Separation:

✅ Enhanced Focus & Efficiency

  • Kraken’s wholesale energy trading requires different expertise than Octopus Energy’s retail customer service.
  • A standalone Kraken can prioritize innovation in grid flexibility, which is crucial for integrating millions of solar panels and electric vehicles (EVs) into the UK grid.

✅ Valuation Uplift

  • By separating Kraken, Octopus Energy can attract institutional investors who specialize in infrastructure and energy trading.
  • The £7.2bn valuation suggests Kraken is now worth more than double its acquisition price, reflecting its growing importance in the energy transition.

✅ Access to Capital for Green Tech

  • Kraken’s battery storage and hydrogen projects need heavy investment—a standalone entity can raise funds more easily from pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and green investment banks.
  • Example: Northvolt (Sweden’s battery giant) raised €1.5bn in 2023 from institutional investors to scale its battery production—similar capital could now flow into Kraken.

✅ Regulatory & Risk Management

  • The UK’s energy market is evolving rapidly, with new carbon pricing mechanisms and grid stability rules.
  • A separate Kraken can navigate these changes more agilely, reducing risk for Octopus Energy’s retail operations.

✅ Alignment with ESG Investors

  • Pension funds and ESG-focused investors are increasingly diversifying into clean energy infrastructure.
  • Kraken’s battery storage and grid services align perfectly with net-zero portfolios, making it an attractive standalone asset.

Octopus Energy shareholders will retain ownership of Kraken post-spin-off, but the exact structure is still being finalized. Key considerations:

  • Potential for higher dividends if Kraken’s profits are reinvested in Octopus.
  • Dilution risk—if Kraken is floated separately, Octopus may issue new shares to fund the separation.
  • Market reaction: The announcement caused a 5% spike in Octopus Energy’s share price (as of June 2024), suggesting positive investor sentiment.

Real-World Examples: Lessons from Past Energy Spin-Offs

1. National Grid’s Separation of Transmission & Distribution (2013)

In 2013, the UK’s National Grid split into National Grid Electricity Transmission (NGET) and National Grid Electricity Distribution (NGD).

  • Outcome: NGET’s share price rose by 30% in the first year post-spin-off, while NGD stabilized as a local utility.
  • Lesson: Specialization improves performance—Kraken’s focus on grid flexibility could see similar gains.

2. Ørsted’s Shift from Oil to Offshore Wind (2000s)

Denmark’s Ørsted (formerly DONG Energy) divested its oil and gas assets and rebranded as a pure-play wind energy company.

  • Outcome: Its share price surged by 400% over a decade, making it Europe’s most valuable renewable energy company.
  • Lesson: A clear ESG focus drives long-term investor confidence—Kraken’s battery and hydrogen push could follow a similar trajectory.

3. NextEra Energy’s Acquisition of Florida Power & Light (2016)

NextEra, the world’s largest renewable energy company, acquired FPL Group (a utility) to diversify into regulated energy assets.

  • Outcome: The combined entity now has over 10 GW of solar and wind, with consistent dividend growth.
  • Lesson: Combining retail energy (Octopus) with wholesale infrastructure (Kraken) can create a powerhouse—if executed well.

4. EDF’s Spin-Off of its Nuclear Assets (2021)

France’s Électricité de France (EDF) considered spinning off its nuclear division to focus on renewables and grid services.

  • Outcome: While the deal didn’t proceed, it highlighted how energy companies are prioritizing flexibility over legacy assets.
  • Lesson: Kraken’s battery storage is the modern equivalent of nuclear—critical for grid stability but not a core retail offering.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Evaluating Energy Investments

1. Ignoring Regulatory Risks in Energy Markets

Mistake: Assuming energy companies are immune to government policy changes. Example: The UK’s Capacity Market reforms (2024) reduced payments for battery storage, affecting similar assets. How to Avoid:

  • Monitor UK energy policy updates from Ofgem and BEIS.
  • Diversify across multiple energy sectors (solar, wind, batteries, hydrogen).

2. Overpaying for "Green" Stocks Without Due Diligence

Mistake: Buying ESG-labeled stocks without checking their actual carbon footprint. Example: Some companies greenwash by calling themselves "renewable" while still relying on gas peaker plants. How to Avoid:

  • Check ESG ratings from MSCI, Sustainalytics, or CDP.
  • Avoid "greenwashing"—look for verified carbon offsets and real renewable energy usage.

3. Focusing Only on Share Price, Not Fundamentals

Mistake: Buying high-flying energy stocks based on short-term hype rather than long-term cash flows. Example: First Solar’s stock crashed by 40% in 2022 after investors ignored its weak balance sheet. How to Avoid:

  • Analyze free cash flow, debt levels, and project pipelines.
  • Compare Kraken to peers like Drax (battery storage) or Vattenfall (wind/hydro).

4. Missing Out on Tax Advantages for Green Investments

Mistake: Not leveraging tax-efficient accounts for renewable energy investments. Example: SEIS (Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme) offers 30% tax relief for early-stage green tech investments. How to Avoid:

  • Use ISAs, SIPPs, or EIS accounts for energy investments.
  • Consult a financial advisor on tax-efficient structuring.

5. Underestimating the Role of AI & Digitalization in Energy

Mistake: Assuming energy investments are low-tech and stable. Example: Octopus Energy’s AI-driven tariffs have reduced customer bills by £100m+ annually. How to Avoid:

  • Look for companies using AI for:
    • Demand forecasting (Kraken’s strength).
    • Grid optimization (reducing blackouts).
    • Fraud detection in energy billing.

6. Not Preparing for a Potential IPO or Secondary Listing

Mistake: Assuming Kraken will remain private indefinitely. Example: National Grid’s IPO in 1990 made early shareholders millionaires. How to Avoid:

  • Set up alerts for Kraken’s potential listing.
  • Consider buying shares before the IPO (if offered to existing Octopus shareholders).
